# I — Desert Surface

## Scene 1 — Opening

Senzo placed his hand on Nandi's shoulder. Three pressures. Slow. Side to side. In their Contact, it meant: *Follow me.* But also: *The world is still trembling.*

She followed him.

The ground vibrated beneath their feet the way it always vibrated, the way it had vibrated the day before and the day before that and every day since days existed. Magnitude 4, maybe 5. The kind of tremor that didn't even deserve a name. Background noise. The world's pulse.

Nandi walked barefoot. Not out of vanity — out of necessity. Soles lied. Leather, rubber, anything separating flesh from ground smothered the signals. She needed bare stone under her toes to read. The desert spoke to her through the soles of her feet and she had no right not to listen.

Ahead of her, Senzo moved at a steady pace, shoulders low, gaze fixed somewhere beyond the horizon line where sand met volcano. The entire expedition lived in that gaze. Not in Sihle's calculations, not in Enama's provisions, not in Inyoni's ropes. In the way Senzo looked at a point and walked toward it as if the whole world were just a detail between him and his destination.

Behind them, the camp was being taken down. Jabu rolled the hammocks, detached the elastic cords from stakes driven two meters into the sand. Everything that wasn't fixed in this world ended up on the ground. Everything that was fixed also ended up on the ground — just more slowly. The architecture of survival came down to that difference: how long things took to fall.

Thabo was testing the air. Crouched facing the wind, a piece of blackened cloth stretched between his fingers, he watched the fiber's movements. Air didn't lie. The ground could give way, walls could collapse, but the air current announced everything before anyone else. Thabo trusted the cloth more than the instruments. Instruments had batteries. The cloth had the wind.

Inyoni, on all fours atop the equipment cart, tightened the straps one by one. Her hands worked without her looking at them. Thirty-two straps. She counted them as she tightened, one gesture per strap, and each gesture was also a word in her head — a word of Contact she repeated on loop like a mechanical prayer. *Holds. Holds. Holds.* Thirty-two times.

Sihle wasn't helping with the teardown. Sihle was sitting on a crate, eyes closed, a portable seismograph on his knees. The device drew curves on a roll of paper — lines that trembled the way everything trembled here, an automatic writing of the world. Sihle read those lines the way others read faces. He saw magnitudes, directions, depths. This morning, the curves were regular. Low. Calm, for this world. He opened his eyes and looked for Enama.

She was at the other end of camp, her back turned. She wasn't looking at Sihle's seismograph. She had never looked at his seismograph. Enama trusted her feet, her hands, the shiver that climbed her spine when the earth changed register. The body knows before the instrument, she said. Sihle answered that the body lies. Their war was ancient. It would last until the bottom of the volcano — or not.

Enama was cutting roots. Fat, fibrous tubers she had dug up at dawn in the sand east of camp, where an underground spring kept the soil damp despite the quakes. She sliced them into strips, crushed them in a stone mortar, added water, crushed again. The thick paste that resulted would be the midday meal. No plates. Gourds. You drank your food here — everything that could spill did spill, and everything always spilled.

Nandi felt the ground change.

Not a quake. Not yet. Something before the quake. A shift in frequency, a slight discord between the vibration she expected and the one arriving. Like a wrong note in an orchestra that no one else could hear. Her stomach clenched. Saliva rose to her lips.

The tremens.

Her body was recalibrating. Every geographical zone had its base frequency, its own seismic register, and the human body adapted to it from birth the way the ear adapts to ambient noise. Nandi was born in the northeastern archipelagos, where the quakes were high and dry, brief snaps like whip-cracks in stone. Here, in the southern desert, the vibrations were low, long, deep — waves that rolled beneath the ground like a sleeping animal breathing.

Her body didn't like these frequencies. It told her so through nausea.

She stopped. Placed a hand on the ground. Closed her eyes.

Under her fingers, the earth murmured. Not words — intentions. The same intention for months, repeated each day a little louder: something was rising. Something deep, slow, massive, climbing toward the surface like a bubble in thick lava. Magnitude 11. The one no one had ever measured. The one the instruments didn't yet know how to draw.

Nandi felt it.

Senzo turned around. He saw her hand on the ground, her eyes closed, and he knew. He came back to her. Placed his palm on her forearm. Two short pressures, then a long one. In their Contact: *What do you feel?*

She opened her eyes. Searched for words. Not voice-words — touch-words. She grabbed Senzo's wrist. Squeezed three times. Fast, fast, slow. Then she placed her index finger on the ground and pressed.

*It's rising.*

Senzo didn't answer right away. He looked at the volcano. The black mass at the edge of the desert, where sand became rock and rock became heat. The mouth of the world. The hole through which all seven of them would descend, to go place a charge at the bottom and trigger what could no longer be prevented — only preempted.

He placed his hand on the back of Nandi's neck. A single pressure. Long. Warm.

*I know.*

Then he turned and walked toward the camp. The teardown continued. The sun was climbing. The ground trembled. No one noticed.

---

When Senzo gave the signal to depart — a raised hand, fist closed, then open, five fingers spread: *We go, now* — the team was already ready. Not because they were efficient. Because there was almost nothing to carry.

Seven people. Four carts. Survival gear, explosives — packed in shock-absorbing cases Sihle had designed himself, triple-suspension boxes that absorbed tremors up to magnitude 8 —, ropes, gourds, liquid rations for sixty days. Sixty days. That was Senzo's estimate. Sihle had calculated forty-five. Enama had said: *We'll see.*

They started walking. Senzo in front. Nandi right behind him — her position in the line was no accident. She was his eyes beneath the ground. His predictor. The only one on the team whose body translated the earth's signals into something resembling language. The others felt quakes the way you feel rain — when it falls. Nandi felt them the way you feel a storm — before.

Behind Nandi, Enama. Then Thabo and Inyoni, side by side, pulling the first cart. Jabu alone on the second. Sihle last, one hand on his portable seismograph bouncing in its chest harness, the other hand free, ready to touch — ready for Contact.

No one spoke.

It wasn't silence. Silence didn't exist here. The world rumbled, sand scraped under the cart wheels, wind whistled through the rock fissures on the horizon. But no one *spoke*, because voice was a useless luxury in a world that drowned out everything. Speaking meant shouting. And shouting meant wasting the energy the body saved for Contact.

They communicated by touch. A hand on a shoulder, the speed of the pressure, the exact location of contact, the duration — everything was significant. Contact had its grammar, its nuances, its registers. A firm grip on the forearm didn't carry the same value as a firm grip on the wrist. The shoulder said urgency. The arm said information. The hand said emotion. The nape of the neck said intimacy.

Senzo had touched the back of Nandi's neck. He didn't touch anyone else's.

The column moved toward the volcano. The desert heated. The ground vibrated. Magnitude 4, constant, regular, boring. The background noise of a world that had never known stillness.

Nandi walked barefoot on the hot sand and she counted the seconds between each micro-tremor. Three seconds. Three seconds. Three seconds. Regular. Normal. Nothing to report.

But beneath the rhythm, below, far below — like a second heart under the first — she felt the other beat. The one with no rhythm. The one that was rising.

She said nothing. Not yet. Senzo knew. And when the others needed to know, he would tell them. That was his role. To decide the route. To decide what gets said. To decide when it gets said.

For now, they walked. Seven people in a desert that trembled. Heading toward a volcano that was going to kill them all.

And the world didn't care.

# II — Intermediate Zones

## Scene 1 — The ground changes

The sand stopped first under Nandi's feet.

Not all at once. Not like a border you cross. More like a sentence that changes meaning midway — the same ground, the same vibration, but the grain was no longer the same. Under her toes, the fine desert dust gave way to something more compact, more dense. Crushed rock. Volcanic gravel that cracked under the body's weight and didn't return signals the same way.

She slowed down. Her feet no longer understood.

That was tremens. Not the pain — the confusion. The body had spent three weeks in the southern desert, calibrating itself to its long, deep waves, its bass vibrations that rolled under the sand like an organ played in slow motion. And now the ground was rising in frequency. The waves became dry. Short. Sharp cracks that climbed up through the ankles and hammered the knees.

Saliva rose in her mouth. She swallowed it.

Ahead, Senzo walked without slowing. His feet were shod — soft boots with thin soles, a compromise between protection and reading. He felt the quakes but didn't *read* them the way Nandi did. His body didn't translate. He moved forward, and direction was enough.

Enama was the second to feel the change. She stopped dead, both hands on the hips of a cart, and turned her head toward Nandi. Their gazes met. Enama didn't need Contact to say it — her eyes said: *You too?*

Nandi nodded. Imperceptible. Not a Contact gesture — a gesture of exhaustion.

The column continued. The terrain climbed. The sand had disappeared. Under the cart wheels, the volcanic gravel protested with each turn, a high-pitched grinding that the volcano's wind carried toward them like a warning.

Thabo, mid-column, crouching on the moving cart, had taken out his cloth. He held it by the tips of his fingers, arm raised above his head, and he watched the fibers dance. The wind came from the volcano. Dry. Hot. Sulfurous. Not enough to be dangerous — but enough for Thabo to frown and tuck the cloth into his shirt pocket with a slow gesture that, in the vocabulary of those who knew him, meant: *Noted.*

The volcano's foothills spread before them like an open maw. The flat desert had lied for days — straight horizon, nothing to see, walk and walk. Now the landscape heaved. Basalt ridges, ravines carved by centuries of tremors, faults in the rock that ran like black veins on the world's grey skin. And everywhere, everywhere, the vibration.

Higher here. More nervous.

Nandi vomited.

She stopped, bent over, and her body emptied what it had in its stomach — Enama's porridge, water, bile. She didn't fight it. You didn't fight tremens. You let it pass and walked after.

Senzo heard — or felt, through the rope linking his cart to the middle one, the vibration of a halt. He turned around. Saw Nandi bent over, hands on her knees. He came back toward her. Not fast. Not slow. The exact speed of someone who refuses to worry but worries.

He placed his hand on the top of her back. Two spaced pressures. Standard Contact: *You okay?*

She wiped her mouth with the back of her hand. Grabbed Senzo's wrist. Three quick pressures, a pause, one long pressure. This wasn't standard vocabulary either — it was their dialect, Senzo-and-Nandi Contact, forged over months of expedition. It meant something like: *My body is translating. Let it.*

Senzo withdrew his hand. Resumed the march.

Behind them, Sihle had opened his eyes. His seismograph was drawing tighter curves — the peaks were closing in, the amplitudes rising. He said nothing. He took a pencil and noted a number in the margin of the paper roll. 6.2.

## Scene 2 — Magnitude 7

It started with the carts.

The first cart — Thabo and Inyoni's — lifted two centimeters. Not much. Enough for the wheels to leave the ground for a fraction of a second and fall back with a dull thud that wasn't the sound of wheels on gravel. It was the sound of the world changing its mind.

Then the ground undulated.

Nandi felt it before everyone — not the tremor, what came *before* the tremor. The micro-silence. The infinitesimal suspension of the background vibration, the fraction of a second where the ground holds its breath. Like a heart skipping a beat.

She grabbed Senzo's arm. Not a pressure — a grip. Total. All five fingers dug into his forearm, nails in flesh. In any Contact, it meant the same thing: *Now.*

Senzo raised his fist. Closed hand, arm outstretched. The signal. Everyone stopped. Even Sihle, who didn't need the signal because his seismograph was already screaming — the needle was tearing the paper, the curves were no longer curves but saw teeth.

The earth struck.

Not like in the desert, where the tremors rolled under the sand, long and muffled, almost lazy. Here the rock absorbed nothing. The wave climbed straight up through the basalt, rigid, brutal, and struck the feet like a hammer blow under the sole. Nandi fell to her knees. Jabu grabbed the cart. Thabo pressed his hands to the ground — not from panic, from a reading reflex. Inyoni, standing on the second cart, bent her knees and held. Held the way she held straps, the way she held ropes, the way she held everything — with the strength of hands that never let go of anything.

The main wave lasted eight seconds. Eight seconds during which the world became vertical, horizontal, diagonal, and none of that anymore. Eight seconds where Contact became something else.

The gestures changed.

Gone the measured pressures, the calibrated touches, the learned codes. Under magnitude 7, Contact turned animal. Enama grabbed Nandi by the collar. Not a word — a reflex. Senzo pressed his palm against the rock and his other hand on the shoulder of Thabo who had fallen beside him, and that gesture had nothing to say except *I'm here*. The arm-locks became cramps. The codified grips became survival clutches.

Contact had an emergency register the desert had never triggered.

Stones fell from the ridge above them. Not big — basalt shards, fist-to-skull-sized, bouncing down the slope. Jabu took one on the shoulder. He grunted — voice, useless, automatic, drowned in the rumbling — and pressed himself against the cart.

Then it stopped.

Not silence — silence didn't exist. But the return to background magnitude. 4. Maybe 5. The world's normal pulse, after the crisis.

Senzo stood up. Blood on his palm — the rock had cut. He looked at the team. Counted. Seven. Standing or getting up. Seven.

He raised his hand. Open fist. Five fingers. *We continue.*

Nobody moved right away. The body needed to recover. The post-seismic tremens — a residual vertigo, like the roll of a ship after the storm. Nandi was on all fours, fingers dug into the gravel. She was breathing through her mouth. Each exhale was a small sound — not a word, not a moan, something between the two. Her body was recalculating. The new frequencies entered through the soles of her feet and climbed up the bones like an electric current seeking ground.

Sihle approached her. It was the first time he had deliberately touched her since departure. He placed two fingers on her wrist. Technical Contact — the wrist is information, not emotion. Two pressures: *Data?*

Nandi raised her head. Looked at him. Her eyes were bloodshot — tremens burst the capillaries. She grabbed Sihle's two fingers and placed them on the ground. Pressed. Hard.

*Listen for yourself.*

Sihle withdrew his hand. His fingers couldn't read the ground. His fingers could read the curves on paper. He stood up and returned to his seismograph without another gesture.

Enama, who had seen the exchange, said nothing. But her hands, picking up a gourd fallen from the cart, trembled with an anger that didn't yet have words.

## Scene 3 — The belly of the world

The entrance to the cavern was not an entrance.

It was a fault — a tear in the flank of the foothills, three meters wide, barely one high, descending at an angle into the dark. The kind of opening the ground had opened by accident and nobody had closed because nobody closed anything here. The world opened. The world didn't close.

Thabo went first. That was his role — where there was a hole, Thabo tested the air before anyone set foot inside. He crouched before the fault, took out his cloth, and held it at the opening's height. The fibers trembled, then leaned inward. Inbound airflow. The volcano was inhaling.

He turned back to Senzo. A single gesture: hand flat, palm toward the ground, lateral motion. Clear Contact. *Air is circulating. We can go.*

Senzo looked at the fault. Looked at the map — a roll of thick cloth on which he had traced the descent lines from the surveys of those who had never come back but whose notes had risen through a fault, carried by the wind, found two years later in the desert. The lines said: here, a passage. The lines didn't say whether the passage still held.

He entered.

One by one, crouching then on their knees then nearly lying flat, they passed through the fault. The carts didn't enter — Inyoni detached the loads, distributed the weight into packs, strapped what could be strapped onto backs. Thirty-two straps on a cart. Forty on four backs. Her hands counted without being asked.

The darkness swallowed them.

Not all at once. The daylight from the foothills slid into the fault like a liquid and thinned with each meter. Five meters: grey. Ten meters: dark grey. Twenty meters: nothing.

Jabu lit the first torch. Not fire — a chemical stick, a resin coated on a copper tube that you cracked like a bone. The greenish light flooded the walls and everyone saw at the same time.

The walls were alive.

Luminous filaments, faint, bluish, ran along the rock like a network of veins. Not moss, not lichen — something between the two, an organic matter that pulsed faintly to the rhythm of the background vibration. Magnitude 4. The wall pulsed at magnitude 4. Like a heart beating to the rhythm of the world.

Enama dropped her pack. It wasn't a voluntary gesture — her hands opened by themselves, the pack fell into the dust, and she moved toward the wall the way you approach an animal you don't want to startle. Her fingers landed on the luminous filaments.

The light reacted to touch. Where Enama pressed, the filaments intensified — bluer, almost white — then calmed when she withdrew her hand. Contact. The wall responded to Contact. Not with words, not with coded pressures, but with light. Another language. A language without skin.

She turned to the group. Her eyes shone — not with tears, with reflected light. Her hand trembled against the wall. She searched for Senzo with her gaze and when she found him, she made a gesture no one had ever seen her make. Hand open, placed flat on the living rock, fingers spread. Not human Contact. World-Contact.

Sihle, behind, was writing on his roll. He wasn't looking at the walls. He was looking at his seismograph. The curves had changed — denser, more regular, as if the rock here absorbed the waves instead of transmitting them. He noted: *biological dampening?* with a question mark that said a lot about what he thought of Enama's biology and her hands placed on the walls like a prayer.

Nandi didn't need to touch the wall to know. Her bare feet on the cavern floor told her everything. The frequency had changed again. Not upward this time — downward. Infra-bass, vibrations so deep they passed below the threshold of consciousness and entered the bones directly. Tremens answered. Her stomach contracted. Her jaw clenched. Her vision blurred.

She leaned against the wall. The luminous filaments lit up around her shoulders like a crown.

Senzo saw her. Came back to her. Placed his hand on the back of her neck. The long pressure. Warm. The one that had no official translation in Contact. The one that said nothing but: *I know you're suffering and I'm here.*

Nandi closed her eyes and let the wall's light pulse against her back.

## Scene 4 — The map and the body

The disagreement broke out at a fork.

The cavern had been descending in a gentle slope for three hours — or what felt like three hours; underground, time had no markers. No sun. No shadow. Just the blue light of the filaments and the green light of the chemical sticks and the constant, unceasing vibration of the world rumbling above, below, everywhere.

The fork was a perfect Y. Two tunnels, same width, same slope, same darkness. The rock gave no visual clue. A binary choice in a world that didn't like choices.

Senzo unrolled his map. The lines said: right. The survey — done by a previous expedition, the one no one had returned from but whose notes had risen through a fault, carried by the wind, found two years later in the desert — indicated the right tunnel as the path to the deep caverns.

Sihle confirmed. His seismograph showed stronger activity to the left — more tremors, more amplitudes. Logic said: avoid the activity. Go right. Where it's calmer. Where the rock isn't fighting itself.

Enama crouched. Placed both hands flat on the ground, at the center of the Y. Closed her eyes.

Nobody spoke. Nobody ever spoke. But the silence here had a different texture — a courtroom silence, where everyone waits for the verdict.

Enama opened her eyes. Grabbed Senzo's wrist. Four pressures. Quick. Then she pointed to the left tunnel.

Sihle stepped forward. Not toward Enama — toward Senzo. He placed his hand on the leader's forearm. Two pressures, a pause, two pressures. Technical Contact: *The data says no.* Then he pointed to the right tunnel.

Senzo looked at one. Looked at the other. Looked at the map. Looked at the ground.

Enama didn't let go of his wrist. Her grip said what her eyes said, what her hands on the ground had said: *The earth speaks, and it says left.*

Sihle brandished the seismograph roll. The curves were definitive. The left tunnel was more active. More dangerous. More unstable.

— The earth speaks, said Enama.

Her voice — her real voice, the one that came from the throat, not the one from her hands — tore through the silence like a stone into water. Everyone flinched. You didn't speak. You *never* spoke on expedition. Voice wasted energy. Voice got lost in the rumbling. Voice was an admission of weakness — the acknowledgment that Contact wasn't enough.

Enama knew it. She did it anyway.

— The earth speaks, she repeated, lower this time, almost to herself. And it says left.

Sihle didn't answer with voice. He answered with Contact — a firm grip on Senzo's forearm, firmer than Enama's, more technical, colder. His fingers said: *I'm giving you numbers. She's giving you beliefs.*

Senzo withdrew both arms. Gently. Without abruptness. He freed them from both grips — Enama's, Sihle's — and stood alone at the center of the Y, arms at his sides, the map rolled in his right hand.

He looked at Nandi.

She was leaning against the wall, eyes half-closed, bare feet on the rock. The blue filaments pulsed around her. Her face was pale. Tremens was eating her from inside. But her feet — her feet were reading.

Senzo approached her. Placed his hand on her shoulder. One pressure. Long. The silent question: *You, what do you feel?*

Nandi didn't answer right away. She listened. Not with her ears — with bones, tendons, the soles of her feet pressed into the basalt dust. The right tunnel vibrated little. Calm. Stable. The left tunnel vibrated hard. Nervous. Alive.

But beneath the nervous, beneath the surface agitation, there was something else. A deep, regular wave that descended. The left tunnel didn't lead toward chaos — it led toward the source. The seismic activity wasn't a danger. It was a path.

She grabbed Senzo's wrist. One pressure. Long. Then she pointed left.

Senzo put away his map. Walked toward the left tunnel.

Sihle stood still for three seconds. His clenched jaw made a sound the seismograph didn't register. Then he followed.

The team plunged into the left tunnel. The vibration rose. The blue filaments intensified. The world descended.

## Scene 5 — The threshold

They walked — or crawled, depending on the passages — for what could have been hours. The slope steepened. The air warmed. Sulfur stung the nostrils. Thabo kept his cloth deployed permanently now, arm outstretched before him like a dowser, stopping from time to time to rub the fibers between his fingers and feel the humidity, the heat, the particles.

The tunnel opened onto an overhang.

Before them, below, a cavern so vast the chemical sticks' light didn't reach the walls. Black. Deep. And at its center, far below, an orange glow that wasn't bioluminescence. That wasn't chemistry.

Lava. Not a river — a thread. A thin incandescent ribbon snaking along the cavern floor like a brushstroke on a black canvas. The first visible proof that the volcano was not a landscape. That the volcano was a machine. That something, beneath, was burning.

The heat rose in waves. Dry waves, without wind, that cracked the lips and split the skin of the hands. Contact was about to become painful. Touching another's skin when your own is burning — the language had a physical cost here.

Senzo stopped at the edge of the overhang. He didn't look down. He turned around and looked up — toward the tunnel entrance, toward the slope they had just descended, toward the surface they would never see again.

Then he looked at his team. Six faces lit from below, by the orange of the distant lava. Six pairs of eyes that knew.

He raised his hand. Open palm. Not the departure signal — something else. A slow gesture, the hand rising, turning over, and descending in an arc. A gesture from ancient Contact, the kind no longer used on the surface because the surface had no need for this word.

*We don't go back up.*

Nandi felt the word in her belly before reading it on Senzo's hand. Tremens had already told her. Her feet had known since the fault. Every meter of descent had been one meter less between them and magnitude 11.

Inyoni counted. A reflex — she always counted. Seven people. Four chemical sticks. Rations for fifty-two days. Three hundred meters of rope. One detonator. One seismograph. One cloth.

And Contact.

She tightened the nearest strap. *Holds.*

The team descended into the cavern. The orange light swallowed them from below, and the blue light of the filaments followed them along the walls, and between the two lights there were the seven bodies carrying the only language the world had not yet destroyed.

But the world hadn't finished trying.

# III — The Last Village

## Scene 1 — Approach

Nandi felt them before she saw them.

Not people — frequencies. An alteration in the background vibration, an inflection that her feet deciphered without her needing to think about it. Something, ahead, was absorbing the waves. Not rock. Not void. Something supple. Something built.

Something alive.

The cavern had changed since the overhang. The ceiling had risen, the walls had widened, and the bioluminescent filaments running along the rock had taken on a different aspect — trimmed. Not naturally distributed but channeled, guided along grooves carved into the wall. Someone had domesticated the blue light. Someone knew that light grew here and had put it in rows.

Thabo stopped. His cloth hung from his right hand, inert, and for the first time since the desert he wasn't looking at the fibers but straight ahead. The air was different. More humid. Warmer too, but with an organic warmth, not mineral — a warmth of bodies, of breathing, of presence.

Senzo raised his fist. Stop signal. The team froze.

Before them, at the end of a natural corridor that the blue light illuminated like a theater hallway, something stood. Shapes. Low, rounded, without angles. Structures — not rock, not metal. Canvas. Tanned leather. Hides stretched over basalt frames, secured by ropes that sank into the ground and were anchored at depths you could guess from the tension.

Dwellings. Built to flex, not to resist. Built for the world as it was — in motion.

Inyoni counted. A reflex. Twelve structures. Maybe fifteen — some merged into the wall, half rock, half fabric. Hammocks visible inside the nearest ones, suspended on elastic ropes identical to those at the surface camp. The same solutions, discovered independently, thousands of meters apart in distance and depth. As if the world dictated the form and the hands merely obeyed.

Senzo turned to the team. Open hand, fingers spread downward, slow movement. Standard Contact: *We approach. No visible threat. Stay alert anyway.*

Enama placed her hands on the ground. Closed her eyes. The background tremor was there — magnitude 4, constant — but filtered. Dampened. The ground under her palms vibrated differently. Someone had done something to the ground. Trenches, perhaps, or layers of absorbent material — she couldn't tell. But the village didn't shake like the rest of the world. It shook less. Or rather: it shook *with*.

Nandi vomited. Not from the tremens — from the change. Her body, recalibrated for three days to the frequencies of the caverns, had just received a new signal. Lower. More stable. Almost… domestic. Her stomach protested as it protested at every transition, but this time something else accompanied the nausea. Something she hadn't felt since departure.

Relief.

Her feet recognized this frequency. Not this one exactly — but the principle. The frequency of an inhabited place. A place where someone had taken the time to listen to the ground and build accordingly. Her body knew it before her mind.

Senzo resumed the march. Slower now. Left hand on his hip, ready for Contact. Right hand free, open, visible. The universal gesture of someone who arrives and means no harm.

They entered the village.

And no one came out to greet them.

## Scene 2 — The Village of the Deaf

The first faces appeared in the openings of the structures — not doors, there were no doors, just slits in the stretched leather, low openings you passed through by bending. Eyes first. Then hands. Hands placed on the ledges, on the ground, on the anchor ropes. Hands that touched the world before looking at the visitors.

A woman came out first. Old — not by her face, by her hands. Hands worn to the bone, skin thin as seismograph paper, nails short, fingers deformed by a lifetime of Contact. She planted herself in front of Senzo and looked at him.

Senzo spoke.

— We come from the surface. Expedition heading down.

His voice resonated in the cavern. Loud. Too loud. Carried by the natural acoustics of the rock, amplified by the concave walls, his sentence bounced from wall to wall like a stone in a well.

The woman didn't react.

Not a flinch. Not a movement of the eyes toward the source of the sound. Nothing. As if Senzo had opened his mouth and nothing had come out.

Enama understood first. She touched Senzo's arm. Two brief pressures, then a hand placed on her own ear. Improvised Contact but clear: *She can't hear.*

Senzo looked at the woman. Looked at her hands. Looked at the others — a dozen faces now, emerged from the structures, standing in the blue light, all eyes turned toward the visitors. All hands placed somewhere — on a wall, on the ground, on a neighboring body. All connected.

None spoke. None seemed to be waiting for speech.

The woman raised her right hand. Open palm, fingers pressed together. Not Senzo's gesture — not their Contact. A different one. The wrist turned inward, fingers pointing down, then a circular movement, slow, ending with the palm laid flat on her own chest.

No one on the team recognized the gesture.

Sihle leaned toward Senzo. Hand on the forearm, technical pressure. *They're deaf. Acoustic damage. Probably the deep quakes — the infra-bass destroys the hair cells.*

Senzo nodded. Then he did the only thing left to do. He raised his own hand. Open palm. And placed it on the woman's shoulder. One pressure. Long. The simplest gesture of Contact. The one that means nothing else but: *I'm here. I'm touching you. We exist in the same world.*

The woman smiled.

Not a smile of joy — a smile of recognition. The smile of someone who finally hears a known language, even with a foreign accent. She placed her hand on Senzo's shoulder in return. Same pressure. Same duration. Same warmth.

But the fingers were different. Where Senzo used the full palm — the surface gesture, broad, inclusive — the woman used only three fingers. Thumb, index, middle. The precision fingers. The Contact here was finer. More economical. Forged by decades of exclusive communication — not a complement to voice, but a total replacement.

Their Contact had never needed to palm broad. Their hands had always been the only instrument.

---

## Scene 3 — The Learning

The first day in the village was a language lesson.

Not a formal lesson — there was no teacher, no method, no table. Just hands placed on other hands and bodies learning by doing.

The old woman — the team never learned her name, because names here weren't spoken, they were touched: a sequence of pressures on the wrist, unique to each individual, like an inverted fingerprint — sat with Nandi in one of the structures. The ground vibrated. The canvas trembled. The blue filaments pulsed to the rhythm of the background magnitude. And the two women touched hands.

Nandi learned fast. Not through intelligence — through sensitivity. The same nerve endings that made her body a seismograph made her fingers antennae. She felt the micro-pressures that others didn't feel. The old woman's Contact wasn't just different in its gestures — it was different in its *resolution*. More nuances per centimeter of skin. More information per millisecond of contact. A high-definition language, where surface Contact was low resolution by comparison.

The woman took Nandi's wrist. Placed three fingers — thumb, index, middle — on the inner side, where the pulse beat. Pressed once. Twice. Then something Nandi had never felt: a rotational movement of the thumb, barely perceptible, a circular micro-glide across the skin while the other two fingers maintained pressure.

The gesture meant something. Nandi didn't know what. But her body responded before her mind — her heart rate slowed. Her shoulders dropped. Her belly unclenched. The gesture didn't carry information — it carried a state. A transfer of state through touch. Contact pushed beyond language, into physiology.

*Calm.*

The woman hadn't told her "calm down." She had *calmed*.

Enama, sitting two meters away, watched. Her hands trembled — not from cold, not from tremens. From professional jealousy. She who had spent her life listening to the body, trusting physical signals against Sihle's instruments, she saw here a woman for whom the body wasn't an argument in a debate. The body was the debate. The body was the language, the message, and the medium. No Sihle to contest. No seismograph to measure. Just skin and intention.

She moved closer. The woman looked at her. Extended a hand. Enama took it.

Contact began.

Meanwhile, Sihle stayed apart. Sitting on a supply crate, his seismograph on his knees, he took notes. The readings here were remarkable — a systematic dampening of 40% compared to the surrounding bare rock. The village absorbed the quakes. Not by magic, not by instinct — by engineering. The trenches he could infer beneath the structures, the fill materials, the deep anchoring of the ropes. Someone had calculated. Someone had measured, tested, adjusted. Someone had done science without instruments.

He looked at his readings. Looked at the old woman's hands on Nandi's wrist. Looked at the trenches in the ground.

And for the first time since departure, he wrote nothing in the margin.

---

## Scene 4 — The Warning

The old man was the last to come.

Older than the woman. Older than anyone in the village, perhaps older than anyone the expedition had ever met. His hands were archives — every wrinkle, every callus, every scar was a word spoken thousands of times. His fingers had the permanent curvature of those who have never stopped touching.

He sat facing Senzo. Not facing — *against*. Their knees touched. Contact began with proximity.

The old man took both of Senzo's hands. Turned them over. Examined the palms like reading a map. Then placed them on the ground. Senzo's hands, pressed flat to the ground by the old man's hands.

*Listen.*

Senzo listened. Not with his ears — with his palms. The ground vibrated. Magnitude 4. Constant. Normal.

The old man withdrew his hands. Placed his own on the ground. Closed his eyes. Then he made a gesture no one understood at the time — he struck the ground. One sharp blow. With the flat of his palm. The sound cracked through the cavern and the blue filaments reacted: a flash, brief, like lightning in a sky of veins.

The ground answered.

Not a quake. Not a tremor. An echo. The vibration from the palm-strike traveled down into the rock, bounced off something below — far below — and came back up. Altered. Slower. Deeper. Like a voice returning from an abyss.

Nandi felt it. Fell to her knees. Her hands pressed flat to the ground on their own, her fingers dug into the dust, and her whole body tensed like a string being pulled. The echo passed through her bones. The feet. The shins. The pelvis. The spine. Up to the teeth that vibrated in her jaws like tuning forks.

The echo was saying something. Not a word. A change.

The old man looked at Senzo. Took his wrist. Three fingers — thumb, index, middle. Rapid pressures. One, two, three, four. Pause. Five, six, seven. Pause. Eight, nine. Pause. Ten.

Senzo didn't understand the count. But Nandi, on her knees, hands on the ground, understood something else. The echo rising from the depths had a rhythm. And the rhythm had changed. Not since yesterday, not since last week — for months. The old man had been counting it. Every day. Every quake. Every echo. And the intervals were shortening.

Something was rising. Faster.

The old man released Senzo's wrist. Placed his hand on the ground, palm flat, and made the circular gesture — the same one the woman had made at first Contact. But this time, the movement didn't say *calm*. The movement was faster. Tighter. The fingers pressed harder.

Enama, who had learned the rudiments of local Contact since that morning, translated. Not with words — with her body. She grabbed Senzo's arm and squeezed. Hard. Too hard for vocabulary. The kind of pressure that has only one translation:

*Hurry.*

Sihle stepped forward. He held out his seismograph toward the old man, the scroll unrolled, the readings visible. He pointed to the needle, the amplitudes, the intervals. A language of data offered to a man who had never seen data.

The old man looked at the seismograph. A long time. Then he placed his hand on it. Not on the readings — on the mechanism. The copper casing. The needle. He felt the vibrations of the mechanism under his fingers. The instrument trembled to the rhythm of the world.

And the old man shook his head.

The gesture belonged to no Contact — neither surface nor village. It was a human gesture. Universal. The one that said: *It's not enough.*

Sihle took back his seismograph. He said nothing. He wrote nothing in the margin.

---

## Scene 5 — The Unknown Gesture

The last evening — if it was an evening; underground, the villagers marked time by the cycles of bioluminescence, the bluish filaments that faded every six hours before regaining their glow —, the old woman came to find Nandi.

Nandi was sitting at the edge of the village, her bare feet in a channel carved into the rock. Warm water flowed there — an underground spring channeled by the villagers, a thread of water that wound between the structures and that, Nandi had understood, served as much for drinking as for communicating. Variations in flow carried messages. When someone upstream diverted the flow, someone downstream felt it. The village had invented a hydraulic Contact.

The woman sat beside her. Not facing — beside. Shoulders touching. The most passive Contact. The one that demands nothing.

They stayed like that a moment. The blue filaments faded. The ground vibrated. Water flowed between Nandi's toes.

Then the woman took Nandi's hands.

Not the wrists — the hands. Palms against palms. An intimate gesture in any register of Contact, surface or depth. The palms were the place of tactile truth — the thinnest skin, the densest nerve endings, the place where the body didn't know how to lie.

The woman closed her eyes. Nandi closed hers.

And the woman made the gesture.

Not a finger gesture. Not a pressure. Not a glide. Something deeper — a tremor. A voluntary tremor, micro-controlled, that started from the old woman's forearms, descended through the wrists, crossed the palms and entered Nandi's hands. A tremor that had a frequency. A rhythm. A meaning.

Nandi felt it and didn't understand.

The gesture resembled tremens. Not Nandi's — a different tremens. A willed tremens. Manufactured. The woman's body reproduced the vibrations of the earth and sent them into another body through the hands. Contact, pushed to its logical extreme: no longer speaking about the trembling, but trembling together.

Nandi opened her eyes. The woman was looking at her. Her eyes were calm. Her hands still trembled. The message continued.

Nandi didn't understand the message. Not yet. Her body had received it — she felt it in her bones, in the resonance of her feet on the ground, in the way her own tremens had shifted frequency for an instant, tuned to the woman's trembling. But the translation wasn't there. The word didn't exist in her Contact.

The woman withdrew her hands. Placed three fingers on Nandi's heart. Pressed. Once.

Then she stood and went back into her structure.

Nandi stayed sitting. Feet in the water. Hands open. Full of a word she didn't know.

She would keep it.

---

## Scene 6 — The Final Threshold

The departure happened without ceremony.

Not because the villagers didn't want one — because Contact, here, didn't know ceremonies. No speeches. No blessings. No "come back alive." Contact said what was. It didn't say what wasn't going to be.

The team gathered the packs. Inyoni checked the straps — thirty-two on the packs, plus the twenty-four on the explosive charge that Sihle had recalculated during the night. Her hands counted. Her hands never stopped counting.

Jabu filled the canteens at the channel. The water was good — better than in the desert, filtered by the rock, warm, slightly mineral. He filled more than necessary. Reflex of an ocean specialist: water, you never have too much. You always don't have enough.

The villagers came out of their structures. Not all — seven. The same number as the expedition. Chance or intention, no one would know. Seven silhouettes standing in the blue light, hands placed on the walls, on the ground, on each other's shoulders. A network of Contact. A closed circuit.

The old woman approached Senzo. Touched his forehead. A gesture the team didn't know — Contact didn't touch the face on the surface. The face was the domain of voice, of eyes, of what is not touched. But here, the face didn't belong to voice — there was no voice. The face belonged to Contact like everything else.

The pressure of her three fingers on Senzo's forehead lasted five seconds. He closed his eyes. When he opened them, the woman had stepped back.

A man — the old man from the warning — came to Thabo. He took the cloth from the Airmaster's hands. Held it a moment. Gave it back. But before giving it back, he blew on it. A long, steady breath that made the fibers dance. The cloth now carried the breath of the village. The air from below. The air that Thabo would have to learn to read.

Thabo put the cloth away without shaking it. The air would stay.

A young girl — the only child visible in the village — approached Inyoni. Didn't touch her. Just looked at her. Then she raised both hands, palms to the sky, and closed them. A gesture that even the village's Contact didn't seem to know — the adults watched her with the surprise of those who hear a new word in their own language.

Inyoni counted the fingers closing. Ten. Then the child opened her hands. Ten fingers. Ten fingers like the old man's ten numbers. Ten like a countdown or like a promise.

Inyoni didn't know which. She tightened the nearest strap. *Holds.*

Then Senzo gave the signal. Hand raised, fist closed, fingers opening. *Let's go.* The same gesture as at departure from the desert. The same gesture as at every departure. But here, in the blue light, before the seven mute silhouettes of the village, the gesture had a weight it had never had. The weight of *last time*.

The team entered the tunnel that descended beyond the village. Narrower. Darker. The blue filaments grew sparse — trimmed less and less far by the villagers, then not at all. The natural bioluminescent light returned, weaker, sporadic, a glow that pulsed like a sick heart.

Nandi walked barefoot. The ground under her toes had changed again — hotter, drier, the vibrations higher, more nervous. The tremens clenched her stomach but she walked through it. Her body had received something in that village. Not an answer — a tool. The old woman's gesture. The voluntary tremor. She didn't yet know what it was, but her hands carried it the way you carry a word learned in a dream.

Enama turned around. The village had disappeared. Not into darkness — into rock. The tunnel had turned and the blue light of the domesticated filaments no longer reached. Behind them: stone. Before them: stone. The only difference between the two directions was that one went up and the other went down.

Senzo didn't turn around. He never turned around. His gaze searched the point ahead, beyond the curve of the tunnel, beyond the rock, somewhere in the dark where the mission waited for him.

He placed his hand on the back of Nandi's neck. The long pressure. Warm. Then he made a gesture the team knew but that he had made only once — at the edge of the overhang, facing the lava.

Hand raised, descending arc.

*We don't go back up.*

Behind them, the village of the deaf continued to live in silence. Contact passed from hand to hand. The ground vibrated. Water flowed in the channels. And the old woman, sitting before her structure, hands placed on the rock, sent a tremor into the ground that descended, descended, followed the seven bodies moving away in the tunnel, and that said — in a language that only Nandi could have translated, if she had known how to listen — something that resembled:

*The world is waiting for you. It will not be kind.*

The blue filaments pulsed one last time. Then the light of the village went out behind the bend, and the team walked into the dark.

Seven people. Toward the bottom. Where Contact was going to begin to die.

# IV — The Fault

## Scene 1 — The vertical passage

The descent became ascent.

That's the volcano's trap — you think you're descending, you descend, and then the tunnel turns on itself like an animal baring its teeth, and the floor becomes wall, the wall becomes ceiling, and you have to climb to keep falling.

The fault opened before them like a vertical wound in the rock. Not wide — two meters, maybe three. Tall — tall enough that the light from the glow sticks didn't reach the top. And above all: deep. The fault didn't rise toward the surface. It descended at an angle, a twisted shaft, tilted at sixty degrees, its walls seeping hot water that smelled of iron and sulfur.

Thabo was the first to stop. His cloth, held at arm's length in front of the opening, no longer danced — it *sucked inward*. The fibers pressed flat against his hand as if glued by an invisible force. The air current was coming from below. Powerful. Steady. Hot.

He turned to Senzo. Hand flat, palm facing the ground, then an upward motion — the inverted gesture. *The air rises. It goes down.*

Senzo approached the edge. Looked. The greenish light of the glow stick only lit the first few meters — rock holds, basalt ledges, cracks where fingers could fit. After that: black. Black that descended.

Inyoni was already on her knees. Her hands traveled the wall like a blind person reading a page. She touched every bump, every hollow, every lip of rock. Her fingers were counting. Not the holds — the distances between the holds. The intervals. The grammar of the descent.

She stood up. Uncoiled the rope. Fifty meters of braided fiber, thick as a thumb, coated in resin against moisture. She tied a knot — a knot only she knew how to tie, an eight-twist knot that tightened under load and never gave, never ever gave, like the straps, like the ropes, like everything Inyoni touched.

The Contact was about to change.

Nandi felt it before the others — not in her feet this time, but in her hands. The rope was a medium. A channel. When seven bodies clung to it, it transmitted everything: weight, movement, fear. But also — and this is where the Contact reinvented itself — intentional vibrations. Deliberate shakes. Messages.

Inyoni set the first anchor. A copper piton hammered into the rock — the sound resonated through the fault like a metallic heartbeat. The rope passed through it. The carabiner snapped shut.

Senzo turned to the team. Six faces in the green light. He raised his hand. Fist closed, then open — but this time, the fingers didn't spread upward. They went down. The same departure gesture, inverted by gravity.

*We descend.*

Inyoni went first. This was her world — the vertical. Her hands on the rope, her feet on the rock, her body between the two, fluid, precise, like a word articulated without hesitation. She disappeared into the fault in three movements. The rope vibrated under her descent — a steady, confident vibration that told the others: *It holds. Come.*

Senzo went second. Then Nandi, bare feet against the wet rock, toes searching for holds like fingers searching for words. Then Enama. Thabo. Jabu.

Sihle went last. His seismograph beat against his chest, the curves drawing sawtooth lines with each impact. He didn't like the rope. The rope didn't give data. The rope didn't measure anything. The rope only connected bodies falling together.

The fault swallowed them.

And for the first time since departure, they couldn't see each other. The darkness between bodies, the rock between hands, the distance between rope knots — everything separated. Direct-touch Contact was dead. The rope remained.

The rope, and what it could say.

---

## Scene 2 — Senzo chooses the route

The fault branched.

After two hours of descent — or what the muscles called two hours, that pain in the forearms that corresponded to roughly seven hundred meters of elevation change — the left wall opened up. A horizontal gallery, wide, its floor appearing dry. The fault continued below, narrower, hotter, water seeping from the walls in threads that gleamed in the glow stick's light.

Inyoni planted a piton. The team regrouped on a ledge — a basalt shelf wide enough for six, not for seven. Jabu stayed suspended, feet wedged in a crack, hands on the rope. He didn't complain. Jabu never complained.

Senzo unrolled the map. A surface gesture — the map had been drawn for horizontal tunnels, not for shafts. The lines didn't know how to go down. But something resembled what they were seeing: a junction, marked with a cross, with two words written by a dead hand. *Stable gallery* to the left. Nothing below. Nothing, because those who had drawn the map hadn't gone down.

Sihle placed his hand on Senzo's arm. Technical Contact. Two fingers on the forearm, measured pressure. He pointed to the left gallery. His seismograph confirmed: seismic activity was lower there. Fewer frequencies. Fewer risks. The gallery was stable — the readings said so, the map said so, logic said so.

Enama crouched on the ledge. Placed her hands on the rock. Closed her eyes. The ritual had become familiar — Enama listening to the ground, Sihle reading his curves, and the world between the two refusing to be summarized.

She reopened her eyes. Grabbed Senzo's wrist. Squeezed. Hard. Then she pointed down. The shaft. The unknown.

Sihle didn't move. He didn't need to move. His grip on Senzo's arm was still there, two fingers still resting, the pressure still technical. *The data says no.*

Senzo looked at the gallery. Looked at the shaft. Looked at the map that didn't know how to go down.

Then he did what Senzo always did. He chose neither one nor the other. He chose a third path — a crack in the opposite wall, narrow, nearly invisible, that nobody had noticed because it was in the gallery's shadow. Not on the map. Not in Sihle's data. Not in Enama's hands.

He pointed to the crack. Hand extended, palm facing up. The gesture of someone who says: *That way.*

Sihle withdrew his fingers from Senzo's forearm. A slow withdrawal. Deliberate. The Contact of someone who disagrees but won't argue. Not here. Not in the void. Not when the only thing anchoring them to the world was a fifty-meter rope.

Enama looked at the crack. Placed her hand on the ground one last time. Something passed in her eyes — not resistance. Resignation. Her body had said nothing about this crack. Her body had felt nothing.

Senzo began to cross. The crack was narrow — the width of a body. He inserted himself sideways, the pack crushed against the wall, hands flat on either side. The rock was hot. Wet. It vibrated under his palms — magnitude 4, constant, the heartbeat of the world.

The team followed. One by one. Through the crack the leader had chosen. The crack no one had seen.

The crack the volcano had left open.

---

## Scene 3 — Rope-Contact

In the crack, the Contact died. Then it was born elsewhere.

Impossible to touch each other — arms pinned to the body, shoulders pressed against rock on both sides, feet advancing centimeter by centimeter. The space between the walls left room only for the body. Not for gestures. Not for Contact words. Nothing.

And the noise. The noise of the fault — a permanent rumble, amplified by the narrow walls, filling the air like water fills a glass. The sound of rock working, contracting, expanding, grinding against itself like teeth clenching. A voice — if anyone had dared to speak — would have been swallowed.

The rope remained.

It ran between them like a nerve. Each body was attached to it by harness, spaced seven meters apart, and when one moved, the other six felt it. Not the movement — the vibration. The rope transmitted the body's intentions before the body carried them out. A foot searching for a hold: the rope quivers. An arm pressing against the wall: the rope undulates. A breath that quickens — even that, the rope carried.

Inyoni, in the lead, discovered it first. She stopped in front of an even narrower passage — the width of a rib cage — and she hesitated. A fraction of a second. Her body, accustomed to never hesitating, suspended its movement, and the rope transmitted that suspension to the six bodies behind her. Not a word. Not a gesture. Just a silence in the vibration.

And everyone understood: *Stop.*

Inyoni gripped the rope with both hands. Shook it three times. Short, short, long. A code invented in the moment — not old Contact, not surface Contact, not even Contact from the deaf village. Rope-Contact. A language born in the last ten minutes, invented by necessity, by the impossibility of doing otherwise.

Three shakes. *Stop.* Or *danger.* Or both.

Behind her, Senzo received the message. His hands on the rope felt the vibrations and something in him — not reason, not instinct, something between the two, the muscle of the leader — translated. He sent back: two shakes. Short. *Understood.*

The code was building itself. Shake by shake, the rope invented its vocabulary. One long shake: *Advance.* Two short: *OK.* Three: *Stop.* A rapid, nervous series: *Problem.* A total slackening, the rope going limp: *Rest.*

But the rope couldn't say everything. It couldn't say *I'm afraid*. It couldn't say *the air is changing*. It couldn't say *listen to the ground*. The rope was a language of survival, not a language of thought. Surface Contact had the nape for intimacy, the shoulder for urgency, the wrist for data. The rope had only tension and movement. Two dimensions where Contact had twenty.

Nandi, third on the rope, felt the difference. Her hands — the predictor's hands, the hands the old woman of the village had filled with an unknown word — touched the rope and didn't find what they were looking for. The rope didn't tremble like the earth. The rope didn't carry deep frequencies. The rope spoke only of bodies.

Not of the world.

And it was in this absence — in the silence between what the rope said and what the earth wanted to say — that Nandi felt the first quake of the fault.

Not through her feet. Not through the rope. Through her bones. A deep shiver, beyond tremens, that came not from the surface but from the interior of the rock itself. The fault was breathing. And its breathing was accelerating.

She couldn't send anything through the rope to say it. The vocabulary didn't exist yet.

---

## Scene 4 — Quakes in the wall

The first quake struck when they were midway through the crack.

Midway up or midway down — the distinction had lost its meaning. The crack descended and ascended simultaneously, a shaft twisted like a stone intestine, and the bodies crossing it no longer knew where up was. The only certainty: the rope. The rope that connected. The rope that knew.

The shock came from the right.

Not from below — from the right. The fault had its own geology, its own fracture lines, and seismic waves didn't travel vertically here. They traveled *laterally*, bouncing between the walls like bullets in a corridor. The two walls closed in by a centimeter — then spread apart by two. Inyoni's body, wedged between the walls, was compressed then released like a beating heart. She didn't scream. Her hands gripped the rope and she sent the signal — three shakes, fast, brutal. *Stop.*

The message traveled down the rope. Body by body. Senzo received it. Passed it on. Nandi. Enama. Thabo. Jabu. Sihle last.

Then the wall struck again.

Magnitude 5. Maybe 6. In the crack, the distinction didn't matter — a magnitude 5 in a space sixty centimeters wide was a world closing in on you. The walls oscillated. Rock fragments fell from above, fist-sized chunks that ricocheted between the walls before vanishing into the black below. The noise became physical — not a sound but a pressure, a shockwave in the air that compressed the lungs.

The rope transmitted everything.

Inyoni's weight pressing against the wall. Senzo's grip tightening. Jabu's panic — a series of involuntary shakes, not vocabulary, just a body trembling that the rope couldn't distinguish from a message. The rope carried meaning and noise. Signal and interference. And in that chaos, someone had to read.

Inyoni read.

Her hands on the rope filtered. She felt the vibrations of the quake — deep, regular, mechanical — and separated them from the vibrations of the bodies — irregular, organic, human. One came from the rock. The other came from the gut. The climber knew how to tell the difference. Not by training. By vocation. Her fingers had always known what the rope carried, the way Nandi's fingers had always known what the ground said.

She sent a message. Not three shakes — a new pattern. Long, short, long, pause, short, short. A complex message, the longest since the beginning of Rope-Contact. The bodies below received it and didn't understand right away.

Senzo understood. He transmitted it to Nandi through the rope, adding his own interpretation — two shakes after the pattern, an addendum that said: *Do it.* Nandi touched the rope and knew: Inyoni wanted them to advance. During the quake. Not after — during. Because the rock between shocks was more unstable than at the moment of impact. The fractures propagated in the silence after, not in the noise during.

The climber was right. Nandi felt it in her bones — the same truth, translated by another body.

They advanced. While the world struck the walls, while the rock compressed them and released them and compressed them again, they crawled through the crack. The rope vibrated with everything — quakes, bodies, messages — and in that everything, those who knew how to read read, and the others trusted.

Sihle, in last position, read nothing in the rope. But his seismograph, pressed against his chest, was drawing curves no one saw in the dark. Curves that said what the rope didn't say: the crack was moving. Not just vibrating — moving. Shifting laterally, millimeter by millimeter, like a jaw closing.

He couldn't tell anyone.

---

## Scene 5 — Senzo

The crack opened onto a landing.

Not a ledge — a real landing, five meters wide, carved into the rock as if the volcano had wanted to offer a reprieve. The blue filaments had returned — rare, scattered, but present, pulsing faintly in the darkness. And before the landing, the fault resumed. Wider now. More open. The descent became again what it had always been: downward.

The team was regrouping. Inyoni was already sitting, the ropes around her like a nest, her hands checking the knots. Nandi had collapsed against the wall, her feet bleeding — the rock in the crack had bitten her soles —, her hands on her stomach. The tremens was punishing her. The seven-hundred-meter descent through new frequencies had shaken her body like a detuned instrument.

Senzo approached her. Not fast. Not slow. The exact speed. He placed his hand on her nape. The long pressure. Warm. The one that had no name in any register of Contact except theirs.

Nandi looked up. Smiled. A tremens smile — lips rising despite the body, will slipping between the spasms.

Senzo straightened up. Looked at what lay ahead. Three routes. Three passages descending from the landing — one to the left, one in the center, one to the right. The fault was branching again, dividing like a root network, each branch plunging toward its own version of the bottom.

He took out the map. The lines said nothing. After the cross of the previous junction, the map was blank. Terra incognita.

Sihle stepped forward. His seismograph was telling a story no one else could read: the left passage vibrated strongly, high and nervous frequencies. The center vibrated little — too little, abnormally little, like a heart skipping beats. The right vibrated steadily — magnitude 4, constant, the pulse of the world.

He placed his hand on Senzo's arm. Pointed right. The gesture had become ritual, almost automatic. *My data says right.*

Enama didn't crouch this time. She stayed standing. Looked at the three passages. Didn't touch the ground. Not because she didn't need to — because something in her body had changed since the crack. The compression between the walls, the Rope-Contact that carried nothing of the world, the obligation to trust something other than her hands. She didn't know what her hands were saying about these three passages. She no longer knew.

She pointed at nothing. For the first time, Enama had no opinion.

Senzo looked at her. Looked at Sihle. Looked at the three passages.

He chose the center.

Sihle withdrew his hand. Not slowly this time — abruptly. The withdrawal of technical Contact when data is ignored. But he followed. They all followed.

The central passage was wide. Almost comfortable — smooth rock, gently sloping floor, blue filaments lining the walls like guides. Too comfortable. Nandi walked barefoot on the smooth floor and her toes found nothing. No micro-vibrations. No signals. The floor was silent. Not like the village floor that trembled *with* — a different silence. A silence of absence.

A silence of trap.

She stopped. Grabbed the rope still linking them. Shook it. Three shakes. *Stop.*

Senzo turned around. Hand on Nandi's shoulder — the question.

She knelt. Placed both hands flat on the floor. Closed her eyes. Searched. Beneath the silence, beneath the absence, beneath the nothing — something. No. Nothing. The floor wasn't speaking. The floor wasn't speaking anymore. As if the rock here had been severed from its seismic roots, isolated from the network of vibrations that irrigated the world.

Her hands searched deeper. Further. Beyond the floor, beyond the rock, into that place beneath the frequencies where the tremens sometimes went when it found nothing left to translate.

And there, at the very bottom, she felt.

The floor wasn't silent. The floor was *held back*. Like a blocked breath. Like a scream rising in the throat that doesn't come out. The rock was accumulating. For hours, days, weeks maybe — she didn't know. The rock was accumulating tension without releasing it. No micro-quakes. No background tremors. No pulse. Because all the energy was concentrating. Compressing. Waiting.

She reopened her eyes. Grabbed Senzo's wrist. Squeezed hard. Too hard for vocabulary. Then she made the gesture — the old Contact gesture, the one Senzo had invented at the beginning of the expedition for situations that had no word:

Open palm. Five fingers spread. Hand closing slowly, finger by finger, into a fist. *It's tightening.*

Senzo understood. He always understood Nandi — not because he felt the ground, but because he felt Nandi. He raised his fist. Stop signal. Then he stepped back one pace, two paces, searched for the wall with his eyes —

The ground gave way.

Not a quake. A slide. The difference was between a punch and a knife — the quake strikes and releases, the slide cuts and doesn't stop. The ground under their feet — that smooth ground, that too-silent ground — began to move. Not downward. To the right. The entire rock was sliding, a basalt slab three meters thick detaching from the mountain like dead skin.

The team fell. Not all at once — in cascade. Nandi first, carried by the ground opening beneath her bare feet. Then Enama who caught her — grip on the wrist, survival grip, not vocabulary. Then Thabo, Jabu, Sihle — the rope linked them all in the fall, pulled them toward each other like a net closing.

Senzo didn't fall.

Senzo saw.

He saw the ground separate — the rock slab sliding, the cracks opening like mouths, the black beneath. He saw Nandi fall. He saw Enama grab. He saw the rope go taut between him and the six bodies below.

And he saw the wall.

Above the spot where the slab had detached, the gallery wall was vibrating. Not background vibration — local, specific vibration, the kind of vibration Sihle would have identified in a second if his seismograph hadn't been pressed against his chest in the fall. The wall was going to follow. The wall was going to fall. Tons of basalt, suspended above the open fault, waiting only for the next shock to collapse onto the six bodies below.

Senzo planted his feet. Pulled the rope. His arms — the arms of the leader, the arms that placed hands on shoulders, that touched Nandi's nape, that raised the fist to say *let's go* — his arms pulled. Hard. Hard enough for the six bodies to slide toward him, toward the stable edge of the landing.

Enama came up first. Then Nandi. Then the others — one by one, gripping the rope, feet against the still-moving rock. Senzo held. The veins in his forearms stood out like the blue filaments on the walls. His hands didn't let go. His hands never let go.

Sihle passed the edge. Last. He rolled onto the stable landing and released the rope. He looked up at Senzo —

The wall fell.

No warning. Not the micro-silence Nandi usually felt. The rock simply broke away — an entire section of the wall, ten meters tall, three meters thick, tilting inward into the gallery like a felled tree.

Senzo was between the team and the wall. He had pulled the rope from the edge of the landing. The edge that ran along the wall. The edge where the wall was falling.

He had time to look at Nandi. No gesture. No Contact. No tactile word for what was happening. Just his eyes — the eyes of the leader, the cartographer, the man who chose the route and who had chosen this one — finding Nandi's eyes in the chaos of dust and rock.

The rope went taut. Taut to the limit — the full length between Senzo and Inyoni, the first anchor, vibrating like a harp string. The rope carried one last message. Not three shakes. Not a code. Just a total, absolute tension, the weight of a body holding on to nothing else.

Then the rope went slack.

All at once. Like a breath leaving. Like a word stopping in the middle.

The dust took a long time to settle. Minutes. When it did, the central passage no longer existed. A wall of fresh rock — basalt ripped from the wall, piled like the rubble of a house no one had lived in — blocked the gallery from wall to wall.

On the other side of the wall: nothing. No sound. No knocking against the rock. No vibration.

Inyoni pulled the rope. The rope came. Light. Without weight. The end was clean — not cut. Torn. The fibers splayed open like fingers spreading apart.

Six people on the landing.

The rope in Inyoni's hands.

And the silence. Not the silence of the world — the silence of the rope. The silence of a voice that has just gone out.

---

## Scene 6 — The first mourning

Nobody moved.

Not from shock — the shock would come later. From incomprehension. The Contact had no word for this. Six people knew how to say *Stop*, *Advance*, *Danger*, *Listen*, *We don't go back up*. Six people knew how to touch an arm, a shoulder, a wrist, a nape. Six people spoke a language of touch that covered survival, information, emotion, intimacy.

None of those words covered absence.

Nandi was the first to try. Her hands — the hands full of the old woman's word, the hands that read the ground, the hands Senzo had held — her hands placed themselves on the wall of rubble. Not to clear it. Not to search. To touch. The last place Senzo had been. The rock that had taken him.

The rock didn't answer. The rock had never answered — Contact was a language between humans, not between humans and stone. But Nandi's hands stayed. A long time. As if they were waiting for the rock to learn.

Enama sat down. Her hands were trembling. Not from tremens — Enama had never had the tremens. Her hands trembled because they were searching for a gesture they couldn't find. She wanted to say something. Something for Senzo. Something large, solemn, final. And the Contact — that language she had defended against Sihle, that body-language she had brandished as truth — the Contact didn't have that word.

Thabo folded his cloth. A slow, methodical gesture, each fold over the last. He wasn't testing the air. He was folding because his hands needed to do something and folding was what he knew. When the cloth was folded — a perfect square, the fibers aligned — he unfolded it and started again.

Inyoni was counting. Her fingers passed and repassed over the rope — the rope whose end was missing a knot. She was counting the torn fibers. Forty-seven. She counted them again. Forty-seven. Again. Forty-seven. The number didn't change. The number was the only word she had.

Jabu did nothing. Standing at the edge of the landing, his gaze turned toward the left passage — the one Sihle had proposed, the one Senzo had refused — he did nothing. His hands hung at his sides. Empty. Contact can't be done alone. Contact needs a recipient. And Jabu, in that moment, had no one to send what he was carrying to.

Sihle put away his seismograph. Slow gesture. Almost ceremonial. He unrolled the paper, looked at the curves from the last few minutes — the sawtooth of the slide, the abrupt stop, the flat line. The flat line that matched the slack rope. Data and body, for once, said the same thing: it's over.

He rolled up the scroll. Buckled the harness. Then he sat down next to Enama without touching her.

The silence lasted.

Not the silence of the world — the world was still rumbling, magnitude 4, constant, faithful to its indifference. The silence of the group. The silence of six people discovering a hole in their language. A hole the exact shape of Senzo.

Because mourning, in the Contact, wasn't just the absence of a person. It was the absence of a vocabulary. The gestures that were Senzo's — his way of placing his hand on a shoulder, three slow pressures, from one side to the other; his fist opening to say *let's go*; his descending arc to say *we don't go back up*; his palm on Nandi's nape, long, warm, untranslatable — those gestures belonged only to him. The same movements, made by another, wouldn't say the same thing. Senzo's idiolect had died with Senzo.

And with his idiolect, something else. The expedition leader had fallen. The cartographer. The route-decider. The man who looked at a point and walked toward it. The man who chose when the others couldn't.

Who would choose now?

Nandi pulled her hands from the wall. Turned around. Looked at the five faces. Five. Not six. The number had changed and the world hadn't changed — the magnitude still vibrated, the blue filaments still pulsed, the air still blew from below upward.

She approached Inyoni. Placed her hand on her shoulder. One pressure. Long.

Inyoni looked up. Forty-seven fibers in her head. She placed her hand on Nandi's shoulder. Same gesture. Same pressure.

Enama stood up. Touched Thabo's shoulder. Thabo touched Jabu's shoulder. Jabu touched Sihle's shoulder. Sihle — Sihle hesitated. His hands, accustomed to instruments, unaccustomed to voluntary Contact, placed themselves on Nandi's shoulder.

The circle closed.

Six shoulders. Six hands. Not a word. The simplest Contact — the one that says nothing other than *I'm here, I'm touching you, we exist in the same world.*

The gesture Senzo had made to the old woman of the village. The first gesture. The only one remaining when all the others had lost their speaker.

They stayed like that. The ground vibrated. The filaments pulsed. Air rose from the depths.

Then Nandi did something no one expected. She raised her hand. Open palm. Descending arc.

*We don't go back up.*

Senzo's gesture. In Nandi's hands. Not the same — thinner fingers, slower movement, wider arc. But the word. Senzo's word, spoken by another mouth. A word that didn't belong to Nandi but that she took anyway. A borrowing. An inheritance. A necessary theft.

The Contact wasn't dead. The Contact had lost a speaker. But the language — the language goes on. Words pass from hand to hand. Gestures survive those who invent them, the way vibrations survive the quakes that launch them.

The team stood up. Not together — one by one. Inyoni first, retying the rope. Then Thabo, who unfurled his cloth. Then the others.

Six people on a basalt landing, before a fault that descended.

And behind them, beneath tons of rock, a body that no longer trembled. The only body in all this world that, at last, no longer trembled.

Nandi placed a bare foot on the edge of the left passage. The ground vibrated. Magnitude 4. The pulse returned. The earth was still speaking — it had always spoken.

The team descended.

Without map. Without leader. Without Senzo.

With the Contact.

And the Contact had one fewer word.

# V — The Deep Caverns

## Scene 1 — The air thins

The air changed before the rock.

Nandi felt it in her lungs before feeling it in her feet — a scarcity, a thinness, as if each breath gave a little less than the last. The body compensated. The heart beat faster. The temples pulsed. The world demanded more and gave less.

Thabo stopped.

His cloth hung between his fingers as it always hung, blackened, worn, carrying the breath of the deaf village for what could have been three days or three weeks — time had no grip here, in the caverns where nothing marked the hours but the rhythm of quakes and the wearing of bodies. Thabo raised his arm. The fibers moved. Less than before. Much less. On the surface, the cloth danced. In the rift, it inhaled. Here, in the deep caverns, it barely floated — a hesitation in the air, as if the wind itself no longer knew which direction to blow.

He turned toward the team. Five faces in the red glow of filaments. Not blue. Red. The bioluminescence had changed color since the entrance to the caverns — the filaments lining the walls were no longer the regular, bluish threads of the village, maintained, trimmed, domesticated. These grew as they could, short, thick, a brick red that pulsed at a rhythm Nandi didn't recognize. Not the background magnitude. Another rhythm. Deeper. Slower. Like the second heartbeat she'd been feeling beneath the first since departure — the magnitude 11, rising.

Thabo made the sign. Hand flat, palm down, lateral motion. *The air circulates, we can go.* But the movement was slower than usual. More hesitant. His fingers hung suspended for a fraction of a second, as if searching for a word the gesture didn't contain.

*We can go. But.*

Thabo's Contact had no "but." The air is good or the air is not good. The cloth dances or doesn't dance. There was no sign for "the air is sufficient but it won't be for much longer." So Thabo made the simple sign. And kept his brow furrowed.

Nandi understood. She always understood the gestures others didn't finish — her tremens gave her that, this reading ability that Sihle despised and Enama envied. She felt in the pressure of her temples, in the accelerated beating of her heart, in the slight nausea that hadn't left her since the rift, she felt what Thabo's cloth couldn't say: the oxygen was dropping. Not fast. Not in freefall. In a gentle slope. The gentle slope that doesn't trigger an alert but that, in the end, brings you to your knees.

The network of caverns opened before them like a digestive system — tunnels that branched, crossed, devoured each other. The ceiling oscillated between two meters and twenty. The floor was volcanic rock cooled in waves, in tongues, in ridges that looked like burned skin, frozen at the moment of burning. Nandi walked barefoot on it. Forty-five degrees. Hot. Not yet painful. Her desert calluses held — three weeks of burning sand had hardened the soles of her feet like supple leather. But she felt the promise beneath the heat. The lava was not far. The lava rose as the earth rose, as everything rose toward them while they descended toward it.

Sihle stopped in front of a partially collapsed passage. Blocks of basalt had detached from the ceiling — a recent collapse, dust still suspended in the thin air, edges sharp. He placed his hand on the rock. Not the way Enama placed her hand — not to listen. To read. His fingers followed the cracks, the stress lines, the light veins in the black basalt. The miner in him saw what the others didn't: the structure. The forces. The points where the rock still held and those where it had stopped holding.

He withdrew his hand. Turned toward Inyoni. Two fingers on her forearm — the technical Contact, cold, measured. Then he pointed to a block suspended above the passage and made a motion: flat hand descending. *That block is going to fall.*

Inyoni approached. Her hands found the holds around the block — not to touch it, to assess it. How much it weighed. How it held. What needed to be moved so the passage would open without the ceiling following. Her fingers counted the cracks. Four. Her hands searched for the leverage point. Found it. Pushed.

The block slid. Not all at once — pivoting, like a tooth being pulled. The rock groaned. Dust rose. The passage opened.

Inyoni stepped back. Counted the cracks in the ceiling around the hole. Five. One more than before. Her lips moved without sound — *holds* — and she passed under the cleared passage, shoulders tucked, hands on the walls, measuring the width in arm span. Enough. Just enough.

Sihle took out his seismograph while the others passed one by one. The paper unrolled its curves. The sawtooth patterns were tighter than on the surface — the micro-quakes here were constant, close together, a background vibration that turned the lines into an almost continuous band of small peaks. He noted in the margin of the roll. Numbers. Frequencies. Intervals between peaks.

Then he measured the atmospheric pressure. The seismograph's auxiliary instrument had a barometer — an addition Sihle had made himself, because Sihle always added instruments to instruments, as if the world could only be understood by measuring it from every angle at once. He noted the number. Compared it to the previous number — the one he'd taken at the cavern entrance. Frowned.

The meteorologist in him — that profession he had almost never shown the team, because meteorology underground seemed an absurd concept, and Sihle didn't like showing what couldn't be proven — the meteorologist calculated. The pressure gradient. The rate of thinning. The number of hours before the threshold.

He sought Thabo's gaze.

Thabo, on the other side of the cleared passage, held his cloth before a lateral crack from which a thread of warm air rose. The Airmaster and the Meteorologist. Two professions that had never crossed — Thabo read the air with his body, Sihle with numbers, and their languages were as different as Contact and voice.

Sihle placed two fingers on Thabo's forearm. Pressed. Showed the seismograph. Showed the number written in the margin. Two fingers that said: *the data confirms.*

Thabo looked at the numbers. He didn't understand the numbers — not those, not in that notation. But he understood the direction. Sihle's gesture pointed downward. The cloth pointed downward. Data and body, for once, for the only time, agreed.

The air was dying.

Thabo nodded. A gesture that was not Contact — a gesture from before Contact, a surface gesture, a nod that meant *I know* without touching anyone. He put the cloth in his pocket. Not fast, not abruptly. With the care of someone putting away something precious. The cloth carried the breath of the village. The air from below. The air he was going to have to learn to read in its final hours.

Enama, behind them, had her hands on the ground. Eyes closed. She wasn't searching for air — she was searching for the route. The earth spoke beneath her palms. Something in the vibrations had changed since the rift. Something deeper, slower, more insistent. As if the rock itself was pushing in a direction. As if the volcano was inhaling.

She opened her eyes. Met Nandi's gaze. Not Contact. Not touch. A look. That ancient, nearly useless thing that Contact had replaced.

Nandi saw what Enama's hands couldn't say.

The earth wanted them.

---

Nandi vomited.

Not the first time — she had lost count. The tremens no longer left her. Since the entrance to the caverns, her body vibrated at a frequency that corresponded to nothing it knew. The desert had its long, deep waves, the foothills their sharp snaps, the village its dampening resonances. But here, in the deep caverns, the frequencies were low. Very low. Lower than anything the human body should hear through the soles of its feet.

Her stomach turned with every shift. Her hands trembled — not the trembling of Contact, not the voluntary trembling the old woman of the village had transmitted to her. A suffered trembling. The body protesting against a world that didn't suit it.

Jabu approached. Silent. He crouched beside her. Set a gourd within reach of her hand. No Contact — he didn't touch her. He placed the object and stepped back. The water would be there when she needed it.

Nandi spat. Wiped her mouth. Took the gourd. The water was warm — everything was warm here, the air, the rock, the water in the gourds heating by conduction. She drank. Set the gourd down. Noticed it was fuller than the others — Jabu had filled his more than necessary. Ocean specialist reflex. Water, you never have too much. You never have enough.

She didn't thank him. He didn't expect it. Jabu never expected anything — he did, and the world turned around him without noticing.

---

## Scene 2 — The tremens at its peak

The quake arrived through Nandi's feet.

Not like the others — not like the background magnitudes, those constant vibrations the body ended up ignoring like the noise of an engine. This one arrived like a new word. A frequency her feet didn't know. Lower than low. So low that sound no longer existed — only movement remained, an oscillation in the ankle bones, in the tibias, that climbed through the knees and exploded in the pelvis like a shock wave in slow motion.

Nandi seized Enama's wrist.

The calibration grip. Their gesture. The gesture of their pair, belonging only to the two of them — Enama gripping Nandi's wrist when the tremens exceeded the bearable, Nandi gripping Enama's wrist when the signal became too strong for one body. The grip was not a message. It was a sharing. Nandi's fingers squeezed Enama's wrist and something passed — not words, not vocabulary, something from before vocabulary. The frequency. The raw signal. Nandi's body shared what it received, and Enama's body absorbed part of the load.

Enama felt it. Her eyes widened. Her feet — shod, hers, her feet didn't read like Nandi's — her feet felt it anyway. Because the grip had transmitted.

She turned toward Sihle. Full grip, five fingers, forearm. *Now.*

Sihle looked up from his seismograph. The curves showed nothing abnormal — the usual sawtooth patterns, the continuous band of micro-quakes. He looked at the paper. Looked at Enama. The conflict didn't need words. His face said: *The data does not confirm.*

Twenty seconds.

The seismograph jumped.

The continuous band of small peaks broke all at once — a giant sawtooth, massive, that crossed the entire roll and made the instrument vibrate in Sihle's harness. Magnitude 7.5. Or what the numbers called 7.5 — the numbers were late.

The ground moved.

Not trembling. *Movement*. The rock beneath their feet undulated like living skin, a telluric spasm that sent everyone to the ground. Inyoni grabbed the wall — her hands found the holds by reflex, as if the rock were a rope and the rope were her home. Thabo pressed flat on his stomach, the cloth beneath him, hands on the ground, measuring the vibrations through his entire body. Jabu didn't move — he was already crouching, back against the wall, hands flat on either side, body coiled. Enama let herself fall, palms to the ground, eyes closed — listening. Listening even in the quake. Especially in the quake. Enama's body didn't panic during earthquakes. It opened.

Sihle, standing, legs apart, hands on his seismograph, read the curves live. His eyes went from paper to rock, paper to rock. The numbers climbed. 7.5. 7.8. The sawtooth patterns overlapped. The instrument vibrated so hard the curves became unreadable — the seismograph's needle could no longer follow the ground's motion.

Eight seconds.

The quake passed.

The rock stabilized. Not calm — the background magnitude returned, 5.5, higher than before, more nervous. Dust fell from the ceiling. A red filament detached from the wall and fell to the ground, pulsing a few more seconds before going dark.

Nandi was panting. Enama's grip on her wrist hadn't let go. The two women, on the ground, connected by their hands, the signal still between them.

Sihle noted the delay.

Twenty seconds. He noted it in the margin of the roll, next to the magnitude. A number and a fact: Nandi's body had detected the quake twenty seconds before the most sensitive instrument of the expedition. Twenty seconds. Sihle noted the number. He did not note what it meant. What it meant would have required a word he didn't possess — the word for "the body was right and the instrument was wrong." That word didn't exist in Sihle's vocabulary. Which is why he only noted the number.

But Nandi saw.

She saw his two fingers inscribe the 20 in the margin of the roll. She saw his face — the tension, the clenching jaw, the eyes that came back to her then looked away. Sihle withdrew his hand from Nandi's forearm — his hand that had been there without him realizing, placed by reflex during the quake, two fingers on the forearm, involuntary technical Contact. He withdrew his hand faster than usual. As if the Contact burned.

The handicap was the gift. Sihle didn't want to admit it. And in twenty seconds or in twenty hours, that inability to admit would cost a life.

Not his.

## Scene 3 — The choice of route

The fork awaited them like a trap without jaws.

Two passages. On the left: a wide gallery whose floor descended in a gentle slope toward a sound of water. Not a trickle — a current. The sound rolled through the tunnel, amplified by the walls, transformed into a deep rumbling that mixed with the background vibrations. The air there was humid. Nandi felt the vapor on her cheeks, that moisture the desert didn't know.

On the right: a dry passage. Narrow. Volcanic rock floor, regular, stable. The air there was drier, hotter. The passage rose slightly before descending again — a rock ridge that would need to be climbed over before finding the slope again.

Two passages. Two directions. And no one left to choose.

Sihle was the first to move. He advanced toward the right passage. His seismograph was already measuring — one hand on the instrument, the other on the wall. The curves in the dry passage were more regular, the sawtooth patterns more spaced. Less seismic activity. More structural stability. The basalt here was compact, cracks rare, stress lines parallel — the rock held. The miner in Sihle saw it. The meteorologist in Sihle confirmed: the airflow in the dry passage was regular, constant, predictable. The data said right.

He turned toward the team. Two fingers on Thabo's forearm. *The data says.*

Thabo extended his cloth before the dry passage. The fibers moved — regularly, laterally. The air circulated. Thabo confirmed Sihle's sign with his own: hand flat, lateral motion. *The air circulates.*

Enama crouched.

Her hands settled on the ground, midway between the two passages. Palms open. Fingers spread. Eyes closed. The ritual. The same ritual as before every fork since chapter II — Enama who listens to the ground, Sihle who reads his curves, and the world between them that won't let itself be summarized.

The silence lasted. Six people motionless in the red glow of filaments. The rumbling of water on the left. The dry breath of air on the right. And in the middle, Enama, her hands placed on the decision.

She opened her eyes.

Grabbed Inyoni's wrist. Firm grip — not Sihle's technical grip, Enama's full grip, the one that seized the way you seize a certainty. She pointed to the left passage. The water.

*That way.*

Sihle didn't move. His two fingers stayed on Thabo's forearm. He looked at Enama. The seismograph in his harness vibrated with the background magnitude, 5.5, the little sawtooth patterns that said: the dry passage is safe. The data would not change because a woman had placed her hands on the ground and closed her eyes.

Nobody spoke. Nobody ever spoke — the voice was a waste, a transgression, a confession. But the silence here had a shape Nandi recognized. The same shape as in chapter II, before the Y-fork, when Sihle had pointed right and Enama left and Senzo had had to choose.

Senzo.

Nandi raised her hand. Palm open. Descending arc. *We don't go back up.* Senzo's gesture in Nandi's hands. But the gesture didn't say *left* or *right*. It didn't say *water* or *rock*. It said: *whatever the direction, we continue.* The chief's gesture had become a gesture of encouragement. Not of decision. The difference between the two, Nandi felt it in her own fingers — the thinner fingers, the slower movement, the wider arc. The same word, spoken by a mouth that didn't have the authority of the original mouth.

The gesture didn't settle it. The gesture could no longer settle it. The chief was dead and the chief's gesture, in someone else's hands, no longer carried the weight of decision.

Jabu moved.

He rose from where he'd been crouching — always set back, always at the edge, always in the margin of the group as he had been in the margin of the cart. He advanced toward the left passage. Crouched at the water's edge.

The underground river was black. Not black from dirt — black from depth. The water was clear but the bottom didn't exist, or so far below the surface that the red filament light couldn't reach it. The current was visible: regular eddies, a lateral push from right to left, a speed Nandi estimated at one or two meters per second. Strong. But not violent.

Jabu plunged his hand in.

His fingers entered the water as into a language. The current pressed against his palm — a constant, measurable pressure that his body knew. Jabu was an ocean specialist. Water was his domain. Not the dormant water of gourds — living water, water that moves, water that has a direction and a will. His fingers spread in the current read the force, the temperature, the turbulence.

He withdrew his hand. Shook it. Drops fell on the hot rock and evaporated with a hiss.

Then he made a gesture no one had ever seen him make.

Hand open. Palm up. Lateral motion. Not Thabo's sign for air — a sign that resembled Thabo's but came from water. An invented sign. A sign that Jabu's Contact had never needed to produce because Jabu almost never spoke.

*The current is manageable.*

Six faces looked at him. Five, actually — Nandi was looking with her feet as much as with her eyes, because the vibrations of the water in the rock confirmed what Jabu's hand had just said. The current was strong but regular. No treacherous turbulence. No falls downstream. No rising tide — or not yet.

Jabu stood back up. His hands hung at his sides. Empty. The gesture was made. The word had been said. Jabu returned to his margin.

But Enama had seen.

She approached him. Slowly. Not Enama's step when she ran toward a fork, not the step of intuition — the step of someone who recognizes something. She placed her hand on Jabu's shoulder.

A pressure. Long.

*I trust you.*

The richest Contact Jabu had received since the start of the expedition. Perhaps the richest he had ever received. Jabu's shoulder, usually empty, usually in the off-frame of Contact the way Jabu was in the off-frame of the group, received Enama's pressure and didn't know what to do with it. He didn't respond. He had no word for that. His idiolect contained only *no need* and *the water is there* and the silence between the two.

Sihle watched. The seismograph vibrated in his harness. The data said right. Enama's body said left. Jabu said left. Nandi said nothing but her bare feet, placed on the rock between the two passages, her feet said what they always said — the truth of the frequency, indecipherable to anyone who didn't know how to read.

The conflict.

Enama turned toward Sihle. Opened her mouth.

"The water."

One word. Her voice. For the second time in the novel — the second time this mouth produced an articulated sound instead of Contact, the second time the air between two people carried something other than the rumbling of the world and the sound of footsteps. The first time was before the Y-fork, in chapter II. Senzo was there. Senzo had chosen.

Senzo was no longer there.

Sihle didn't respond with his voice. He responded with Contact — and the Contact he chose said everything: two fingers on Enama's forearm, brief pressure, withdrawal. The coldest Contact in his repertoire. Data against body. Instrument against hand. Two fingers withdrawing like a verdict.

*No.*

Enama didn't flinch. Her hand stayed on Jabu's shoulder. Her gaze on Sihle. Water rumbled in the left passage. Air blew in the right passage. And between the two, six people who didn't know how to decide because the decision had died in chapter IV, under tons of rock, with a man who was looking at a point and walking toward it.

## Scene 4 — The separation

The rope was the last word.

Inyoni cut it. Not with a sudden gesture — with a surgeon's gesture. She measured. One hundred forty meters since the rift, minus the sections cut after Senzo's death, minus the anchor knots. She counted. Her lips moved. The numbers scrolled like a prayer in reverse — not *holds, holds, holds* but *eighty-two, eighty-three, eighty-four*. She reached the end of her count. Placed the knife on the fiber. Looked at Sihle.

Sihle nodded. The gesture that was not Contact.

The fiber gave. Two sections. Inyoni handed the shorter one to Sihle — fifty-eight meters. Kept the longer — eighty-four. The imbalance was no accident. Four people needed more rope than two.

The group split. Not in violence. Not in shouts. In silence — that specific silence of Contact that can't find its words, that absence of touch which is the worst of touches because it says: I don't know how to touch you for what's coming.

Sihle took the right passage. He stowed his seismograph, adjusted his harness, knotted the rope section around his waist. His movements were methodical. Precise. The movements of someone who knows exactly what he's doing and why. The data said right. Sihle went right. The world was simple when you measured it.

Thabo followed him.

Not out of loyalty — out of necessity. The dry passage needed an Airmaster. The air there was hotter, drier, potentially toxic. Thabo went where the air needed him. He wasn't choosing the route — he was choosing the danger he knew how to read.

Before leaving, Thabo turned toward Inyoni. Their shoulders touched. The Contact of their pair — the passive Contact, shoulder against shoulder, the one that says nothing other than *I am here, you are here, we are side by side*. Not a farewell. Contact didn't do farewells. Contact said what was, not what was going to be.

Inyoni didn't hold him back. Her hands were busy — they were counting the remaining meters of rope, the knots to redo, the Charge straps to check. Thirty-two straps on the bags, twenty-four on the Charge. Her hands counted while her shoulder registered the pressure of Thabo's shoulder. The "test-and-holds" — Thabo tests the air, Inyoni tightens the strap. The dialogue of their pair. The last dialogue.

Inyoni's fingers tightened on the rope. *Holds.*

Enama, Nandi, Inyoni, Jabu took the left passage. The water.

Four against two. The body group against the data group. Nandi in the middle, bare feet in the water vapor, the tremens pounding in her temples. Inyoni behind, the Charge on her back, twenty-four straps, thirty-five kilos, the weight of the mission. Jabu in front, his hands hanging, his eyes reading the water the way Thabo read the air — without instrument, without measure, with what the body had always known. And Enama, beside Nandi, her hand still warm from Jabu's shoulder.

Nandi looked at the right passage. Sihle and Thabo were sinking into it. Thabo's cloth — a square of blackened fibers, the breath of the village, the air the dead had breathed — disappeared into the tunnel's darkness. Sihle's seismograph jolted in its harness, its curves recording the last vibrations before distance erased them.

Then the tunnel turned and swallowed them.

Six people became four and two. Contact split. Words divided like bodies.

Before the separation, a moment. A moment nobody noticed except Nandi, because Nandi always watched the hands.

Jabu had crossed Sihle's path at the moment of division. Their trajectories had grazed — Jabu going toward the water, Sihle toward the rock. And Jabu had made his gesture. The small gesture. The almost involuntary shoulder pressure, barely Contact, the most minimal movement in his repertoire — the gesture that meant *no need*.

Sihle hadn't responded. Sihle hadn't felt it. His hands were on the seismograph, his eyes on the curves, his body already in the right passage.

The last Contact between Jabu and Sihle was a non-Contact. A pressure addressed to someone who didn't receive it. The most discreet word of Contact sent into the void.

Nandi saw it. Kept it. Didn't yet know it was a farewell.

## Scene 5 — Death of Jabu

The water passage descended.

Not in a gentle slope — in steps. Ledges of volcanic rock, irregular, slippery, separated by drops of a meter or two that the water crossed in cascades. The underground river had nothing of a great river — it was an adolescent torrent, nervous, changeable, filling all available space and leaving nothing dry.

The water reached their ankles.

Jabu led.

For the first time in the expedition, Jabu led. His body had found its posture — shoulders straight, chin raised, hands no longer hanging but open, fingers in the current. He read the water the way Nandi read the ground. Flow rate by pressure on the palms. Temperature by the burn on the skin — the water here was warm, heated by the rock, thirty-five degrees perhaps. Eddies by vibrations in the wrists. The invisible bottom by the movement of the feet.

He stopped at each ledge. Plunged his hand. Measured. Then the gesture — the gesture he had invented, his only gesture, his only sentence in Contact: hand open, palm up, lateral motion. *The current is manageable.*

The group advanced in the black water.

Nandi walked barefoot in the current. Her toes found holds beneath the water — the volcanic rock had asperities, bumps, hollows that gave purchase if you knew how to search. The water was warm on her burning feet and for the first time in hours, the heat was not pain — it was relief. Warm water on desert calluses. A bath. Almost.

But beneath the water, beneath the warmth and the relief, her feet felt something else. The background tremor. The magnitude 5.5 passing through rock, through water, through her bones. And in the background tremor, something new. An arrhythmia. Like a heartbeat skipping one beat out of three. The frequency was changing — not rising, not falling. In *irregularity*.

Nandi said nothing. Not yet. The arrhythmia could be normal — the caverns were a complex system, frequencies bounced, overlapped, canceled out. Maybe the water was blurring the signal. Maybe her feet were lying.

Nandi's feet never lied.

The water rose to the knees.

Jabu stopped. Measured. The current had accelerated — one or two meters per second more. Not alarming. But the direction had changed. The current no longer pushed right to left — it pushed top to bottom. The water was descending faster than before.

Jabu made the gesture. Hand open, palm up, lateral motion. *The current is manageable.* But this time, the lateral movement had a jolt at the end — a hitch, a tremor. Not a tremor of the body. A tremor of the word. As if the gesture itself was no longer sure of what it said.

Enama placed her hand on Jabu's shoulder. The same pressure as at the fork. Long. *I trust you.*

Jabu advanced.

The water rose to the thighs.

Nandi felt the arrhythmia become a pattern. The skipped beat was no longer one in three — it was one in two. The frequency beneath her feet pounded: boom, silence, boom, silence, boom. The rock was accumulating something. Energy. Tension. Like an elastic being stretched — the rock was tensing and the water was rising as a consequence, because water goes where the rock tells it to go.

She seized Enama's wrist. The calibration grip. Hard. Urgent. Not the usual sharing — a warning.

Enama felt it. Her eyes sought Nandi. Nandi's hands were saying something Contact had no words to express. Not *danger* — the grip on the forearm says *danger*. Not *stop* — three pressures say *stop*. Something between the two. Something that resembled *it's going to change*.

The water rose to the waist.

Jabu stopped. The current was pushing him now — not hard, but constantly, a lateral pressure that demanded effort to stay standing. His hands in the water could no longer find the bottom — the river had deepened, the floor had given way to a liquid abyss, and Jabu's feet touched only the current.

He turned toward the group. His face, in the red light of the filaments, had an expression no one had ever seen on him. Not fear — Jabu didn't show fear. Concentration. The expression of the ocean specialist reading a current he doesn't understand. The water had changed. The water wasn't doing what it was supposed to do. The water was doing something else.

The quake was short.

Five seconds. Magnitude 6, maybe 6.5. Not a quake — a jolt, a spasm of rock, the kind of thing that didn't even deserve a name on the surface. But here, in a water passage, in a tunnel where the river filled the space between the walls, the spasm changed everything.

The rock contracted. The tunnel narrowed by a centimeter — invisible, imperceptible, but sufficient. The water no longer had room. The descending water found itself compressed in a reduced space and did what water always does when compressed: it accelerated.

The current doubled in an instant.

Jabu was hit first. Standing at the front, feet without purchase, body exposed, he took the mass of water like a horizontal punch. His hands reached for the wall — found it, gripped it, held. The water specialist knew water. He knew how to swim. He knew how to fight. His fingers in the rock, his feet searching for support, his body leaning into the current like a tree against the wind.

He held.

The second wave arrived twenty seconds later — the quake had opened a fissure upstream, somewhere in the depths of the rock where water had been accumulating for centuries, and this reserve was now pouring into the tunnel with the force of everything held back too long. The current didn't double. It quadrupled.

Jabu held the wall. His hands held. His feet had found an asperity — not a hold, a suggestion of a hold, a centimeter of rock protruding from the wall that supported the weight of a man and of the river pushing him.

Behind him, Enama had grabbed Nandi. Inyoni had grabbed the wall with one hand and the Charge with the other — the twenty-four straps taut, the case pulling, water infiltrating the joints. *Holds.* Her lips moved. *Holds.*

Jabu looked at the team over his shoulder. The current passed around his body like around a boulder — eddies, whirlpools, the black water rising and rising. He saw Enama, clinging to Nandi. He saw Inyoni, clinging to the Charge. He saw the passage ahead — the tunnel descending, the water plunging into it, the darkness swallowing everything.

And he knew water.

He knew this current. The flow rate, the pressure, the trajectory. His hands in the water had read the direction before the wave arrived — a lateral current, a secondary passage, a path the water took to the side of the main tunnel. A navigable path. A path the water specialist could take to reach the next ledge and extend the rope to the others.

Jabu let go of the wall.

He didn't let go by accident. He let go because he knew how to swim and he knew how to read the current and he knew — he *knew* — that the lateral passage was there, three meters, four maybe, and his body could reach it.

The water took him.

Not the way you take an object — the way you take a person. The water lifted him from the wall, spun him, carried him into the current with the same indifference as the ground carried everything in this world. The geological irony: the water specialist, carried away by water. The man who knew the current, swallowed by the current.

His hands swam. Nandi saw them — in the dying light of the red filaments, Jabu's hands cut through the water with the competence of someone for whom water was a language. No panic. No flailing. Precise, calculated movements, each stroke aimed at the lateral passage.

He reached it. One arm hooked the edge of the lateral fissure. His fingers found the hold. His body hoisted itself — halfway, waist out of the water, legs still in the current.

The third wave.

Bigger than the first two. The quake hadn't only opened a fissure upstream — it had destabilized an entire wall, and tons of water accumulated in a geological pocket were now pouring into the system. The rumbling filled the tunnel. The black water rose all at once — from belly to chest, from chest to neck.

Nandi felt in her feet the vibration change when Jabu's body gave way.

Not a sound. Not a cry — Jabu didn't cry out, Jabu didn't complain, Jabu never made a sound. His hands released the edge. The water carried him into the darkness. The red light of the filaments illuminated for an instant his fingers — spread, open, the same fingers that had measured the current ten minutes earlier — then the current turned and the filaments showed nothing but water.

Nandi closed her eyes.

Beneath her feet, the frequency had changed. A vibration was missing. A body struggling in water had a signal — a movement, a resistance, a weight fighting against the current that the rock transmitted. That signal had just disappeared. Jabu's body was no longer fighting. The water no longer met an obstacle.

The silence of water after the tumult.

Nandi reopened her eyes. The water was receding. Slowly, like a sigh. The current was slowing. The third wave had passed and the system was rebalancing — the upstream pocket was empty now, the water finding new paths, the pressure dropping.

Four people. Three. The number had changed and the water hadn't noticed.

Inyoni counted the straps of the Charge. Twenty-four. *Holds.* Her eyes were dry.

Enama released Nandi's wrist. The calibration grip undone. Her hands were trembling — not from tremens, from the flood. The water had soaked everyone, drenched the clothes, weighed down the bags. Enama looked at her wet hands. The hands that had touched Jabu's shoulder. *I trust you.*

Trust hadn't been enough.

Jabu had no ritual. No shoulder circle. No new word invented to name the absence. Jabu had always been in the margin of Contact — the silent character, the lone cart, the *no need*. His death was in his image: an erasure. The water had come and carried away the silence. Nobody had seen him go. Nobody had held his hand. Jabu's final Contact didn't exist — and that was perhaps the Contact most faithful to what he had always been.

Nandi set her bare feet on the rock above the water. Rock at fifty degrees. Hot. Painful. Her calluses cracked — the first blister. Under the left heel, the skin lifted, a dome of flesh filled with fluid that pulsed to the rhythm of the background magnitude.

She didn't stop. You didn't stop. You walked and the world trembled and Contact had one word fewer.

Four people toward the rally point. Where Sihle and Thabo were waiting.

Or not.

## Scene 6 — The off-screen death

Nandi felt Sihle die.

Not die exactly. Disappear. The background vibration lost a note — not a magnitude, not a frequency, something more subtle. Like an orchestra whose one instrument falls silent. Nandi's body, tuned to the rock's vibrations since the desert surface, her body that read the earth through the soles of her feet and the palms of her hands, her body felt the absence before understanding it.

She stopped. Placed her hands on the ground. Eyes closed.

The ground vibrated. The background magnitude, 5.5, the micro-quakes that never ceased. But in this fabric of vibrations, a thread was missing. A body that had been walking in a neighboring tunnel, a body weighing seventy kilos that transmitted its steps to the rock, that body had stopped walking. Stopped weighing. Stopped being.

Nandi opened her eyes. Looked at Enama. Looked at Inyoni. Said nothing. Touched no one. She didn't yet know what she had felt. She didn't know which of the two.

---

The rally point was a wide cavern, a space where the two passages rejoined — the water tunnel and the dry tunnel converging toward the same rocky basin before plunging back into the darkness. The bioluminescence was stronger there — the red filaments lined the ceiling in thick clusters, pulsing like organs.

Thabo arrived first.

Alone.

Nandi saw him before understanding. Thabo, standing at the entrance to the dry passage, the cloth hanging between his fingers, his hands black with soot or gas — something had burned, something had exploded in the dry tunnel, a gas pocket perhaps, an emanation that even the Airmaster hadn't been able to avoid. His cloth was intact — stowed under his shirt at the moment of the explosion. His hands, no.

But it wasn't his hands Nandi was looking at. It was the emptiness behind him.

Thabo was alone.

He advanced into the cavern. His steps were slow — not methodical as usual, not Thabo's step that measures and notes. Slow from fatigue. Slow from what comes after.

He sought Enama's gaze. Found her. Approached.

Enama touched him. Two hands on the shoulders. The Contact of the one who asks. The pressure that questions. *Where is—*

Thabo didn't respond with a codified gesture.

He extended his right hand. Palm open. Facing down. Then he closed it. Slowly. Fingers folding one by one, like a fist clenching nothing, like a hand reaching and not finding.

*Fallen.*

A silence.

The word didn't exist in the team's Contact. Contact had a word for *danger*, for *stop*, for *advance*, for *I am here*. Contact had a gesture for mourning — the shoulder circle, invented after Senzo's death. But Contact had no word for *he fell into the void*. Thabo had just invented it. A hand that descends and closes on nothing.

Enama released Thabo's shoulders.

Her hands made a gesture Nandi didn't recognize.

The right hand closed — hard, fingers clenched, knuckles white. Then it opened. Then it closed. Then it opened. Like a heart. Like a lung. Like a gesture learned in a dream of falling.

Hand that seizes. Hand that releases. Hand that seizes. Hand that releases.

The gesture of guilt. The gesture of a hand repeating what it failed to do — seize and hold. Enama hadn't been there when Sihle fell. Enama was in the water passage, four hundred meters away, in another tunnel, another world. But her hands made the gesture anyway. Her hands seized Sihle's hand five minutes too late, in a different cavern, before people who hadn't seen the fall.

Nandi took Enama's wrist. Squeezed. No message — just the pressure. The calibration grip diverted from its function. No longer a frequency sharing — an anchor. A weight in Enama's hand so the hand would have something to seize that wouldn't let go.

Enama raised her eyes. Her fingers continued their movement. Seize. Release. The rhythm of the gesture was the rhythm of a heart — regular, inevitable, as if Enama's hands couldn't stop.

Nandi understood.

Not because Enama had told her. Not because Thabo had mimed the fall. Not because the ground had vibrated differently when Sihle's body struck the bottom of the chasm. Nandi understood because Contact had no word for this gesture — and when Contact had no word, it meant the word had never needed to exist before.

Nobody had ever needed to say *I held a hand and I let it go*. Contact didn't let go. Contact held. Contact was made to hold — the grips, the pressures, the straps, the ropes, the entire vocabulary of Contact was a vocabulary of holding. You seize. You press. You don't let go.

Enama's hands had invented a new word.

And the word said: *too late*.

---

What Thabo recounted — through Contact, in fragments, through gestures his hands found while searching — assembled the puzzle.

The dry passage had held. Sihle's data had been right — the rock was stable, the frequencies low, the stresses distributed. The miner in Sihle had read the structure correctly. The dry passage would not have collapsed.

Except that the dry passage ran above a chasm.

An invisible chasm — covered by a layer of cooled basalt that formed a natural bridge, thick, solid in appearance. Sihle's seismograph hadn't detected it because the seismograph measured vibrations, not voids. The rock vibrated the same way above the chasm and above solid rock. The data said *stable*. The data was right — the basalt crust was stable.

Until the quake.

The same quake that had sent the flood into the water passage — magnitude 6, five seconds — had cracked the basalt bridge. Not everywhere. At one point. A single point of weakness the miner in Sihle hadn't seen because the point wasn't visible — it was underneath, in the chasm, where the rock had been melting for centuries and where the crust was nothing more than a shell.

Sihle was walking on the shell when it gave way.

Thabo was ahead. Thabo had felt it — not through the cloth, not through the air, through his feet. The ground had vibrated differently under Sihle than it vibrated under Thabo. A second of difference. Thabo had turned. Had seen the ground beneath Sihle crack in a star pattern. Had seen Sihle look at his feet with the expression of someone checking his data and finding that the data was lying.

Had seen Sihle fall.

Thabo didn't recount what followed. His hands made a gesture then stopped. The gesture said: *I reached out my hand.* The stop said: *I didn't reach.*

Sihle had fallen into the chasm. The seismograph with him — the instrument that had measured the world since departure, that had drawn its curves on the roll, that had noted the magnitudes and the frequencies and the numbers Sihle lined up in the margins. The instrument and the man, together, in the void. Data and body, falling at the same speed.

In the margin of the last roll, the number 20. Twenty seconds' lead of body over instrument. The last number Sihle had noted. The number he hadn't wanted to understand.

## Scene 7 — Aftermath

Four people.

The number pounded in Nandi's head like a magnitude. Four. Seven at the start. Six after Senzo. Four now. The world was losing numbers. Contact was losing words. Nandi's hands were full of vocabulary and half that vocabulary no longer had a recipient.

Enama no longer touched anything.

It was the most visible sign. Enama, whose hands had been everywhere since departure — on the ground, on the walls, on the rock, on the shoulders, on the wrists, on the gourds, on the roots —, Enama no longer touched. Her hands hung at her sides the way Jabu's hands had hung. An involuntary inheritance. The hands of the biologist, the cook, the survivalist, the hands that knew how to cut roots and prepare the porridge and feel the earth speak beneath the palms — those hands were closed. Clenched. The seize-release gesture had stopped but the fingers stayed curled, as if protecting something inside. Or as if they no longer wanted to touch for fear that everything they touched would fall.

Thabo and Inyoni walked shoulder against shoulder. The "test-and-holds" reinforced — without the cloth this time, without the strap, without the usual dialogue. Just shoulder pressure. Shoulder against shoulder. The passive Contact of their pair, the one that says nothing other than *I am here* and that sufficed when the rest no longer sufficed.

Contact among the survivors had changed. Softer. More hesitant. Not by choice — by fear. Every gesture carried the risk of touching the void. Hands seeking a shoulder risked finding no one. Contact, in a shrinking group, becomes an exercise in memory — the hand reaches toward where Sihle used to stand, toward where Jabu used to crouch, and the hand finds only air.

Nandi sat down. The ground at fifty-five degrees burned through her soaked clothes — the salt of the underground water crackled on the fabric, steam rose from the wet pants as from a cloth placed on an iron. Her feet. The blister under the left heel had burst in the flood. The skin around it was red, blistered. The sole of the right foot still held but the heat pounded it with every contact with the rock. Fifty-five degrees. The pain threshold.

She closed her eyes. Beneath her feet, the background tremor. Magnitude 5.5. Higher than at the entrance to the caverns — the magnitude climbed with depth, like the temperature, like the danger, like everything rising toward them while they descended.

And in the background tremor, the absences. The missing notes. Three bodies no longer walking, no longer weighing, no longer vibrating in the rock. Senzo beneath the rubble of the rift. Jabu in the underground current. Sihle at the bottom of a chasm, seismograph against his chest, curves still wet.

The group's Contact was amputated by half. Of seven idiolects, three were dead. Senzo's three slow pressures. Sihle's two technical fingers. Jabu's useful silence. These gestures would never be made again — not the way Senzo made them, not the way Sihle made them, not the way Jabu didn't make them. The same movements, in other hands, wouldn't say the same thing. Words pass from hand to hand — but they change with each passage, like frequencies change with each layer of rock.

---

Inyoni approached Nandi.

Not a word. Not a shoulder gesture. Inyoni sat beside her and took out the Charge from her bag.

The shock-absorbing case was wet — the floodwater had infiltrated the straps, soaked the joints. Inyoni examined it. Her hands on the casing, her fingers on the straps. Twenty-four. She counted them. Counted the wet straps. Twenty-four. The dry straps. Zero. The case had taken water but the mechanism inside — Sihle's triple suspension — seemed intact. The rubber shock absorbers responded when Inyoni pressed. The ceramic structure showed no cracks.

She turned the Charge over. The calibration dial — a metal disc engraved on the upper face — was intact. The water hadn't reached it. Inyoni turned it slightly. The mechanism clicked — a minuscule sound, a click of metal against metal, that Nandi felt in her hands resting on the rock more than she heard it in the air.

Inyoni placed the Charge between them. Looked at Nandi.

What followed had no words.

Inyoni took Nandi's hands. Placed them on the Charge. The metal was cold — strangely cold in the heat of the caverns, as if the Charge absorbed temperature instead of emitting it. Nandi's fingers touched the surface. The cylinder was the size of her forearm. Smooth. Dense. Twenty-two centimeters in diameter — her hands couldn't reach around it.

Inyoni guided Nandi's hands to the locks. Three. One by one.

The first lock was on the left side — a rotary cylinder embedded in the Charge's body. Inyoni placed Nandi's fingers on it. Turned — clockwise. The lock resisted, then gave, a quarter turn. Click.

*One.*

The second lock, right side. Same mechanism. Clockwise. A quarter turn. Click.

*Two.*

The third lock, underneath. Smaller. More delicate. Counter-clockwise — the trap, the one that would fool someone who didn't know. Inyoni turned Nandi's fingers the other way. Counter-clockwise. A quarter turn. Click.

*Three.*

Nandi counted. Inyoni's hands on hers. The metal under her fingers. The clicks in the rock.

*Holds. Holds. Holds.*

Not Nandi's words. Inyoni's words, passed into Nandi's hands the way Senzo's gesture had passed into Nandi's hands in chapter IV. An inheritance. A loan. Contact transmitting not a message but a knowledge — hands teaching hands.

Inyoni repeated the operation. Faster this time. Her fingers guided Nandi's: clockwise, clockwise, counter-clockwise. Three clicks. Nandi's hands memorized. The position of the locks. The resistance of the metal. The direction of rotation. The number.

Then Inyoni showed the dial.

The calibration disc, engraved with fine lines, with numbers Nandi couldn't read — frequencies, hertz, measurements Sihle would have understood and that the seismograph at the bottom of the chasm could no longer confirm. Inyoni turned the dial slowly. Nandi felt the vibration change under her fingers — not a mechanical vibration, something deeper. The dial modified the Charge's internal frequency. At each notch, at each graduation, the metal emitted a sound the ears couldn't hear but the fingers could feel.

Inyoni stopped at a graduation. Showed it. Pressure on Nandi's finger — *here*. The base frequency. The frequency Inyoni had preset for the backup. The starting point.

Then Inyoni did something the activation plan hadn't foreseen. She took Nandi's hands and placed them flat on the Charge, palms against the metal. And she waited.

Nandi felt.

The metal vibrated. Not from the ground's vibration — its own vibration. The Charge had an internal frequency, a hum in the metal, like a sleeping heart. Nandi's palms on the metal. The palms that had been reading the ground since the desert surface. The same palms.

The dial had to be turned until the Charge's frequency matched the ground's frequency. That's what Sihle's seismograph would have measured. That's what instruments would have done. But the instruments were at the bottom of the chasm.

Nandi's hands didn't need an instrument.

Inyoni knew it. Inyoni, the compulsive counter, the woman of straps and numbers, the climber who never let go — Inyoni had understood what Sihle had never wanted to admit. Nandi's hands felt frequencies. Nandi's tremens was a seismograph. If anyone could calibrate the Charge without an instrument, it was the woman who read the world through the soles of her feet.

Nandi withdrew her hands from the Charge. Looked at Inyoni. A look — not Contact, not touch. A look that said: *I understand what you're asking of me.*

Inyoni reset the locks to their initial position. Stowed the Charge in the bag. Checked the straps. Twenty-four. *Holds.*

The transfer was done. The hands knew. If Inyoni fell — when Inyoni fell — Nandi's hands would know what to do.

The team resumed the march.

Four people. The cavern was heating up. The red filaments were turning orange in places — not the blue light of the village, not the red light of the intermediate caverns. Orange. The color of lava seen from afar. The heat was rising.

Nandi placed a bare foot on the ground. Fifty-eight degrees. The blister under the left heel sent a jolt of pain into the calf. The sole of the right foot — still intact, but reddened, the skin tight and shiny like wet leather.

Thabo approached her. Not to touch her — to show her. His finger pointing at the rock. Two types of rock on the ground: one light, almost grey, the other dark, almost black. The light rock was cooler — cooled by the little air still circulating. The dark rock was hotter — heated by the magma below, by the lava rising, by the earth cooking.

Thabo placed his hand on the light rock. Then on the dark rock. The difference — ten degrees perhaps, fifteen. The light rock was bearable. The dark rock was not.

He looked at Nandi's feet. Then the rocks. Then Nandi's feet.

The Contact of pedagogy — without words, without codified gesture. The oldest gesture in the world: showing. *Look. The light rock is cool. The dark rock is hot. Walk on the light.*

Nandi looked at the ground. The checkerboard of light and dark rocks spread before her like a map. The light rocks formed a path — not continuous, not simple, but a path. Hold to hold. Light rock to light rock.

Her feet understood. The pain would be there. The pain wouldn't leave. But the pain could be sorted — separated from the signal, separated from the tremens, isolated in a corner of the body while the rest continued to read. Thabo had just given her a tool. The geologist's tool — color as indicator. Eyes in service of feet.

Nandi placed her foot on the first light rock. Forty-five degrees. Bearable. She advanced to the next. Forty-three. Then the next.

The path of light rocks.

Enama walked ahead. Hands closed. Contact retracted. She no longer touched the walls. No longer touched the ground. Enama's world-Contact — that capacity for dialogue with the earth, that open hand on living rock that had dazzled the reader in chapter II — Enama's world-Contact was shut. Not because the earth had gone silent. Because Enama no longer wanted to listen.

The earth had told her *water*. The water had killed Jabu.

The earth had told her *not rock*. The rock had killed Sihle.

Intuition had been right and reason had died anyway. Neither one had saved. Only Contact between the two could have — but Contact between Sihle and Enama had never existed. They had had a conflict, not a dialogue. Two fingers against the whole palm. Data against body. And the body of the conflict lay at the bottom of a chasm with its numbers.

Enama walked and her hands were closed and Nandi, behind her, saw the clenched fingers, the white knuckles, the seize-release gesture that kept repeating endlessly inside the fists.

The air was warming. The ground vibrated. Magnitude 6. Higher than before. The earth rumbled louder, as if impatient, as if eager for the bodies to descend faster toward it.

Inyoni tightened the straps of the Charge. Twenty-four. *Holds.*

Thabo tested the air with his cloth. The fibers barely moved. The air was still dying — more slowly since the water tunnel had created a lateral current, but dying all the same. Thabo put the cloth away. Frowned.

Nandi walked barefoot on the light rocks. The checkerboard. The path Thabo had shown her. The pain under the left heel. The signal under the right heel. The tremens in the bones. The magnitude in the feet. The absence in the hands.

Four people. Toward the bottom. Where Contact would continue to die.

And Contact had three fewer words.

# VI — The Volcanic Zones

## Scene 1 — Heat as a weapon

The heat did not arrive. It was already there.

Nandi understood the difference on the third step into the new tunnel. The deep caverns had been hot — fifty, sixty degrees, the rock cooking underfoot, sweat drying before it could run. But the heat of the caverns was ambient heat. A bath. A lukewarm oven. You could stand it.

The volcanic zones were something else.

The heat here had a direction. It came from below and it rose — not the way steam rises, in swirls, in approximations. It rose like a wall. A front. A continuous thermal wave pressing against the skin and never retreating. The air was no longer air — it was a fluid, thick, charged with sulfur and mineral vapor, and each breath burned the lungs like a first coffee swallowed too fast.

Nandi set her foot on the ground. Sixty-five degrees. The checkerboard of light rocks that Thabo had taught her still existed — but the light rocks were less light. Gray pulling toward brown. Less cooled. The air that had been ventilating them was dying, and without air, the light rocks were losing their thermal lead. Ten degrees of difference in the caverns. Five here. In three hours, maybe two. Every rock would be the same temperature.

The ground vibrated. Magnitude 6.5. Higher than in the deep caverns, more constant, more insistent. The micro-quakes no longer stopped — they overlapped, superimposed, a permanent trembling that made the orange filaments dance on the walls. Orange. No longer red — orange. The color had crossed a threshold that the bioluminescence registered better than instruments.

Thabo walked at the front.

His cloth never left his hand. At every fork, at every widening of the tunnel, he extended his arm and the fibers responded — but the fibers responded less and less. The air still circulated, but weakly, like a heart at the end of its run. The lateral currents of the caverns, fed by water networks and natural ventilation shafts, had dried up. The water had evaporated. The shafts had been blocked by quakes. All that remained was an ascending breath — hot air rising from the magma, loaded with gas, and the little breathable air that descended by gravity, heavier, cooler, hugging the ground.

Thabo crouched. Held the cloth flush with the ground. The fibers moved — a quiver, faint but real. The breathable air was down below. Where Nandi's feet walked. Where the heat was at its maximum.

Not metaphorically. The heat made touch painful. Seventy degrees at ground level, fifty in the air, the skin damp with sweat and vapor, and each press of a hand on skin lasted a few seconds before the pain rose — the heat trapped between the two skins, amplified by contact, transformed into a burn. Contact, in the volcanic zones, cost. Every tactile word had a price in pain.

## Scene 2 — Enama's guilt

The camp — if you could call camp a one-hour stop in a hollow of rock — was silent.

Not the silence of the caverns, which was full of noise — the background rumble, the crackling of filaments, the seeping of water. A Contact silence. A silence of hands that do not move. Of gestures that are not made. The worst of silences in a world where the silence of the voice is normal but the silence of touch is an amputation.

Enama sat apart.

Not far — the cavern did not allow far. But apart. Her back against the wall, knees drawn up, arms around her knees. Hands hidden. Her hands that had been the most active in the group since departure — the hands that cut roots, that prepared the porridge, that touched the ground and the walls and the shoulders — her hands were buried between her knees and her chest. Outside Contact. Outside the world.

Nandi observed her.

Not out of curiosity. Through the tremens. Nandi's body registered the vibrations of every body around her — the steps, the weights, the heart rates transmitted through the rock. Enama's body had been vibrating differently since the dry passage. Faster. More irregular. ## Scene 3 — The critical Airmaster

The gas was waiting for them around the bend.

The cloth. The cloth was not moving. Not a breath, not a quiver — the fibers hung, dead, inert. The air had stopped. No — the air no longer existed. The air had been replaced.

## Scene 4 — Nandi's premonition

The quake lasted seventeen seconds.

Nandi felt it forty seconds before it struck. Forty seconds. Twice the quake in the caverns. Triple what surface seismographs could have predicted.

Her body twisted.

Not a trembling — a convulsion. The tremens at its absolute maximum. Every muscle in her body vibrated at a frequency her bones could not contain. Her jaws clenched so hard that a chip of molar broke free and she tasted blood and calcium. Her hands clenched — fingers curled like claws, tendons protruding, nails in the palms. Her feet — her feet that were her instrument, her feet that read the world — her feet convulsed and the pain of the burn fused with the pain of the tremens and there was no longer any difference between reading and suffering.

Enama seized her.

The calibration grip. For the first time since Sihle's death — the grip Nandi had offered and Enama had refused. Nandi's wrist in Enama's hand. The pressure that shared the frequency. That absorbed a part of the signal.

Nandi felt the sharing. The frequency passing from her body into Enama's through the joined hands. The trembling distributing itself between two bodies instead of one. Not half — bodies do not divide like numbers. But enough. Enough for Nandi to open her eyes. To see.

The cavern was shaking.

The orange filaments were detaching from the ceiling in clusters. The rock was seeping — not water, lava. Drops. Beads of molten rock appearing in the ceiling's fissures and falling to the ground with a sharp hiss, like raindrops in the desert inverted. The first visible lava. The first proof that the magma was no longer far below — it was there, in the walls, in the fissures, in the flesh of the mountain.

The quake struck.

Magnitude 7.5. The ground heaved like a back arching. Inyoni pinned the Charge against the wall — her arms around the cylinder, her legs around the case, her body forming a human cradle for the device. *Holds.* The straps groaned. The crystallized salt exploded from three of them — three strands giving way at once. Twenty-one straps. Inyoni counted in the quake. Twenty-one.

Thabo, flat on his stomach, hands on the ground, the cloth crushed under his chest. He was breathing the rock. His body registered the vibrations — not with Nandi's precision, not with the tremens. With the geologist's knowledge. The frequencies in the ground told him what the rock was doing. And the rock was doing something new.

The rock was melting.

Not here — not yet. But below. The basalt layer separating them from the magma chamber was thinning. Each quake weakened it. Each additional degree of temperature softened it. The frequencies Thabo felt in the ground were no longer the frequencies of hard rock — they were the frequencies of plastic rock. Sound deformed as it passed through a layer that was no longer quite solid.

The quake stopped. Seventeen seconds. The rock stabilized. Background magnitude: 7. No longer 6.5 — 7. The floor had risen by half a point.

Nandi was panting. Enama's grip on her wrist still held. The heat between the two skins — unbearable now, the sweat and the heat and the pressure printing the Contact into the flesh. Nandi felt Enama hesitate. The hand wanting to let go — not the grip-release gesture of guilt. Physical pain. Contact burned.

Enama held.

Her eyes on Nandi. The pressure maintained despite the burn. Despite the cost. Contact had a price and Enama was paying it.

Nandi placed her free hand on the ground. Closed her eyes. The background tremor — 7 — and beneath, always beneath, the second heartbeat. Magnitude 11. Stronger. Closer. Not days. Not weeks. Hours.

She opened her eyes. Seized Enama's forearm — not the wrist, the forearm. Higher. The register of information, of the collective. The grip did not say *I'm in pain*. The grip said: *we need to descend faster*.

Nandi pressed. Hard. Constant. The grip that would not let go. Not Senzo's gesture — *follow me*. Nandi's gesture — the gesture of someone whose body felt the earth rising and who knew the earth would not wait.

Enama understood. Her eyes changed — not in expression, in depth. The gaze of the biologist who sees the signal in the other's body. The signal did not say *danger*. The signal said: *now*.

## Scene 5 — Enama's sacrifice

The passage narrowed.

Ahead of them, the gallery Thabo had followed since the CO2 pocket divided. No — it did not divide. It was collapsing. The ceiling descended. The walls closed in. The tunnel became a crawlway, then a hole, then — if the eyes did not lie in the orange light of the filaments — nothing. A wall.

Thabo held out the cloth. The fibers danced — hard. Harder than at any point since the start of the volcanic zones. The air was coming from the other side of the wall. The air was rushing through invisible fissures, capillaries in the rock, and the cloth caught it like an antenna.

But the wall held. Solid rock. No passage.

Inyoni stepped forward. Her hands on the wall — the climber's gesture, fingers searching for fissures, ridges, lines of weakness. She found one. A vertical fissure, twenty centimeters wide, rising into the ceiling. Not wide enough for a body. Not wide enough for the Charge.

She shook her head.

Nandi placed her bare feet on the ground at the foot of the wall. Seventy-eight degrees. The pain rose through her ankles like hot water — not the flood water, the scalding bath water, the water that makes the foot recoil by reflex. Nandi did not recoil. The soles of her feet — the right one still covered with skin, thickened, hardened by days of walking on burning rock; the left one raw, the burst blister covered with a brown crust — her feet read the ground.

There was a void behind the wall. A space. The ground vibrated differently on each side — the frequencies traveled faster through the void than through solid rock, and Nandi felt the acceleration under her toes. Behind the wall, a cavern. Behind the cavern, the descent continued.

But the wall held.

Thabo examined the rock. Placed his hands — blackened, burned, the skin of his palms cracked from the gas pocket — on the wall's surface. The geologist. He felt what his eyes could not see. The rock here was not homogeneous. The basalt formed a hard layer at the surface — one centimeter, perhaps two — but behind it, the rock was porous. Weakened by the heat. Saturated with trapped gas.

Thabo made the gesture. Hand flat, palm toward the wall, then a forward motion. *It can give way.* But his fingers opened at the end of the gesture — *if someone pushes*.

Inyoni looked at the Charge. Thirty-five kilos of seismic resonator. The sole reason for their descent, the object they had carried since the surface, protected by twenty-one straps and a shock-absorbing case eaten by salt. The Charge could not fit through the twenty-centimeter fissure.

The wall had to fall.

Nandi looked at Enama. Enama was looking at the wall. Her hands were open — open since the CO2 pocket, open since the gas had forced the Contact-world, open now no longer from the gas but by choice. Her open hands on the rock. Her palms flat against the wall.

The Contact-world.

Enama felt what Thabo had touched and what Nandi had read. The wall was thin. Fragile. Ready to give. But not here — farther. Lower in the tunnel, where the lava drops seeped from the ceiling, where the rock was hotter still and where the basalt layer thinned to nothing more than a membrane.

Enama removed her hands from the wall. Looked at the tunnel behind them. The path they had taken. Then the tunnel ahead — the tunnel that continued, that descended, that passed beneath the wall through a detour of two hundred meters perhaps through the zone of lava seepage.

Two hundred meters. In the hottest zone. Where lava fell from the ceiling.

Enama turned to Nandi.

She took her hands. Both hands. Not a wrist grip — the hands, palm against palm. The most direct Contact. The most painful Contact in the heat of the volcanic zones. Enama's skin against Nandi's skin, the sweat between them, the burn rising with each second of contact.

Enama did not let go.

Her right hand pressed Nandi's palm. Long pressure. Not a known Contact word. Not a codified grip. Not a calibration grip. Something else. A gesture Enama's hands were inventing at that instant, a gesture that had never needed to exist because no one had ever needed to say what Enama was saying.

The pressure was firm. Constant. Not a farewell — not yet. A transfer. Enama's hands were transmitting something to Nandi's hands. Not knowledge. Not a word. A weight. The weight of the earth. The weight of responsibility. Enama was placing the earth into Nandi's hands the way Inyoni had placed the Charge.

*I entrust you the ground.*

Nandi understood. She did not understand with words — Contact had no words for this. She understood with her hands. Enama's hands that had touched the earth since chapter I, that had felt the routes and the forks and the directions the earth indicated — those hands were transmitting their experience to Nandi's hands. Not the technique — you cannot transmit intuition through touch. But the permission. The permission to trust her feet.

Enama let go.

Her hands left Nandi's. The skin separated with the sound of wet cloth being peeled away — the sweat and the heat had nearly fused the two skins. Nandi felt the absence. The cold of the hot air on palms that no longer touched anything.

Enama turned toward the tunnel.

She said nothing. Contact said nothing. Her hands hung along her body — open this time. No longer closed. No longer clenched. Open and calm.

She walked.

Toward the tunnel that descended beneath the wall. Toward the lava seepage. Toward the two hundred meters of lethal zone that no one had asked to explore.

Thabo extended his arm. The Airmaster's gesture — the cloth before Enama's body, fibers dancing, the test. The air circulated. The air in the tunnel was breathable. Not the question. The question was the lava.

Inyoni moved. One step toward Enama. The hand reaching for the shoulder — the climber's reflex, the gesture of the one who catches, who holds back, who never lets go. *Holds.*

Enama placed her hand on Inyoni's fingers. Pushed them away. Gently. The Contact of refusal — but not Sihle's refusal. A different refusal. The refusal of the one who says: *this part is mine*.

She entered the tunnel.

The ground rose in temperature with each step. Nandi felt it in her feet — not Enama's steps, the vibrations. The rock was heating. Eighty. Eighty-five. The lava seeped from the ceiling in irregular drops, each drop a hiss, each hiss a potential burn.

Enama walked.

Her open hands touched the walls on each side. The Contact-world reopened, fully, definitively. She was speaking to the rock. The rock answered — not with words, with paths. The fissures where lava did not fall. The zones where the basalt was thicker. The places where a foot could land without meeting magma.

Nandi could no longer see her. The tunnel had turned. Enama had disappeared behind the bend of rock and all that remained were the vibrations — Enama's steps in the ground, Enama's weight on the rock, Enama's heart rate transmitted through Nandi's feet. Fifty-seven kilos of biologist, of cook, of survivalist, of woman who carried the guilt of a man fallen into an abyss.

Then a sound. A sound the rock transmitted to Nandi's feet before the air transmitted it to her ears. A crack. The sound of rock giving way — not the sound of massive collapse, not the sound of the abyss. The sound of a wall fissuring. The sound of two hundred meters of weakened basalt yielding under the blows of Enama's open hands.

The wall fell.

The air rushed in — a violent current, burning, charged with gas and lava dust, but air all the same, air coming from the other side, from the cavern Nandi had felt beneath her feet. Thabo's cloth exploded in the current. The fibers danced as they had not danced since the caverns.

Thabo looked at the passage. The wall was no longer a wall — a hole. A passage. Two meters wide, one meter fifty high. Enough for a body. Enough for the Charge.

Nandi listened to the vibrations.

Enama's steps had ceased.

Not ceased — changed. Not the vibration of a body walking. The vibration of a body stopping. The vibration of a body motionless in a zone at ninety-five degrees, in a tunnel where lava seeped from the ceiling, in a place where the basalt she had just broken was releasing the gas trapped behind it.

Nandi closed her eyes. Her feet on the rock. The frequencies. Enama's body — still standing. Still alive. Heart rate slower than before. No panic. No running.

Enama was not coming back.

Enama's hands — Nandi felt it without seeing, understood it without understanding — Enama's hands were flat on the ground. The Contact-world. The last Contact-world. The biologist's palms on the molten rock and the rock answering and the dialogue between the woman and the earth that had lasted since chapter II and that was ending here, in a lava tunnel, in the heat that does not forgive.

Enama had chosen.

Not the lava. Not suicide. The path. The path the earth showed her did not go back. The path went deeper — downward, toward the magma, toward the truth of the earth her hands had always sought. Enama's hands had listened to the ground all her life and the ground was saying now: *come*. And Enama was going.

Guilt was not the motive. Not only. Guilt had closed her hands after Sihle's death. The earth had reopened them. And Enama's open hands, touching the ground one last time, had heard what the earth had been telling her from the beginning: *you were right. Water was the path. Rock was the trap. You were right and he died anyway. But you were right.*

The reason for being right when everyone dies.

That is what Enama's last gesture had said to Nandi — the long pressure, the new pressure, the pressure invented at the instant of departure. *I entrust you the ground.* Not *I'm sorry*. Not *forgive me*. *The ground is yours now. Your feet know.*

Nandi placed her feet on the rock. Seventy-five degrees. The pain. The signal. Magnitude 7 vibrating beneath magnitude 11 rising.

Under her feet, the vibrations of Enama's body were slowing. The heart rate was descending. The weight was diminishing — not from falling. From stillness. From a body that stops fighting the heat and lets itself be taken by the earth.

The group's Contact lost one more word.

The calibration grip. The open hand on the rock. The Contact-world that had dazzled everyone in chapter II. The hands that cut roots and prepared porridge and felt the earth speak. The hands closed then reopened. The hands that said guilt and earth and transfer.

Dead.

Enama's grip on Nandi's wrist would never be made again. The two women of the earth — Enama through the hands, Nandi through the feet — were no longer two. Nandi was alone with the ground. Alone with the earth. Alone with her burned feet and her tremens and the weight of the world that Enama's hands had placed in her palms.

Three people.

The number was obscene. Seven at the start. Three now. More than half the group in the rock. More than half the Contact in the earth. More than half the words in silence.

Thabo's cloth. Inyoni's straps. Nandi's feet.

The 11 that was a pain in Nandi's bones, a pressure in her temples, a second heartbeat stronger than the first.

The air was forty-five degrees. The ground seventy-five. The lava seeped from the ceiling's fissures in regular drops. Three people toward the depths.

Where the Crawlway waited.

# VII — The Interior of the Volcano

## Scene 1 — The Gut

The Gut resembled nothing they had traversed.

The tunnels had been passages — wide or narrow, vertical or horizontal, but always passages. Spaces where a body could stand upright, raise its arms, turn its head. Human spaces. The Gut was not a human space.

A tube. A tube of hardened volcanic rock, perfectly cylindrical, polished by millennia of gas flow, whose walls gleamed in the orange light of the filaments like cooled molten metal. Sixty centimeters in diameter. Horizontal. No slope — a straight tube that pierced the rock over a distance Thabo could not measure because the cloth, held up at the opening, was sucked in so hard the fibers disappeared inside.

The air was flowing. Hard. Harder than anywhere since the start of the volcanic zones. The Gut was a conduit — a natural ventilation channel between two caverns, and the air rushed into it with the force of a river current. Hot air rose on one side. Cold air — less hot, rather — descended on the other. And between the two, the tube vibrated.

Nandi placed her hand on the opening. The metal — no, not metal, vitrified rock, basalt melted and cooled so fast it had taken the texture of glass — the tube vibrated under her palm. Not the background vibration. The vibration of the tube itself, which resonated like an instrument. The Gut was singing. A low, deep sound, inaudible to the ears but perfectly legible for Nandi's hands. The tube amplified the seismic frequencies like a tuning fork amplifies sound.

Beneath that vibration, the second pulse. The magnitude 11. Stronger here than anywhere else. The Gut amplified it too — the frequency of the catastrophe passed through the tube like a message, a warning, a countdown that Nandi's body registered without being able to quantify it.

Thabo knelt before the opening. His cloth — retrieved from inside the tube, the fibers soaked with condensation — hung between his burned fingers. He tested it one last time. The fibers danced. Hard. The air entered from the left side of the tube, exited from the right. Breathable. The oxygen was there.

He turned toward Inyoni and Nandi. Hand flat, lateral movement. *The air is flowing, we can go.*

The gesture had the same shape as in chapter I. The same hand, the same movement. But the hand was burned, the fingers cracked, the movement slower. The same word spoken by a different body — a body that had crossed the desert, the rift, the caverns, the volcanic zones. The words *the air is flowing* no longer meant the same thing. They meant: *the air is flowing and this may be the last time I say it*.

Inyoni examined the tube.

Sixty centimeters. The Charge was forty-five centimeters long and twenty-two in diameter. It fit. But the dampening case — the straps, the joints, the protective structure Sihle had designed — added fifteen centimeters on each side. The case did not fit.

Inyoni removed the case.

Her hands on the straps — one by one. The twenty-one original straps, the three improvised ones. She undid them. Counted as she undid. Twenty-four minus one. Twenty-four minus two. The reverse countdown. The mechanical prayer in reverse. Each undone strap was one less protection, one less of Sihle's calculations, one less layer of reason between the resonator and the world.

The bare Charge in Inyoni's hands. The cylinder of metal and ceramic, cold in the heat, dense in the void, thirty kilos without the case. The calibration dial glowed faintly in the orange light — Sihle's graduations, the numbers the seismograph would never read again.

Inyoni attached a short rope to the cylinder. The rope — the stump of rope, ten meters, all that remained of the two hundred meters from the start. Ten meters of desiccated, fragile fiber, eaten down to the weave by humidity and heat. Inyoni knotted. Checked. *Holds.*

She pushed the Charge into the tube.

The cylinder slid on the vitrified surface — the polished rock offered so little friction that the Charge sank two meters before Inyoni held back the rope. Metal against rock — a clear, pure sound, a bell sound that the tube amplified and that resonated in Nandi's bones like a gong.

The order in the Gut.

Inyoni entered first. After the Charge, ahead of Thabo. The climber flat on her belly, elbows on the ground, knees spread, the body crawling through the tube with the same fluidity it had descended the rift in chapter IV. Sixty centimeters. The space between the walls and Inyoni's body — ten centimeters on each side. Shoulders brushing the vitrified rock. Back touching the ceiling. No room to turn around. No room to raise the head. No room for anything other than crawling.

Thabo followed. Wider than Inyoni — the Airmaster's shoulders filled the tube, scraped the walls. The cloth folded beneath his chest, against the floor of the tube, the fibers crushed. The air flowed around him — against the walls, in the space between his body and the rock — but Thabo could no longer stretch the cloth to read it. In the Gut, the Airmaster was blind.

Nandi entered last.

Bare feet in the tube. The vitrified rock was hot — sixty-five, seventy degrees — but smooth. No roughness. No checkerboard of light and dark rocks. A single surface, uniform, burning. Nandi's feet on the tube. Nandi's palms on the tube. The entire body in contact with the rock.

Total Contact.

In the Gut, you could not NOT touch. The body against the rock on all sides. Elbows, knees, hips, belly, hands, feet — everything was Contact. The architecture imposed touch. The Gut was a forced Contact with the earth. Contact-world in its most radical form — not a dialogue, not a listening. An immersion. The body IN the earth.

Nandi felt everything.

The magnitude 7 in every centimeter of rock. The second pulse — the 11 — in the walls of the tube, amplified, magnified, as if the Gut were a stethoscope placed on the heart of the mountain. The magma below — not far. Two hundred meters perhaps. The heat rising. The gases circulating in the porous rock beneath the tube. The living earth. The earth that trembled and cooked and waited.

Ahead of her, Thabo's body crawled. His feet twenty centimeters from her face — the worn shoes, the soles melted in places, the laces replaced by strap ends. Thabo's feet did not read the ground like Nandi's — Thabo's feet walked. Thabo's hands read — air, cloth, currents. But in the Gut, Thabo's hands could not read the air. Thabo's hands crawled.

The tube resonated. Every movement — an elbow scraping, a knee pushing, a breath exhaled — the tube amplified it and transmitted it to everyone. The sound of Thabo's body reached Nandi's ears before the sound of her own. The noise of Inyoni's breathing, twenty meters ahead, resonated in the tube as if she were breathing into Nandi's ear.

The Gut was speaking.

Not in Contact. In sound. In raw vibration, uncodified, uninterpreted. The noise of bodies and rock and air. In the Gut, Contact lost its grammar. All that remained was the pure signal — the body against the earth, without filter, without code, without words.

## Scene 2 — Forced Contact

Inyoni stopped.

The rope — the link between Inyoni and the Charge — went taut. Nandi felt the vibration change. Inyoni's body had stopped crawling. Thabo's body, behind Inyoni, continued one meter before stopping too. The compression — three bodies in a tube sixty centimeters wide.

Thabo's feet touched Nandi's hands.

Contact. Not willed — geometric. Thabo's feet, Nandi's hands, in a space that did not permit distance. Involuntary touch. Imposed Contact.

Nandi felt Thabo's feet. The melted sole, the body's heat through the leather, the bones of the foot shifting beneath the skin. Thabo's feet were saying something — not a word, not a message. A state. The state of the Airmaster's body. Fatigue in the tendons. Hesitation in the ankles. Fear in the trembling of the toes.

The forced-Contact of the Gut did not filter. Chosen Contact — the codified gestures, the shoulder pressures, the grips — chosen Contact was a language. You said what you wanted to say. You hid what you wanted to hide. Forced-Contact hid nothing. Bodies in the tube touched each other the way bodies in the womb touch — by necessity, without shame, without filtering, and everything came through. Fear. Fatigue. Pain. Heat. Everything.

Nandi felt Thabo's fear in the soles of his feet.

Not the fear of danger — Thabo knew danger. The fear of the tube. The fear of smallness. The Airmaster, the man of breath, the man who read the air with a cloth stretched in the wind, the man who needed space for the fibers to dance — that man was locked in sixty centimeters of rock and he could not raise his arm to test the air and he could not turn around and he could not breathe wide and he could not be what he was.

The forced-Contact transmitted Thabo's fear to Nandi through the feet. And Nandi's fear — the tremens at maximum, the 11 in the bones, the tube amplifying everything — Nandi's fear traveled back up into Thabo's body through the same feet. The two fears crossed as frequencies cross in rock. Interference. The fear of one amplified the fear of the other.

The rope vibrated. The Charge, ahead of Inyoni, had shifted. The tube's vibrations had displaced it a few centimeters. The ten-meter rope — fragile, desiccated — transmitted the movement.

Inyoni crawled forward. The rope slackened. Thabo's body followed. Thabo's feet left Nandi's hands — one centimeter, two — and the forced-Contact broke.

The relief was physical. Nandi felt Thabo's fear leave her hands the way you feel a shoulder pressure that lifts. The absence of Contact. The relief of no longer feeling.

Then Thabo's feet came back. The tube did not permit distance. And the fear came back with the feet.

## Scene 3 — The Precursor Quake

Nandi felt it eighty seconds before.

Double the premonition of the volcanic zones. Triple that of the caverns. Eighty seconds. The Gut amplified the signal the way it amplified everything — the second pulse of the 11 rose in the walls of the tube and entered Nandi's body through every centimeter of skin in contact with the rock, and every centimeter of skin was in contact with the rock.

Nandi's body convulsed.

In the Gut, the convulsion had nowhere to go. Arms pinned to the walls. Legs in the tube. Back against the ceiling. Belly against the floor. Nandi's body vibrated in the tube the way a guitar string vibrates in its frame — imprisoned, amplified, transmitted.

Thabo felt the convulsion in his feet. The forced-Contact. Nandi's fear — not a fear, a signal. A warning. Nandi's body speaking to the earth and the earth answering and the tube transmitting the answer to every body.

Thabo struck the wall with his elbow. Three blows. Short, short, long. The rope code — the Contact-rope invented in chapter IV, translated into Contact-tube. *Stop. Danger.*

Ahead, Inyoni heard. Inyoni did not stop — she accelerated. The climber knew what the three blows meant. The three blows meant: the quake is coming and we are in a tube and there is nowhere to go.

There is nowhere to go.

Nandi counted. Sixty seconds. Fifty. The trembling was rising. The tube resonated — a low hum climbing in power like an organ whose stops are being opened. The vitrified rock vibrated in unison with the frequency of the approaching quake. The Gut was singing the catastrophe.

Forty seconds.

Inyoni crawled. The Charge ahead of her, pulled by the rope, the cylinder scraping the wall with a sound of metal on glass. Behind Inyoni, Thabo crawled. Behind Thabo, Nandi crawled. Three bodies in a tube sixty centimeters in diameter and the earth rising toward them.

Thirty seconds.

The hum became a rumble. The vitrified rock began to shake — not the background vibration, the vibration of the quake itself, the precursor vibration that precedes the main shock the way lightning precedes thunder.

Twenty seconds.

Nandi screamed.

Not a word — a sound. The sound of the body that can no longer contain the frequency. The sound of the tremens overflowing. The sound of the earth in the throat of a woman lying in a tube of sixty centimeters of vitrified rock. The scream resonated in the tube like one more shock — amplified, distorted, transformed into something no longer human. The tube screamed with her.

Ten seconds.

Inyoni reached the exit. The end of the Gut — an opening, a cavern, space. Her hands gripped the edge. The Charge came out first — pulled, pushed, projected out of the tube by Inyoni's arms. The cylinder rolled on the cavern floor. Thirty kilos of resonator without protection. Without case. Without straps. Bare.

Inyoni came out.

Thabo was mid-tube.

The quake struck.

Magnitude 8. The Gut twisted. The vitrified rock — hard, resistant, polished — cracked all at once along its entire length. A sound of shattering glass — sharp, violent, and the tube that had been a single piece became a hundred pieces that squeezed and separated to the rhythm of the quake. Sixty centimeters became fifty. Then forty-five. Then sixty again. The tube pulsed. The walls pressed the bodies and released them the way a heart presses blood and releases it.

Thabo was compressed. The walls against his shoulders. The ceiling against his back. The floor against his chest. The cloth beneath him — the fibers crushed, the breath of the village, the air of the entire expedition compressed between a man and a tube. The walls squeezed and Thabo heard his ribs creak. Not break — creak. The sound of bone that resists but bends. The sound of the body that says *holds*.

Nandi was in the middle of the tube.

The quake shook her in the rock like a pebble in a glass tube. Her body bounced against the walls — forehead against the ceiling, knees against the floor, elbows against the sides. Each impact left a mark. The tube printed the quake into Nandi's flesh. Total Contact — forced-Contact at maximum — the rock speaking to the body by striking it.

And the tube broke.

Not in the middle. Not at the end. Between Thabo and Nandi. A weak point — the initial crack, widened by the quake, become fracture, become rupture. The tube broke the way a bone breaks — clean, with a dry sound. The rock above the break point collapsed. Tons of basalt — the rock from the ceiling of the upper cavern, weakened by previous quakes, softened by heat — tons of rock fell into the tube and plugged it.

The wall.

The wall between Thabo and Nandi. One meter thick. Fallen rock, compacted by the weight of what was still falling. The sound of the collapse — a deep, continuous rumble, like thunder in the earth. The dust that filled the tube. The air that vanished.

The quake lasted twenty-three seconds. The rock trembled. The dust settled. The tube stabilized — broken, cracked, but stable. The collapse was over.

The silence.

## Scene 4 — The Separation

Nandi crawled toward the wall.

In the dark — the orange filaments had been torn away by the collapse, crushed beneath the rock. No light. The tube ahead of her, the fallen rock ahead of her, total darkness. Her hands on the debris. The hot rock. The sharp angles. Broken basalt, cutting, that sliced her palms.

She pushed.

The rock did not move. Tons of rock. Tons of mountain between her and Thabo. Between her and Inyoni. Between her and the rest of the team. Nandi pushed and the earth did not move and her hands cut on the edges and the blood flowed and the blood on the hot rock hissed.

On Nandi's side: the middle of the tube. Behind her — the tube toward the entrance. The way back. The way that was useless because you did not go back up.

On Thabo and Inyoni's side: the exit. The cavern. The Charge.

The Charge was on the other side of the wall.

No.

Nandi remembered. The Charge had been pushed through first. Inyoni had gotten it out of the tube before the quake. The Charge was in the cavern. Inyoni was in the cavern. Thabo was between the wall and the cavern — ten meters perhaps, the distance between the break point and the tube's exit.

Nandi crawled backward.

The tube behind her — cracked, unstable, but open. The quake had broken the tube at its weakest point but had not plugged the rear. Nandi crawled backward, toward the entrance of the Gut, toward the volcanic zones she had just traversed. Crawled until the tube opened into the cavern they had started from.

The burning air of the volcanic zones. The orange filaments. The light.

She turned around and looked at the Gut. The cracked tube, debris at the entrance, dust still escaping. The tube that led to Thabo and Inyoni. The tube that was plugged.

Nandi placed her feet on the cavern floor. Seventy-eight degrees. The pain. The signal. The tremens.

And in the tremens — two vibrations from the other side of the wall. Two bodies. Alive. Thabo and Inyoni were alive.

The vibrations were close. Close to each other. The two bodies were together — side by side, shoulder against shoulder perhaps, in the cracked tube or in the cavern on the other side. Thabo's and Inyoni's vibrations overlapped, intertwined, the way frequencies overlap in the tube.

Nandi listened.

The two bodies were moving. Slowly. Thabo's rhythm — regular, methodical, the rhythm of the Airmaster who explores, who tests, who searches for air. Inyoni's rhythm — faster, more nervous, the rhythm of the climber who searches for holds, who evaluates cracks, who counts options.

They were looking for a passage.

Nandi knew — her feet knew — that there was none. The wall was too thick. The rock too dense. The tons of fallen basalt formed a plug that two bodies could not displace. On the other side of the wall, Thabo and Inyoni searched and did not find.

Nandi placed her hands on the ground.

Contact-world. The cavern floor, the vibrations of the earth, the background frequency and the second pulse. The ground did not separate — the ground transmitted. The ground transmitted the vibrations of Thabo and Inyoni from the other side of the wall, through the rock, through the earth, through hundreds of meters of basalt that carried the steps of the living the way the ropes carried the words.

Nandi felt Thabo stop. Thabo's body ceased moving. The vibration of his steps vanished. What remained was the vibration of his weight — seventy-five kilos on rock, the body standing, motionless.

The cloth.

Nandi could not see the cloth. But she knew Thabo. Thabo, when he stopped, stretched the cloth. Always. By reflex. By identity. Thabo, on the other side of the wall, had stretched the cloth and had read the air.

The air on the other side could not be good.

Inyoni's vibrations stopped too. The two bodies motionless. Side by side. Shoulder against shoulder. The "test-and-holds." Thabo tests the air. Inyoni tightens.

Tightens what? No more straps. No more rope. No more Charge. Inyoni, on the other side of the wall, had nothing left to count.

Inyoni's fingers must have been counting anyway. Something. Anything. The fibers of Thabo's cloth perhaps. Or Thabo's fingers. Or her own fingers. Inyoni was always counting.

## Scene 5 — The Shared Death

The vibrations changed.

Not suddenly. Slowly. Like a tide going out. Thabo's rhythm — still regular, still methodical — slowed. Inyoni's rhythm — still fast, still counting — slowed too. The two rhythms converged. Two different tempos drawing closer, aligning, becoming a single tempo.

Nandi felt the two bodies draw closer. Not moving — drawing closer. Thabo's weight shifted a few centimeters toward Inyoni's weight. Inyoni's weight shifted toward Thabo's. The vibrations overlapped.

Shoulder against shoulder. More than the shoulder — the whole body. Thabo and Inyoni, on the other side of the wall, had sat down. Side by side. Backs against the tube wall perhaps. Bodies in contact.

The Contact between them.

Nandi could not see it. Nandi could not feel it directly — the vibrations of Contact were too fine to cross a meter of fallen rock. But she felt the consequences. The heart rates of Thabo and Inyoni aligned. Like two metronomes on the same table that end up beating together — the two hearts, separated by a few centimeters of skin and ribs, were synchronizing.

The "test-and-holds" reduced to its purest form.

No cloth. No strap. No air to read. No fiber to count. Just two bodies side by side in a tube of sixty centimeters of broken rock, and the Contact between them that no longer said anything other than: *I am here. You are here.*

The simplest Contact. The oldest. The Contact from before words, from before codified grips, from before arm-locks and shoulder pressures. The Contact of pure touch — skin against skin, body heat against body heat, and nothing else.

Nandi stayed.

She stayed with her hands on the ground, her feet on the rock, her whole body listening. She stayed and she felt the two vibrations on the other side of the wall continue. Continue together. Two rhythms become one. Two breaths become one. The Contact of Thabo and Inyoni continued on the other side of the wall and Nandi, on this side, was the witness of a dialogue she could not hear but could feel — through the rock, through the ground, through the earth that transmitted everything.

The vibrations slowed further.

The hearts barely beat. The breaths were murmurs in the rock. The oxygen — almost nothing left. Thabo's brain was falling asleep. Inyoni's brain was falling asleep. The Contact between them was not falling asleep — the Contact between them continued. Inyoni's fingers were still counting. More slowly. But still.

One... two... three...

Then the vibrations stopped.

Not at the same time — almost. Thabo's heart stopped first. Inyoni's beat on — three beats, four — then stopped too. The vibrations of the two bodies ceased on the other side of the wall. The weight remained — two bodies of seventy-five and fifty-five kilos still pressing on the rock. But dead weight does not vibrate. Dead weight does not walk. Dead weight does not make Contact.

Nandi lifted her hands from the ground.

Placed them on her knees. Looked at them. Palms cut by broken basalt, dried blood mixed with dust. The hands that had felt the death of Thabo and Inyoni through the rock. The hands that still felt — not the dead, not the living. The nothing. The absence of vibration where two vibrations had been intertwined.

Forty-seven fibers on the Gut's rope. Inyoni had counted them when she tied the Charge. Forty-seven. Nandi did not know that number. Nandi would never know that number.

But the number existed. And Inyoni's fingers had counted it. And the counting was Inyoni's last Contact — fingers counting the fingers of the man beside her in a tube of sixty centimeters of broken rock. One, two, three, four, five. *Holds.*

The only shared death in the novel. The only death where Contact does not die before the bodies. On the other side of the wall, Thabo and Inyoni were together. The Contact between them had survived after Contact with the group had ceased. Shoulder against shoulder. Hand in hand. The cloth and the strap. The test and the holds. Together.

The idiolect of their pair did not die with them. It died between them. Which is different. Which is worse. Which is better.

## Scene 6 — Nandi Alone

Nandi looked at the Gut.

The cracked tube, plugged by the collapse. On the other side: two bodies. The exit. The cavern. And the Charge — the thirty-kilo cylinder that Inyoni had ejected from the tube before the quake.

The Charge was on the other side of the wall.

Nandi crawled into the tube.

Not toward the wall — backward. Toward the volcanic zones. Toward the cavern they had started from. The cracked tube creaked under her weight — sounds of glass, screeching, the vitrified rock breaking centimeter by centimeter. The tube would not survive a second quake. The tube might not survive five minutes.

She came out. The cavern. The orange filaments. The burning air.

Then she looked at the cavern wall.

The cavern had another passage. A passage they had seen before entering the Gut — a narrower, lower passage that descended steeply toward the south. Thabo had tested it. The cloth had barely moved — air circulated poorly there. The passage was hot, narrow, dangerous. But it existed.

The passage went around the Gut.

Nandi knew it before verifying. Her feet on the cavern floor, the vibrations, the underground geography that her body had learned to read since the surface of the desert. The passage descended, turned, passed under the Gut, and opened — somewhere, at a lower level, into the cavern on the other side. Where the Charge waited.

Nandi entered the passage.

Alone.

The ground at eighty degrees. The walls at sixty. The air — Thabo was no longer there to read it. The cloth was no longer there to dance. Nandi breathed and the air was heavy and hot and she did not know if the air was breathable or if it would kill her slowly. She did not know. The cloth dead and the Airmaster dead and the knowledge of air dead.

The checkerboard had vanished. Everything was dark.

Nandi walked on the dark rocks.

Pain and tremens were merging. Seventy-five degrees — pain. The tremens — signal. Pain and signal blended in Nandi's feet and she could no longer separate them. The burn resembled a frequency. The frequency resembled a burn. The body no longer distinguished between reading and suffering.

The passage descended. Descended. Lower than the Gut. Lower than the cavern on the other side. Nandi descended into the earth, alone, without rope, without Charge, without Contact. Her hands touched the walls — the hot walls, almost burning, the rock that oozed no longer water but something more viscous, slower, lava in the process of solidifying. The rock was melting around her. The earth was melting.

The passage turned. Widened. Opened out.

A cavern.

The cavern on the other side of the Gut. Nandi arrived from below — the passage had brought her beneath the cavern, into an opening at ground level, a hole in the wall through which she crawled the last meters.

The Charge was there.

The metal cylinder lay on the cavern floor, three meters from the Gut's opening. Bare. Without case. Without straps. The calibration dial facing the ceiling. The metal had rolled on the hot ground and come to rest against a ridge of rock.

Nandi crawled toward the Charge. Took it in her arms. Thirty kilos. The weight was a presence. A body. The heaviest thing she had carried since — since when? Since the beginning. Since the surface of the desert.

She pressed the cylinder against herself. The cold metal — always cold, always absorbing — against her burning chest. The temperature contrast was a relief. The Charge was cool. The Charge was the only object in this world that did not burn.

Then she raised her eyes toward the Gut.

The opening — plugged. The wall of rock. On the other side: Thabo and Inyoni. Their bodies. Their Contacts. Thabo's cloth — the breath of the village, the air of the entire expedition, the fibers that had danced since chapter I — the cloth was on the other side. Inyoni's hands — the hands that counted, that tightened, that never let go — the hands were on the other side.

Thirty kilos. Without straps. Without case. The bare metal against her shoulder blades. The makeshift rope over the shoulders — three meters of desiccated fiber that cut into the skin through the shreds of clothing.

One person.

Contact was dead. No more recipient. No more hands to touch. No more shoulder against shoulder. Nandi's vocabulary — the grips, the pressures, the gestures learned from seven people over eight chapters — the vocabulary still existed in her hands but had no one left to speak to.

Nandi's hands were full of words and the world was empty.

She took a step. The ground at eighty-five degrees. The pain. The signal. The magnitude 7.5 vibrating beneath the 11 that was rising. The cavern descended southward. Toward the heart of the volcano. Toward the detonation point.

Nandi walked. Alone. The Charge on her back. Her feet on the rock. Contact without recipient. The body that knew.

The rest was only descent.

# VIII — The Final Cave

## Scene 1 — Last approach

The world was shrinking.

Not the tunnel — the tunnel was widening, actually. The cavern Nandi was crossing was the largest since the village of the deaf — a vast space, a cathedral of rock, its ceiling vanishing into the orange dark of dying filaments. The physical space was growing. The world was shrinking.

Nandi's world fit in her feet and in her hands and in the thirty kilos of metal on her back. No more fabric to read the air. No more seismograph to measure frequencies. No more shoulder against shoulder. No more voice — not even the transgression of voice, not even Enama's single word, *water*, not even that existed anymore because voice in the void says nothing to anyone.

Nandi walked.

The ground at ninety degrees. The rocks — all dark now. The checkerboard had vanished with Thabo. The dead geologist's tool dead with the geologist — the eyes that distinguished light from dark, cold from hot, path from trap. Nandi's eyes couldn't tell the difference. Nandi's feet no longer told the difference. Pain was everywhere, uniform, a background noise covering the signal.

No. Not covering. MERGING with the signal.

The pain of the burn vibrated at a frequency. The tremens vibrated at a frequency. The two frequencies were close — so close that Nandi's body no longer knew which was the earth and which was the flesh. Pain resembled a magnitude. Magnitude resembled pain. Nandi's body was becoming an instrument that could no longer be calibrated because the measurement and the thing measured were the same thing.

The Charge weighed on her back. Thirty kilos without the case, without straps, the makeshift rope sawing into her shoulders. The metal cylinder absorbing the heat from Nandi's body — always cold, always hungry — and giving it back through the dial, through the locks, through the inaudible sound the resonator emitted in the dark.

The ground trembled. Magnitude 8. The base floor had risen a point since the Crawlway. The quakes no longer stopped — they had never truly ceased, but the distinction between quake and calm had vanished. The world trembled continuously. The ground vibrated, the walls vibrated, the air vibrated. Nandi walked through a world in permanent vibration and her body vibrated with it and there was no longer any boundary between the body and the world.

The orange filaments were turning red in places. Dark red — almost black. The bioluminescence was dying. The organisms that had survived millennia in the bowels of the volcano, that had thrived in the moderate heat of the caverns, those organisms no longer survived in the zones near the magma. The filaments retracted. The lights went out. The dark advanced.

Nandi walked in the dark.

No more light. No more filaments. No more visual markers. The world was a sound, a vibration, a temperature. Nandi's feet on the burning rock — the only contact. Nandi's hands on the walls — the only guide. The Body in the earth.

She descended. Each step brought her closer to the point her tremens had designated since the surface of the desert — the point where the magnitude 11 was born, the point of maximum resonance, the point where the Charge had to be placed. Her body knew. Her body had always known. The tremens was not a disease. The tremens was a compass.

The air was unbreathable. Almost. Her lungs burned with each breath. The sulfur, the CO2, the volcanic emanations — everything Thabo would have detected, everything the fabric would have signaled. Nandi detected nothing. Nandi breathed and the world entered her lungs and she didn't know if the next breath would kill her.

She breathed anyway.

One step. Then another. The Charge on her back. Feet on the rock. The tremens in her bones. The dark ahead. The dark behind.

And beneath the dark, beneath the trembling, beneath the pain — the second heartbeat. The magnitude 11. Stronger than ever. Closer than ever. The heart of the earth beating beneath the feet of the last survivor.

## Scene 2 — Nandi alone

Contact died.

Not all at once — it had been dying since chapter IV. Word by word. Gesture by gesture. Idiolect by idiolect. But in the previous chapters, Contact died by amputation — one speaker less, one vocabulary less. The language shrank but still existed because there was still someone to talk to.

Now there was no one.

Nandi set the Charge on the ground. Sat down. The ground at ninety-five degrees. The fabric of her pants — shreds, crusts of salt and sweat and blood — the fabric between skin and burning rock. Hands on her knees. Hands that knew dozens of words, hundreds of gestures, an entire language learned from birth and perfected by the expedition — hands that had no one left to speak to.

Nandi raised her hands. Looked at them. In the dark, she couldn't see them — but she felt them. Palms cut by the basalt of the Crawlway. Fingers cracked by the heat. Dried blood beneath the nails. Hands that had touched Senzo, Sihle, Enama, Thabo, Inyoni, Jabu. Hands that had received the old woman's gesture. Hands that had learned the locks of the Charge.

Nandi's hands made a gesture.

Not a Contact gesture — no recipient. A reflex gesture. The right hand rose. Descending arc. *We don't go back up.* Senzo's gesture in Nandi's hands. The dead leader's gesture in the hands of the last one alive.

The gesture fell into the void.

No one saw it. No one felt it. The descending arc of Nandi's hand — the same movement, the same trajectory, the same word — fell into the dark like a stone falls into a well. No echo. No answer.

Contact without a recipient is not Contact. It's movement. It's choreography. It's wind — and in a world where wind has been replaced by earth, it's nothing.

Nandi lowered her hand.

The silence of Contact. Not acoustic silence — the volcano's rumbling filled the space, the quakes made the air and rock vibrate, the world was full of noise. But tactile silence. The silence of hands that find nothing anymore. The silence of skin that touches nothing but rock.

The most intimate language in the world — the one that passed only through touch — had no more reason to exist. Contact was born because the earth trembled and humans needed to speak to each other despite the trembling. Contact was dying because all the humans were dead and the earth still trembled.

Nandi stood up.

The Charge on her back. Feet on the rock. The tremens in her bones.

And beneath the tremens, a question. A question the body was asking and the mind couldn't formulate: if Contact between humans is dead, does a Contact with something else remain?

The earth vibrated beneath her feet. The earth had always vibrated beneath her feet. Nandi's feet on the earth — world-Contact. The Contact Enama had practiced with her hands, the Contact the old woman had pushed beyond language, the Contact that was not between two humans but between a body and the ground.

Nandi was alone. But the earth was there.

Contact was perhaps not dead. Contact was changing recipient.

## Scene 3 — The 127 hours

The passage narrowed.

Nandi crawled. No more tunnel — a crevice. Rock on each side, above, below. Sixty centimeters, then fifty, then forty. Nandi's body threaded through the earth like a finger threads into a glove. The Charge ahead of her — pushed, pulled by the rope stump, the cylinder scraping against rock.

The temperature rose. A hundred degrees at the ground. A hundred. The rock burned to the touch. Nandi's hands — the hands that were her reading tool, her Contact instrument, the hands that had touched six people and the earth — Nandi's hands burned on the rock. Each placement left a mark. The skin stuck. The skin peeled away. The skin stayed.

She crawled on her knees. Not on her feet — her feet could no longer bear the ground. The sole of the left foot was an open wound, flesh raw, nerves exposed. The sole of the right foot — the cracks in the callus had given way, the skin had come off in patches, the flesh underneath was red and weeping and each contact with rock sent a discharge of pain that shot up to the hip.

Nandi's feet no longer read.

No — Nandi's feet no longer read ANYTHING BUT pain. And pain was the signal. Pain and the tremens had fused in Nandi's feet and there was no longer any difference and suffering WAS the reading — suffering was the magnitude, suffering was the frequency, suffering was the earth speaking in the only language left when every other language was dead.

Nandi crawled and the earth spoke to her through pain.

The passage narrowed further. Thirty-five centimeters. The Charge no longer fit straight through — Nandi had to tilt it, turn it, pivot it in the crevice. The dial scraped against the wall. A sound of metal against rock — a screech that echoed through the passage like a scream.

Her body could no longer continue.

That's what the muscles said. The arms — exhausted, trembling, fibers no longer responding to commands. The legs — knees raw, kneecaps exposed, blood sticky on the rock. The back — thirty kilos of Charge during the hours of descent, vertebrae compressed, the spine grinding like the rock.

Nandi's body said *stop*.

Nandi did not stop.

Not out of heroism. Not out of courage. Because the tremens did not stop. The tremens didn't know *stop*. The tremens was the signal of the earth and the earth never stopped and Nandi's body, tuned to the earth since birth, could no more stop than the earth could cease to tremble.

She crawled.

The passage widened. Thirty-five centimeters became fifty. Fifty became a meter. A meter became — a space. A void. The passage opened out.

Nandi fell.

Not far — a meter, maybe two. The passage gave onto a ledge, a lip of rock above a larger space, and Nandi, crawling out of the passage, toppled over the edge and fell. Her body struck the ground — a hundred and five degrees. Pain exploded. Ribs. Knees. Hands. Everything.

The Charge, pulled by the rope, toppled after her. The cylinder fell. Struck the ground beside Nandi. The metal rang — a bell sound, deep, that resonated through the cavern like a gong. The sound of the resonator striking rock. The sound of the mission arriving at its destination.

Nandi lay on the ground. Eyes closed. Body broken. Pain everywhere. The tremens everywhere. Magnitude everywhere. Everything was the same thing.

She opened her eyes.

The dark. Total dark. Not a filament. Not a glimmer. The bioluminescence was dead — too hot, too deep, too far from everything. The world was a sound. A sound of constant rumbling, deep, vast, like the sound of the ocean she had never seen. The sound of the magma chamber.

Nandi was in the detonation chamber.

The heart of the volcano.

## Scene 4 — The ghosts

Three pressures on the nape of the neck.

Slow. Warm. From one side to the other.

Nandi didn't move. The three pressures — Senzo's pressure. The most intimate Contact. Fingers on the nape, the warmth of the hand, the slow rhythm. *Follow me. The world still trembles.*

The fingers were warm. Too warm — a hundred degrees, the temperature of the rock. The fingers were not fingers. The burst capillaries in Nandi's neck, the blood pulsing through overheated veins, the blood pressure mimicking the pressure of a hand. The body remembered what the mind was losing.

Nandi raised her hand to her nape. Touched the skin. Warm. Wet with sweat. No hand. No fingers. No Senzo.

She went back to crawling.

The Charge ahead of her. The rope stump in her left hand. Right hand on the ground. Knees on the rock. The body advancing in the dark toward the point the tremens designated — the point of maximum resonance. Not far. Each step brought it closer. Each vibration confirmed it. The ground beneath Nandi's knees said: *more*. The ground beneath Nandi's hands said: *almost*.

Two cold fingers on the forearm.

Sihle's technical Contact. Two fingers — index and middle, placed flat, pressure measured, deliberate. The Contact of data. The Contact of pure information.

Nandi looked at her forearm. The skin burned, cracked, covered in crusts and blood. The two fingers were not there. But the pressure was there — real, physical, measurable. The veins of the forearm, swollen by dehydration, pulsed against the skin and the pulsation had the exact shape of two fingers. The body was reproducing Sihle's Contact with its own vessels.

And for an instant — a brief instant, an instant of oxygen deprivation and extreme heat — Nandi felt the data. The frequencies. The magnitudes. The numbers Sihle noted in the margins of the scroll. As if the two cold fingers on her forearm were transmitting readings from the seismograph no one carried anymore. Pure information. Reason in a world of flesh.

Magnitude 9. Ground temperature: a hundred and ten. Estimated depth: two thousand meters below the surface. The numbers formed in Nandi's mind with Sihle's precision — numbers she couldn't know, that no instrument measured anymore, that only a body at the end of its degradation could invent.

The cold fingers vanished. The forearm vein stopped pulsing.

Nandi crawled.

The grip arrived at the wrist.

Strong. Too strong — Enama's pressure, the calibration grip, the whole hand on the wrist. The grip that shared the frequency. The grip that had said *I entrust the ground to you*. The grip of Enama's last Contact — the long pressure, invented, the pressure that would not come again.

Nandi's wrist pulsed. Dehydration, heat, the heart beating too fast and too hard. The pulse mimicked the grip. The pulse mimicked Enama. Nandi's body was reproducing the dead woman's Contact with its own blood.

Nandi clenched her fist on the void. Enama's grip — but no one to grasp. Nandi's fingers closed on the burning air. *I entrust the ground to you.* The ground vibrated beneath Nandi's knees. The ground answered.

A breath on the face.

The air — a draft, hot, charged with sulfur. The current passed through a crack in the rock and grazed Nandi's face like a fabric held out before you. Thabo's fabric. The village's breath. The breath of the Airmaster testing the air before Nandi one last time.

*The air is breathable. You can continue.*

The breath was not Thabo. The breath was the volcano — the burning air of the magma chamber seeping through cracks. But Nandi's face interpreted it the way Nandi's face had interpreted Thabo's fabric since chapter I — as a test, as a signal, as a *the air circulates, we can go*.

Nandi continued.

Fingers on her fingers.

Pressures. One, two, three. Quick, methodical. Inyoni's fingers counting. Not Inyoni's fingers — the spasms of Nandi's fingers. Dehydration made her hands tremble, and the trembling of her hands resembled Inyoni's pressures. One, two, three. *Holds. Holds. Holds.*

The ghost-fingers counted the Charge's locks. One — clockwise. Two — clockwise. Three — counterclockwise. Inyoni's counting in Nandi's hands. The transfer was complete. The dead woman's fingers guided the living woman's fingers toward the three clicks.

Nandi placed her hands on the Charge. Touched the locks. One, two, three. Not yet. Not here. But the hands knew.

Silence within the silence.

Jabu.

No pressure. No fingers. No grip. No breath. Nothing. Jabu's ghost was the absence of ghost. The most discreet character remained the most discreet even in death. Jabu's Contact had always been non-Contact — the *no need*, the gourds filled in silence, the cart pulled alone.

And Jabu's absence was there. Nandi felt it. Not a touch — a lack. The missing water. The gourd empty for hours. The dry throat. The salt on the lips. Jabu had never touched Nandi — he had filled her gourds. And the absence of water was Jabu's Contact. The vanished care. The useful silence vanished.

The ghosts multiplied.

The nape. The forearm. The wrist. The face. The fingers. The absence. Six dead. Six touches. Nandi's body was reproducing the dead's Contact with its own failures — the burst capillaries, the overheated veins, the muscle spasms, the drafts. The body remembered because the body IS memory. Flesh carries the trace of the hands that touched it.

The ghosts superimposed.

All at once. The nape and the forearm and the wrist and the face and the fingers and the absence — six simultaneous Contacts. Like the shoulder circle of chapter IV — seven people in a circle, hands on the neighbors' shoulders, mourning Senzo. Six hands on Nandi's shoulders. The circle reconstituted. Contact complete — all the speakers, all the idiolects, all the words.

The most beautiful Contact of the novel. The cruelest. The Contact that did not exist. The Contact fabricated by a body dying in a volcano, the body reproducing the dead's touches with its own wounds. The truest hallucination in the world.

Nandi raised her hands.

The hands that carried six ghosts. The hands that felt six Contacts. The hands full of words that no longer had speakers.

She placed her hands on the Charge.

The ghosts vanished.

All at once. All of them. The nape became a nape again. The forearm became a forearm again. The wrist, the face, the fingers — everything became body again. The ghosts dissipated like vapor dissipates when the temperature changes. The ghost-Contact died the moment Nandi's hands touched the metal.

The ghost-Contact died when the world-Contact was born.

The dead yielded their place to the earth.

## Scene 5 — The detonator

The point of maximum resonance.

Nandi knew because her body knew. Not by calculation. Not by measurement. The tremens — the tremens that had been her curse, her suffering, the signal Sihle rejected and the world didn't understand — the tremens reached a state Nandi had never known.

The tremens stopped.

Not the cessation of pain — pain was still there. The cessation of difference. Nandi's body vibrated at exactly the same frequency as the earth. The tremens was no longer a received signal — it was the signal itself. Body and earth vibrated together. Synchronization.

This is the place.

Nandi set the Charge on the ground.

The cylinder on the rock. Metal against stone. The seismic resonator at the exact spot where it had to be — the point where frequencies converged, the point where the magma chamber was closest, the point where the magnitude 11 was born.

Her hands on the dial.

Sihle's graduations. Numbers engraved in the metal — frequencies, hertz, measurements Nandi couldn't read. Sihle's seismograph was at the bottom of a chasm. Thabo's readings were on the other side of a wall of rock. Inyoni's calculations had stopped with her last breath.

Nandi had no instrument. Nandi had no data. Nandi had no training.

Nandi had the gesture.

The old woman's gesture. The gesture of the village of the deaf. The gesture received in chapter III and carried for five chapters without being understood. The gesture that was not a word but an act. The gesture that was not Contact but synchronization.

Nandi placed her palms on the Charge.

Palms against metal. The dial beneath the right palm. The locks beneath the fingers. The cylinder — cold, dense, the last object in the world — beneath the hands of the last human.

She initiated the trembling.

Not the tremens — the trembling. The voluntary trembling. The old woman's gesture learned at the village. The forearms that vibrate. Not at random — at a frequency. The frequency Nandi had carried in her bones since birth. The frequency of the earth.

The trembling descended. From the forearms to the wrists. From the wrists to the hands. From the hands to the palms. From the palms to the metal.

The Charge responded.

The resonator — designed to amplify frequencies, designed to enter resonance with rock — the resonator sensed Nandi's frequency. The metal vibrated. The dial turned — no, the dial didn't turn. The dial didn't need to turn. The Charge calibrated through the hands. Nandi's frequency passed through the metal and the metal tuned itself.

The unknown gesture was not a message. It was the key.

The old woman of the village had seen Nandi's tremens — that vibration the surface rejected, that instruments despised, that Sihle noted in the margins without understanding. The old woman had recognized what no one else had recognized: Nandi's body was tuned to the earth. Nandi's body WAS a seismograph. All Nandi lacked was the technique to emit instead of receive.

The gesture had transformed the antenna into a transmitter.

And the transmitter was now pressed against a resonator that amplified the signal and transmitted it to the rock and the rock transmitted it to the magma chamber and the magma chamber trembled at Nandi's frequency.

The body had been right from the beginning.

Nandi opened her eyes. The dark. The rumbling. The heat. The Charge beneath her hands, vibrating, tuned, ready.

Her fingers found the first lock. The rotary cylinder on the left. Clockwise. A quarter turn. Click.

*One.*

The second. On the right. Clockwise. A quarter turn. Click.

*Two.*

The third. Underneath. Counterclockwise. The trap. Nandi's fingers turned the right way. Counterclockwise. A quarter turn. Click.

*Three.*

*Holds. Holds. Holds.*

Inyoni's words. Nandi's hands. The locks open.

The Charge was armed.

Nandi searched for the trigger. The button — a disc recessed into the top of the cylinder, protected by a metal rim. Nandi's fingers found it. Metal beneath the thumb.

Ninety seconds. That's what Inyoni had said. Ninety seconds after detonation. Irreversible.

Nandi placed her thumb on the button.

The old woman's gesture still vibrated in her palms. The voluntary trembling. The emission. Nandi's body speaking to the earth in the only language the earth understood — vibration.

Nandi's last Contact would not be with a human.

Nandi's last Contact would be with the Earth.

Nandi pressed.

## Scene 6 — The detonation

Ninety seconds.

The counting began in Nandi's hands. One, two, three, four, five. Inyoni's counting. Fingers counting the seconds as they had counted the straps, the fibers, the locks, Thabo's fingers.

The Charge hummed.

The resonator emitted. The frequency — Nandi's frequency, the earth's frequency, the same frequency — propagated through the rock. The sound was not audible. The sound was tactile. The ground vibrated beneath Nandi's knees. The walls vibrated against her shoulders. The air vibrated in her lungs.

Everything vibrated at the same frequency. The body. The Charge. The earth.

Thirty seconds.

The rock responded. Nandi felt it in every bone — the resonance. The resonator amplified and the rock sent it back and the resonator amplified the return and the loop tightened. The frequency rose. The power rose. The resonance cycle — the same cycle that brought down a bridge when soldiers marched in step, the same cycle that shattered a glass when a voice found its frequency — the cycle was running away.

Sixty seconds.

The magma chamber responded.

Nandi felt the response in her feet — her destroyed feet, her feet that read nothing but pain anymore, her feet that no longer told the difference between the burn and the signal. But the magma chamber's response was not a subtle signal. The response was a wall. A wall of frequency rising from the depths of the earth and striking the ground beneath Nandi's feet with the force of everything that had been held back too long.

The magnitude began to rise.

8.5. The ground shook. The walls cracked. The rock fissured in a star pattern around the Charge — the stress lines giving way, the forces accumulated over millennia finally finding their path.

9. The rumbling became a roar. The sound of the earth opening. The sound of the magma chamber answering the resonator like a bell answers the hammer.

Seventy-five seconds.

Nandi was no longer counting. Inyoni's fingers were no longer counting. The counting had given way to the noise — the noise of the earth, the noise of the rock, the noise of the world rearranging itself.

9.5. The ground beneath Nandi rose. Not by a centimeter — by a meter. The entire floor of the cavern rose like a diaphragm and Nandi was thrown into the air and fell back and the Charge beside her vibrated so hard the metal sang — a pure sound, a crystal sound, the sound of a resonator that had found its frequency and would never let it go.

10.

The rock no longer held. The walls of the cavern were collapsing — not inward, outward. The magma chamber was pushing. The pressure from below met the rock from above and the rock gave way. The fissures opened. Lava appeared — not drops this time. Rivers. Walls of lava rising through the fissures and filling the cavern.

Eighty-five seconds.

Nandi was on the ground. Hands on the Charge. Palms on the vibrating metal. The voluntary trembling — the old woman's gesture — had become the trembling of the world. Nandi's frequency and the earth's frequency were the same frequency and the resonator amplified them and the magma chamber responded.

10.5. The mountain moved.

Not the cavern. The mountain. Two thousand meters above Nandi, the surface of the volcano cracked. The accumulated energy — millennia of compression, billions of tons of rock, the pressure of a world that had trembled since forever — the energy found its path. The resonance had opened the door.

Ninety seconds.

Magnitude 11.

The sound vanished. Not because there was no more sound — because the sound had become so total, so absolute, so omnipresent that it was no longer distinguishable from silence. The sound of the magnitude 11 was the sound of the entire world vibrating at the same frequency. The sound of the earth finally speaking — not in the language of humans, not in Contact, not in arm-locks and shoulder pressures and coded grips. In its own language. The language of earthquakes. The language that existed before humans and would exist after.

Nandi felt the Charge beneath her hands. The metal no longer vibrated — the metal had become the vibration. Nandi's hands on the Charge. The hands that had learned Contact on the surface of the desert, the hands that had touched six people, the hands that had received the old woman's unknown gesture and the Charge's locks from Inyoni and the transfer of the earth from Enama — Nandi's hands resting on the resonator and the resonator's vibration in the hands and the earth's vibration in the resonator and the earth's vibration in the hands.

Contact with the Earth.

Nandi's hands were sending a final Contact. Not to Senzo. Not to Sihle. Not to Enama, Thabo, Inyoni, Jabu. To the Earth. Contact between a body and the world. The Contact that had begun when Nandi placed her bare feet on the desert ground and that was ending here, palms on the resonator, body in the volcano, vibration in the hands.

The loop was closing.

Contact was born because the earth trembled. Contact was returning to the earth.

## Epilogue

Silence.

For the first time in the novel — the first time in eight chapters, in seven deaths, in thousands of Contact gestures and hundreds of quakes and days and nights of ceaseless trembling — for the first time, the earth no longer trembled.

The silence was not the absence of noise. The silence was the absence of vibration. The ground — this ground that had rumbled beneath Nandi's feet since her birth, this ground that had carried the magnitudes and the frequencies and the tremens, this ground that had been the interlocutor of world-Contact — the ground was still.

The magnitude 11 had released the constraints. The resonance had emptied the chamber. The energy accumulated over millennia had discharged at once — controlled, directed, channeled by the resonator that seven people had carried from the surface and that only one had activated.

The mountain was breathing.

Not the breath of air in the tunnels. Not the current Thabo read with his fabric. The breath of rock releasing. The breath of pressure yielding. The breath of the earth which, for the first time, no longer needed to tremble.

Contact no longer had a reason to exist.

Contact was born because the earth trembled and humans needed to speak to each other despite the trembling. If the earth stopped trembling — not forever, not for long, but for this instant, for this silence that followed the detonation — then Contact no longer had a cause. The language born of catastrophe no longer had a catastrophe.

But Contact had existed.

The words had been spoken. The gestures had been made. The hands had touched shoulders and forearms and wrists and napes. The vocabulary — the arm-locks, the shoulder pressures, the coded grips, the idiolects — the vocabulary had existed in the hands of seven people and had vanished with them. An entire language, born and dead in eight chapters.

And beneath the silence, in the rock, in the earth, in the bones of the mountain — the trace. Nandi's vibration. The frequency of her body, transmitted by the resonator, amplified by the chamber, propagated through the rock. The last Contact. Contact with the Earth. The vibration of a human body in the body of the mountain.

The earth no longer trembled.

But the earth remembered.
