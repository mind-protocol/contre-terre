# IV — The Fault

## Scene 1 — The vertical passage

The descent became ascent.

That's the volcano's trap — you think you're descending, you descend, and then the tunnel turns on itself like an animal baring its teeth, and the floor becomes wall, the wall becomes ceiling, and you have to climb to keep falling.

The fault opened before them like a vertical wound in the rock. Not wide — two meters, maybe three. Tall — tall enough that the light from the glow sticks didn't reach the top. And above all: deep. The fault didn't rise toward the surface. It descended at an angle, a twisted shaft, tilted at sixty degrees, its walls seeping hot water that smelled of iron and sulfur.

Thabo was the first to stop. His cloth, held at arm's length in front of the opening, no longer danced — it *sucked inward*. The fibers pressed flat against his hand as if glued by an invisible force. The air current was coming from below. Powerful. Steady. Hot.

He turned to Senzo. Hand flat, palm facing the ground, then an upward motion — the inverted gesture. *The air rises. It goes down.*

Senzo approached the edge. Looked. The greenish light of the glow stick only lit the first few meters — rock holds, basalt ledges, cracks where fingers could fit. After that: black. Black that descended.

Inyoni was already on her knees. Her hands traveled the wall like a blind person reading a page. She touched every bump, every hollow, every lip of rock. Her fingers were counting. Not the holds — the distances between the holds. The intervals. The grammar of the descent.

She stood up. Uncoiled the rope. Fifty meters of braided fiber, thick as a thumb, coated in resin against moisture. She tied a knot — a knot only she knew how to tie, an eight-twist knot that tightened under load and never gave, never ever gave, like the straps, like the ropes, like everything Inyoni touched.

The Contact was about to change.

Nandi felt it before the others — not in her feet this time, but in her hands. The rope was a medium. A channel. When seven bodies clung to it, it transmitted everything: weight, movement, fear. But also — and this is where the Contact reinvented itself — intentional vibrations. Deliberate shakes. Messages.

Inyoni set the first anchor. A copper piton hammered into the rock — the sound resonated through the fault like a metallic heartbeat. The rope passed through it. The carabiner snapped shut.

Senzo turned to the team. Six faces in the green light. He raised his hand. Fist closed, then open — but this time, the fingers didn't spread upward. They went down. The same departure gesture, inverted by gravity.

*We descend.*

Inyoni went first. This was her world — the vertical. Her hands on the rope, her feet on the rock, her body between the two, fluid, precise, like a word articulated without hesitation. She disappeared into the fault in three movements. The rope vibrated under her descent — a steady, confident vibration that told the others: *It holds. Come.*

Senzo went second. Then Nandi, bare feet against the wet rock, toes searching for holds like fingers searching for words. Then Enama. Thabo. Jabu.

Sihle went last. His seismograph beat against his chest, the curves drawing sawtooth lines with each impact. He didn't like the rope. The rope didn't give data. The rope didn't measure anything. The rope only connected bodies falling together.

The fault swallowed them.

And for the first time since departure, they couldn't see each other. The darkness between bodies, the rock between hands, the distance between rope knots — everything separated. Direct-touch Contact was dead. The rope remained.

The rope, and what it could say.

## Scene 2 — Senzo chooses the route

The fault branched.

After two hours of descent — or what the muscles called two hours, that pain in the forearms that corresponded to roughly seven hundred meters of elevation change — the left wall opened up. A horizontal gallery, wide, its floor appearing dry. The fault continued below, narrower, hotter, water seeping from the walls in threads that gleamed in the glow stick's light.

Inyoni planted a piton. The team regrouped on a ledge — a basalt shelf wide enough for six, not for seven. Jabu stayed suspended, feet wedged in a crack, hands on the rope. He didn't complain. Jabu never complained.

Senzo unrolled the map. A surface gesture — the map had been drawn for horizontal tunnels, not for shafts. The lines didn't know how to go down. But something resembled what they were seeing: a junction, marked with a cross, with two words written by a dead hand. *Stable gallery* to the left. Nothing below. Nothing, because those who had drawn the map hadn't gone down.

Sihle placed his hand on Senzo's arm. Technical Contact. Two fingers on the forearm, measured pressure. He pointed to the left gallery. His seismograph confirmed: seismic activity was lower there. Fewer frequencies. Fewer risks. The gallery was stable — the readings said so, the map said so, logic said so.

Enama crouched on the ledge. Placed her hands on the rock. Closed her eyes. The ritual had become familiar — Enama listening to the ground, Sihle reading his curves, and the world between the two refusing to be summarized.

She reopened her eyes. Grabbed Senzo's wrist. Squeezed. Hard. Then she pointed down. The shaft. The unknown.

Sihle didn't move. He didn't need to move. His grip on Senzo's arm was still there, two fingers still resting, the pressure still technical. *The data says no.*

Senzo looked at the gallery. Looked at the shaft. Looked at the map that didn't know how to go down.

Then he did what Senzo always did. He chose neither one nor the other. He chose a third path — a crack in the opposite wall, narrow, nearly invisible, that nobody had noticed because it was in the gallery's shadow. Not on the map. Not in Sihle's data. Not in Enama's hands.

He pointed to the crack. Hand extended, palm facing up. The gesture of someone who says: *That way.*

Sihle withdrew his fingers from Senzo's forearm. A slow withdrawal. Deliberate. The Contact of someone who disagrees but won't argue. Not here. Not in the void. Not when the only thing anchoring them to the world was a fifty-meter rope.

Enama looked at the crack. Placed her hand on the ground one last time. Something passed in her eyes — not resistance. Resignation. Her body had said nothing about this crack. Her body had felt nothing.

Senzo began to cross. The crack was narrow — the width of a body. He inserted himself sideways, the pack crushed against the wall, hands flat on either side. The rock was hot. Wet. It vibrated under his palms — magnitude 4, constant, the heartbeat of the world.

The team followed. One by one. Through the crack the leader had chosen. The crack no one had seen.

The crack the volcano had left open.

## Scene 3 — Rope-Contact

In the crack, the Contact died. Then it was born elsewhere.

Impossible to touch each other — arms pinned to the body, shoulders pressed against rock on both sides, feet advancing centimeter by centimeter. The space between the walls left room only for the body. Not for gestures. Not for Contact words. Nothing.

And the noise. The noise of the fault — a permanent rumble, amplified by the narrow walls, filling the air like water fills a glass. The sound of rock working, contracting, expanding, grinding against itself like teeth clenching. A voice — if anyone had dared to speak — would have been swallowed.

The rope remained.

It ran between them like a nerve. Each body was attached to it by harness, spaced seven meters apart, and when one moved, the other six felt it. Not the movement — the vibration. The rope transmitted the body's intentions before the body carried them out. A foot searching for a hold: the rope quivers. An arm pressing against the wall: the rope undulates. A breath that quickens — even that, the rope carried.

Inyoni, in the lead, discovered it first. She stopped in front of an even narrower passage — the width of a rib cage — and she hesitated. A fraction of a second. Her body, accustomed to never hesitating, suspended its movement, and the rope transmitted that suspension to the six bodies behind her. Not a word. Not a gesture. Just a silence in the vibration.

And everyone understood: *Stop.*

Inyoni gripped the rope with both hands. Shook it three times. Short, short, long. A code invented in the moment — not old Contact, not surface Contact, not even Contact from the deaf village. Rope-Contact. A language born in the last ten minutes, invented by necessity, by the impossibility of doing otherwise.

Three shakes. *Stop.* Or *danger.* Or both.

Behind her, Senzo received the message. His hands on the rope felt the vibrations and something in him — not reason, not instinct, something between the two, the muscle of the leader — translated. He sent back: two shakes. Short. *Understood.*

The code was building itself. Shake by shake, the rope invented its vocabulary. One long shake: *Advance.* Two short: *OK.* Three: *Stop.* A rapid, nervous series: *Problem.* A total slackening, the rope going limp: *Rest.*

But the rope couldn't say everything. It couldn't say *I'm afraid*. It couldn't say *the air is changing*. It couldn't say *listen to the ground*. The rope was a language of survival, not a language of thought. Surface Contact had the nape for intimacy, the shoulder for urgency, the wrist for data. The rope had only tension and movement. Two dimensions where Contact had twenty.

Nandi, third on the rope, felt the difference. Her hands — the predictor's hands, the hands the old woman of the village had filled with an unknown word — touched the rope and didn't find what they were looking for. The rope didn't tremble like the earth. The rope didn't carry deep frequencies. The rope spoke only of bodies.

Not of the world.

And it was in this absence — in the silence between what the rope said and what the earth wanted to say — that Nandi felt the first quake of the fault.

Not through her feet. Not through the rope. Through her bones. A deep shiver, beyond tremens, that came not from the surface but from the interior of the rock itself. The fault was breathing. And its breathing was accelerating.

She couldn't send anything through the rope to say it. The vocabulary didn't exist yet.

## Scene 4 — Quakes in the wall

The first quake struck when they were midway through the crack.

Midway up or midway down — the distinction had lost its meaning. The crack descended and ascended simultaneously, a shaft twisted like a stone intestine, and the bodies crossing it no longer knew where up was. The only certainty: the rope. The rope that connected. The rope that knew.

The shock came from the right.

Not from below — from the right. The fault had its own geology, its own fracture lines, and seismic waves didn't travel vertically here. They traveled *laterally*, bouncing between the walls like bullets in a corridor. The two walls closed in by a centimeter — then spread apart by two. Inyoni's body, wedged between the walls, was compressed then released like a beating heart. She didn't scream. Her hands gripped the rope and she sent the signal — three shakes, fast, brutal. *Stop.*

The message traveled down the rope. Body by body. Senzo received it. Passed it on. Nandi. Enama. Thabo. Jabu. Sihle last.

Then the wall struck again.

Magnitude 5. Maybe 6. In the crack, the distinction didn't matter — a magnitude 5 in a space sixty centimeters wide was a world closing in on you. The walls oscillated. Rock fragments fell from above, fist-sized chunks that ricocheted between the walls before vanishing into the black below. The noise became physical — not a sound but a pressure, a shockwave in the air that compressed the lungs.

The rope transmitted everything.

Inyoni's weight pressing against the wall. Senzo's grip tightening. Jabu's panic — a series of involuntary shakes, not vocabulary, just a body trembling that the rope couldn't distinguish from a message. The rope carried meaning and noise. Signal and interference. And in that chaos, someone had to read.

Inyoni read.

Her hands on the rope filtered. She felt the vibrations of the quake — deep, regular, mechanical — and separated them from the vibrations of the bodies — irregular, organic, human. One came from the rock. The other came from the gut. The climber knew how to tell the difference. Not by training. By vocation. Her fingers had always known what the rope carried, the way Nandi's fingers had always known what the ground said.

She sent a message. Not three shakes — a new pattern. Long, short, long, pause, short, short. A complex message, the longest since the beginning of Rope-Contact. The bodies below received it and didn't understand right away.

Senzo understood. He transmitted it to Nandi through the rope, adding his own interpretation — two shakes after the pattern, an addendum that said: *Do it.* Nandi touched the rope and knew: Inyoni wanted them to advance. During the quake. Not after — during. Because the rock between shocks was more unstable than at the moment of impact. The fractures propagated in the silence after, not in the noise during.

The climber was right. Nandi felt it in her bones — the same truth, translated by another body.

They advanced. While the world struck the walls, while the rock compressed them and released them and compressed them again, they crawled through the crack. The rope vibrated with everything — quakes, bodies, messages — and in that everything, those who knew how to read read, and the others trusted.

Sihle, in last position, read nothing in the rope. But his seismograph, pressed against his chest, was drawing curves no one saw in the dark. Curves that said what the rope didn't say: the crack was moving. Not just vibrating — moving. Shifting laterally, millimeter by millimeter, like a jaw closing.

He couldn't tell anyone.

## Scene 5 — Senzo

The crack opened onto a landing.

Not a ledge — a real landing, five meters wide, carved into the rock as if the volcano had wanted to offer a reprieve. The blue filaments had returned — rare, scattered, but present, pulsing faintly in the darkness. And before the landing, the fault resumed. Wider now. More open. The descent became again what it had always been: downward.

The team was regrouping. Inyoni was already sitting, the ropes around her like a nest, her hands checking the knots. Nandi had collapsed against the wall, her feet bleeding — the rock in the crack had bitten her soles —, her hands on her stomach. The tremens was punishing her. The seven-hundred-meter descent through new frequencies had shaken her body like a detuned instrument.

Senzo approached her. Not fast. Not slow. The exact speed. He placed his hand on her nape. The long pressure. Warm. The one that had no name in any register of Contact except theirs.

Nandi looked up. Smiled. A tremens smile — lips rising despite the body, will slipping between the spasms.

Senzo straightened up. Looked at what lay ahead. Three routes. Three passages descending from the landing — one to the left, one in the center, one to the right. The fault was branching again, dividing like a root network, each branch plunging toward its own version of the bottom.

He took out the map. The lines said nothing. After the cross of the previous junction, the map was blank. Terra incognita.

Sihle stepped forward. His seismograph was telling a story no one else could read: the left passage vibrated strongly, high and nervous frequencies. The center vibrated little — too little, abnormally little, like a heart skipping beats. The right vibrated steadily — magnitude 4, constant, the pulse of the world.

He placed his hand on Senzo's arm. Pointed right. The gesture had become ritual, almost automatic. *My data says right.*

Enama didn't crouch this time. She stayed standing. Looked at the three passages. Didn't touch the ground. Not because she didn't need to — because something in her body had changed since the crack. The compression between the walls, the Rope-Contact that carried nothing of the world, the obligation to trust something other than her hands. She didn't know what her hands were saying about these three passages. She no longer knew.

She pointed at nothing. For the first time, Enama had no opinion.

Senzo looked at her. Looked at Sihle. Looked at the three passages.

He chose the center.

Sihle withdrew his hand. Not slowly this time — abruptly. The withdrawal of technical Contact when data is ignored. But he followed. They all followed.

The central passage was wide. Almost comfortable — smooth rock, gently sloping floor, blue filaments lining the walls like guides. Too comfortable. Nandi walked barefoot on the smooth floor and her toes found nothing. No micro-vibrations. No signals. The floor was silent. Not like the village floor that trembled *with* — a different silence. A silence of absence.

A silence of trap.

She stopped. Grabbed the rope still linking them. Shook it. Three shakes. *Stop.*

Senzo turned around. Hand on Nandi's shoulder — the question.

She knelt. Placed both hands flat on the floor. Closed her eyes. Searched. Beneath the silence, beneath the absence, beneath the nothing — something. No. Nothing. The floor wasn't speaking. The floor wasn't speaking anymore. As if the rock here had been severed from its seismic roots, isolated from the network of vibrations that irrigated the world.

Her hands searched deeper. Further. Beyond the floor, beyond the rock, into that place beneath the frequencies where the tremens sometimes went when it found nothing left to translate.

And there, at the very bottom, she felt.

The floor wasn't silent. The floor was *held back*. Like a blocked breath. Like a scream rising in the throat that doesn't come out. The rock was accumulating. For hours, days, weeks maybe — she didn't know. The rock was accumulating tension without releasing it. No micro-quakes. No background tremors. No pulse. Because all the energy was concentrating. Compressing. Waiting.

She reopened her eyes. Grabbed Senzo's wrist. Squeezed hard. Too hard for vocabulary. Then she made the gesture — the old Contact gesture, the one Senzo had invented at the beginning of the expedition for situations that had no word:

Open palm. Five fingers spread. Hand closing slowly, finger by finger, into a fist. *It's tightening.*

Senzo understood. He always understood Nandi — not because he felt the ground, but because he felt Nandi. He raised his fist. Stop signal. Then he stepped back one pace, two paces, searched for the wall with his eyes —

The ground gave way.

Not a quake. A slide. The difference was between a punch and a knife — the quake strikes and releases, the slide cuts and doesn't stop. The ground under their feet — that smooth ground, that too-silent ground — began to move. Not downward. To the right. The entire rock was sliding, a basalt slab three meters thick detaching from the mountain like dead skin.

The team fell. Not all at once — in cascade. Nandi first, carried by the ground opening beneath her bare feet. Then Enama who caught her — grip on the wrist, survival grip, not vocabulary. Then Thabo, Jabu, Sihle — the rope linked them all in the fall, pulled them toward each other like a net closing.

Senzo didn't fall.

Senzo saw.

He saw the ground separate — the rock slab sliding, the cracks opening like mouths, the black beneath. He saw Nandi fall. He saw Enama grab. He saw the rope go taut between him and the six bodies below.

And he saw the wall.

Above the spot where the slab had detached, the gallery wall was vibrating. Not background vibration — local, specific vibration, the kind of vibration Sihle would have identified in a second if his seismograph hadn't been pressed against his chest in the fall. The wall was going to follow. The wall was going to fall. Tons of basalt, suspended above the open fault, waiting only for the next shock to collapse onto the six bodies below.

Senzo planted his feet. Pulled the rope. His arms — the arms of the leader, the arms that placed hands on shoulders, that touched Nandi's nape, that raised the fist to say *let's go* — his arms pulled. Hard. Hard enough for the six bodies to slide toward him, toward the stable edge of the landing.

Enama came up first. Then Nandi. Then the others — one by one, gripping the rope, feet against the still-moving rock. Senzo held. The veins in his forearms stood out like the blue filaments on the walls. His hands didn't let go. His hands never let go.

Sihle passed the edge. Last. He rolled onto the stable landing and released the rope. He looked up at Senzo —

The wall fell.

No warning. Not the micro-silence Nandi usually felt. The rock simply broke away — an entire section of the wall, ten meters tall, three meters thick, tilting inward into the gallery like a felled tree.

Senzo was between the team and the wall. He had pulled the rope from the edge of the landing. The edge that ran along the wall. The edge where the wall was falling.

He had time to look at Nandi. No gesture. No Contact. No tactile word for what was happening. Just his eyes — the eyes of the leader, the cartographer, the man who chose the route and who had chosen this one — finding Nandi's eyes in the chaos of dust and rock.

The rope went taut. Taut to the limit — the full length between Senzo and Inyoni, the first anchor, vibrating like a harp string. The rope carried one last message. Not three shakes. Not a code. Just a total, absolute tension, the weight of a body holding on to nothing else.

Then the rope went slack.

All at once. Like a breath leaving. Like a word stopping in the middle.

The dust took a long time to settle. Minutes. When it did, the central passage no longer existed. A wall of fresh rock — basalt ripped from the wall, piled like the rubble of a house no one had lived in — blocked the gallery from wall to wall.

On the other side of the wall: nothing. No sound. No knocking against the rock. No vibration.

Inyoni pulled the rope. The rope came. Light. Without weight. The end was clean — not cut. Torn. The fibers splayed open like fingers spreading apart.

Six people on the landing.

The rope in Inyoni's hands.

And the silence. Not the silence of the world — the silence of the rope. The silence of a voice that has just gone out.

## Scene 6 — The first mourning

Nobody moved.

Not from shock — the shock would come later. From incomprehension. The Contact had no word for this. Six people knew how to say *Stop*, *Advance*, *Danger*, *Listen*, *We don't go back up*. Six people knew how to touch an arm, a shoulder, a wrist, a nape. Six people spoke a language of touch that covered survival, information, emotion, intimacy.

None of those words covered absence.

Nandi was the first to try. Her hands — the hands full of the old woman's word, the hands that read the ground, the hands Senzo had held — her hands placed themselves on the wall of rubble. Not to clear it. Not to search. To touch. The last place Senzo had been. The rock that had taken him.

The rock didn't answer. The rock had never answered — Contact was a language between humans, not between humans and stone. But Nandi's hands stayed. A long time. As if they were waiting for the rock to learn.

Enama sat down. Her hands were trembling. Not from tremens — Enama had never had the tremens. Her hands trembled because they were searching for a gesture they couldn't find. She wanted to say something. Something for Senzo. Something large, solemn, final. And the Contact — that language she had defended against Sihle, that body-language she had brandished as truth — the Contact didn't have that word.

Thabo folded his cloth. A slow, methodical gesture, each fold over the last. He wasn't testing the air. He was folding because his hands needed to do something and folding was what he knew. When the cloth was folded — a perfect square, the fibers aligned — he unfolded it and started again.

Inyoni was counting. Her fingers passed and repassed over the rope — the rope whose end was missing a knot. She was counting the torn fibers. Forty-seven. She counted them again. Forty-seven. Again. Forty-seven. The number didn't change. The number was the only word she had.

Jabu did nothing. Standing at the edge of the landing, his gaze turned toward the left passage — the one Sihle had proposed, the one Senzo had refused — he did nothing. His hands hung at his sides. Empty. Contact can't be done alone. Contact needs a recipient. And Jabu, in that moment, had no one to send what he was carrying to.

Sihle put away his seismograph. Slow gesture. Almost ceremonial. He unrolled the paper, looked at the curves from the last few minutes — the sawtooth of the slide, the abrupt stop, the flat line. The flat line that matched the slack rope. Data and body, for once, said the same thing: it's over.

He rolled up the scroll. Buckled the harness. Then he sat down next to Enama without touching her.

The silence lasted.

Not the silence of the world — the world was still rumbling, magnitude 4, constant, faithful to its indifference. The silence of the group. The silence of six people discovering a hole in their language. A hole the exact shape of Senzo.

Because mourning, in the Contact, wasn't just the absence of a person. It was the absence of a vocabulary. The gestures that were Senzo's — his way of placing his hand on a shoulder, three slow pressures, from one side to the other; his fist opening to say *let's go*; his descending arc to say *we don't go back up*; his palm on Nandi's nape, long, warm, untranslatable — those gestures belonged only to him. The same movements, made by another, wouldn't say the same thing. Senzo's idiolect had died with Senzo.

And with his idiolect, something else. The expedition leader had fallen. The cartographer. The route-decider. The man who looked at a point and walked toward it. The man who chose when the others couldn't.

Who would choose now?

Nandi pulled her hands from the wall. Turned around. Looked at the five faces. Five. Not six. The number had changed and the world hadn't changed — the magnitude still vibrated, the blue filaments still pulsed, the air still blew from below upward.

She approached Inyoni. Placed her hand on her shoulder. One pressure. Long.

Inyoni looked up. Forty-seven fibers in her head. She placed her hand on Nandi's shoulder. Same gesture. Same pressure.

Enama stood up. Touched Thabo's shoulder. Thabo touched Jabu's shoulder. Jabu touched Sihle's shoulder. Sihle — Sihle hesitated. His hands, accustomed to instruments, unaccustomed to voluntary Contact, placed themselves on Nandi's shoulder.

The circle closed.

Six shoulders. Six hands. Not a word. The simplest Contact — the one that says nothing other than *I'm here, I'm touching you, we exist in the same world.*

The gesture Senzo had made to the old woman of the village. The first gesture. The only one remaining when all the others had lost their speaker.

They stayed like that. The ground vibrated. The filaments pulsed. Air rose from the depths.

Then Nandi did something no one expected. She raised her hand. Open palm. Descending arc.

*We don't go back up.*

Senzo's gesture. In Nandi's hands. Not the same — thinner fingers, slower movement, wider arc. But the word. Senzo's word, spoken by another mouth. A word that didn't belong to Nandi but that she took anyway. A borrowing. An inheritance. A necessary theft.

The Contact wasn't dead. The Contact had lost a speaker. But the language — the language goes on. Words pass from hand to hand. Gestures survive those who invent them, the way vibrations survive the quakes that launch them.

The team stood up. Not together — one by one. Inyoni first, retying the rope. Then Thabo, who unfurled his cloth. Then the others.

Six people on a basalt landing, before a fault that descended.

And behind them, beneath tons of rock, a body that no longer trembled. The only body in all this world that, at last, no longer trembled.

Nandi placed a bare foot on the edge of the left passage. The ground vibrated. Magnitude 4. The pulse returned. The earth was still speaking — it had always spoken.

The team descended.

Without map. Without leader. Without Senzo.

With the Contact.

And the Contact had one fewer word.
