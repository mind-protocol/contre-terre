# V — The Deep Caverns

## Scene 1 — The air thins

The air changed before the rock.

Nandi felt it in her lungs before feeling it in her feet — a scarcity, a thinness, as if each breath gave a little less than the last. The body compensated. The heart beat faster. The temples pulsed. The world demanded more and gave less.

Thabo stopped.

His cloth hung between his fingers as it always hung, blackened, worn, carrying the breath of the deaf village for what could have been three days or three weeks — time had no grip here, in the caverns where nothing marked the hours but the rhythm of quakes and the wearing of bodies. Thabo raised his arm. The fibers moved. Less than before. Much less. On the surface, the cloth danced. In the rift, it inhaled. Here, in the deep caverns, it barely floated — a hesitation in the air, as if the wind itself no longer knew which direction to blow.

He turned toward the team. Five faces in the red glow of filaments. Not blue. Red. The bioluminescence had changed color since the entrance to the caverns — the filaments lining the walls were no longer the regular, bluish threads of the village, maintained, trimmed, domesticated. These grew as they could, short, thick, a brick red that pulsed at a rhythm Nandi didn't recognize. Not the background magnitude. Another rhythm. Deeper. Slower. Like the second heartbeat she'd been feeling beneath the first since departure — the magnitude 11, rising.

Thabo made the sign. Hand flat, palm down, lateral motion. *The air circulates, we can go.* But the movement was slower than usual. More hesitant. His fingers hung suspended for a fraction of a second, as if searching for a word the gesture didn't contain.

*We can go. But.*

Thabo's Contact had no "but." The air is good or the air is not good. The cloth dances or doesn't dance. There was no sign for "the air is sufficient but it won't be for much longer." So Thabo made the simple sign. And kept his brow furrowed.

Nandi understood. She always understood the gestures others didn't finish — her tremens gave her that, this reading ability that Sihle despised and Enama envied. She felt in the pressure of her temples, in the accelerated beating of her heart, in the slight nausea that hadn't left her since the rift, she felt what Thabo's cloth couldn't say: the oxygen was dropping. Not fast. Not in freefall. In a gentle slope. The gentle slope that doesn't trigger an alert but that, in the end, brings you to your knees.

The network of caverns opened before them like a digestive system — tunnels that branched, crossed, devoured each other. The ceiling oscillated between two meters and twenty. The floor was volcanic rock cooled in waves, in tongues, in ridges that looked like burned skin, frozen at the moment of burning. Nandi walked barefoot on it. Forty-five degrees. Hot. Not yet painful. Her desert calluses held — three weeks of burning sand had hardened the soles of her feet like supple leather. But she felt the promise beneath the heat. The lava was not far. The lava rose as the earth rose, as everything rose toward them while they descended toward it.

Sihle stopped in front of a partially collapsed passage. Blocks of basalt had detached from the ceiling — a recent collapse, dust still suspended in the thin air, edges sharp. He placed his hand on the rock. Not the way Enama placed her hand — not to listen. To read. His fingers followed the cracks, the stress lines, the light veins in the black basalt. The miner in him saw what the others didn't: the structure. The forces. The points where the rock still held and those where it had stopped holding.

He withdrew his hand. Turned toward Inyoni. Two fingers on her forearm — the technical Contact, cold, measured. Then he pointed to a block suspended above the passage and made a motion: flat hand descending. *That block is going to fall.*

Inyoni approached. Her hands found the holds around the block — not to touch it, to assess it. How much it weighed. How it held. What needed to be moved so the passage would open without the ceiling following. Her fingers counted the cracks. Four. Her hands searched for the leverage point. Found it. Pushed.

The block slid. Not all at once — pivoting, like a tooth being pulled. The rock groaned. Dust rose. The passage opened.

Inyoni stepped back. Counted the cracks in the ceiling around the hole. Five. One more than before. Her lips moved without sound — *holds* — and she passed under the cleared passage, shoulders tucked, hands on the walls, measuring the width in arm span. Enough. Just enough.

Sihle took out his seismograph while the others passed one by one. The paper unrolled its curves. The sawtooth patterns were tighter than on the surface — the micro-quakes here were constant, close together, a background vibration that turned the lines into an almost continuous band of small peaks. He noted in the margin of the roll. Numbers. Frequencies. Intervals between peaks.

Then he measured the atmospheric pressure. The seismograph's auxiliary instrument had a barometer — an addition Sihle had made himself, because Sihle always added instruments to instruments, as if the world could only be understood by measuring it from every angle at once. He noted the number. Compared it to the previous number — the one he'd taken at the cavern entrance. Frowned.

The meteorologist in him — that profession he had almost never shown the team, because meteorology underground seemed an absurd concept, and Sihle didn't like showing what couldn't be proven — the meteorologist calculated. The pressure gradient. The rate of thinning. The number of hours before the threshold.

He sought Thabo's gaze.

Thabo, on the other side of the cleared passage, held his cloth before a lateral crack from which a thread of warm air rose. The Airmaster and the Meteorologist. Two professions that had never crossed — Thabo read the air with his body, Sihle with numbers, and their languages were as different as Contact and voice.

Sihle placed two fingers on Thabo's forearm. Pressed. Showed the seismograph. Showed the number written in the margin. Two fingers that said: *the data confirms.*

Thabo looked at the numbers. He didn't understand the numbers — not those, not in that notation. But he understood the direction. Sihle's gesture pointed downward. The cloth pointed downward. Data and body, for once, for the only time, agreed.

The air was dying.

Thabo nodded. A gesture that was not Contact — a gesture from before Contact, a surface gesture, a nod that meant *I know* without touching anyone. He put the cloth in his pocket. Not fast, not abruptly. With the care of someone putting away something precious. The cloth carried the breath of the village. The air from below. The air he was going to have to learn to read in its final hours.

Enama, behind them, had her hands on the ground. Eyes closed. She wasn't searching for air — she was searching for the route. The earth spoke beneath her palms. Something in the vibrations had changed since the rift. Something deeper, slower, more insistent. As if the rock itself was pushing in a direction. As if the volcano was inhaling.

She opened her eyes. Met Nandi's gaze. Not Contact. Not touch. A look. That ancient, nearly useless thing that Contact had replaced.

Nandi saw what Enama's hands couldn't say.

The earth wanted them.

---

Nandi vomited.

Not the first time — she had lost count. The tremens no longer left her. Since the entrance to the caverns, her body vibrated at a frequency that corresponded to nothing it knew. The desert had its long, deep waves, the foothills their sharp snaps, the village its dampening resonances. But here, in the deep caverns, the frequencies were low. Very low. Lower than anything the human body should hear through the soles of its feet.

Her stomach turned with every shift. Her hands trembled — not the trembling of Contact, not the voluntary trembling the old woman of the village had transmitted to her. A suffered trembling. The body protesting against a world that didn't suit it.

Jabu approached. Silent. He crouched beside her. Set a gourd within reach of her hand. No Contact — he didn't touch her. He placed the object and stepped back. The water would be there when she needed it.

Nandi spat. Wiped her mouth. Took the gourd. The water was warm — everything was warm here, the air, the rock, the water in the gourds heating by conduction. She drank. Set the gourd down. Noticed it was fuller than the others — Jabu had filled his more than necessary. Ocean specialist reflex. Water, you never have too much. You never have enough.

She didn't thank him. He didn't expect it. Jabu never expected anything — he did, and the world turned around him without noticing.

## Scene 2 — The tremens at its peak

The quake arrived through Nandi's feet.

Not like the others — not like the background magnitudes, those constant vibrations the body ended up ignoring like the noise of an engine. This one arrived like a new word. A frequency her feet didn't know. Lower than low. So low that sound no longer existed — only movement remained, an oscillation in the ankle bones, in the tibias, that climbed through the knees and exploded in the pelvis like a shock wave in slow motion.

Nandi seized Enama's wrist.

The calibration grip. Their gesture. The gesture of their pair, belonging only to the two of them — Enama gripping Nandi's wrist when the tremens exceeded the bearable, Nandi gripping Enama's wrist when the signal became too strong for one body. The grip was not a message. It was a sharing. Nandi's fingers squeezed Enama's wrist and something passed — not words, not vocabulary, something from before vocabulary. The frequency. The raw signal. Nandi's body shared what it received, and Enama's body absorbed part of the load.

Enama felt it. Her eyes widened. Her feet — shod, hers, her feet didn't read like Nandi's — her feet felt it anyway. Because the grip had transmitted.

She turned toward Sihle. Full grip, five fingers, forearm. *Now.*

Sihle looked up from his seismograph. The curves showed nothing abnormal — the usual sawtooth patterns, the continuous band of micro-quakes. He looked at the paper. Looked at Enama. The conflict didn't need words. His face said: *The data does not confirm.*

Twenty seconds.

The seismograph jumped.

The continuous band of small peaks broke all at once — a giant sawtooth, massive, that crossed the entire roll and made the instrument vibrate in Sihle's harness. Magnitude 7.5. Or what the numbers called 7.5 — the numbers were late.

The ground moved.

Not trembling. *Movement*. The rock beneath their feet undulated like living skin, a telluric spasm that sent everyone to the ground. Inyoni grabbed the wall — her hands found the holds by reflex, as if the rock were a rope and the rope were her home. Thabo pressed flat on his stomach, the cloth beneath him, hands on the ground, measuring the vibrations through his entire body. Jabu didn't move — he was already crouching, back against the wall, hands flat on either side, body coiled. Enama let herself fall, palms to the ground, eyes closed — listening. Listening even in the quake. Especially in the quake. Enama's body didn't panic during earthquakes. It opened.

Sihle, standing, legs apart, hands on his seismograph, read the curves live. His eyes went from paper to rock, paper to rock. The numbers climbed. 7.5. 7.8. The sawtooth patterns overlapped. The instrument vibrated so hard the curves became unreadable — the seismograph's needle could no longer follow the ground's motion.

Eight seconds.

The quake passed.

The rock stabilized. Not calm — the background magnitude returned, 5.5, higher than before, more nervous. Dust fell from the ceiling. A red filament detached from the wall and fell to the ground, pulsing a few more seconds before going dark.

Nandi was panting. Enama's grip on her wrist hadn't let go. The two women, on the ground, connected by their hands, the signal still between them.

Sihle noted the delay.

Twenty seconds. He noted it in the margin of the roll, next to the magnitude. A number and a fact: Nandi's body had detected the quake twenty seconds before the most sensitive instrument of the expedition. Twenty seconds. Sihle noted the number. He did not note what it meant. What it meant would have required a word he didn't possess — the word for "the body was right and the instrument was wrong." That word didn't exist in Sihle's vocabulary. Which is why he only noted the number.

But Nandi saw.

She saw his two fingers inscribe the 20 in the margin of the roll. She saw his face — the tension, the clenching jaw, the eyes that came back to her then looked away. Sihle withdrew his hand from Nandi's forearm — his hand that had been there without him realizing, placed by reflex during the quake, two fingers on the forearm, involuntary technical Contact. He withdrew his hand faster than usual. As if the Contact burned.

The handicap was the gift. Sihle didn't want to admit it. And in twenty seconds or in twenty hours, that inability to admit would cost a life.

Not his.

## Scene 3 — The choice of route

The fork awaited them like a trap without jaws.

Two passages. On the left: a wide gallery whose floor descended in a gentle slope toward a sound of water. Not a trickle — a current. The sound rolled through the tunnel, amplified by the walls, transformed into a deep rumbling that mixed with the background vibrations. The air there was humid. Nandi felt the vapor on her cheeks, that moisture the desert didn't know.

On the right: a dry passage. Narrow. Volcanic rock floor, regular, stable. The air there was drier, hotter. The passage rose slightly before descending again — a rock ridge that would need to be climbed over before finding the slope again.

Two passages. Two directions. And no one left to choose.

Sihle was the first to move. He advanced toward the right passage. His seismograph was already measuring — one hand on the instrument, the other on the wall. The curves in the dry passage were more regular, the sawtooth patterns more spaced. Less seismic activity. More structural stability. The basalt here was compact, cracks rare, stress lines parallel — the rock held. The miner in Sihle saw it. The meteorologist in Sihle confirmed: the airflow in the dry passage was regular, constant, predictable. The data said right.

He turned toward the team. Two fingers on Thabo's forearm. *The data says.*

Thabo extended his cloth before the dry passage. The fibers moved — regularly, laterally. The air circulated. Thabo confirmed Sihle's sign with his own: hand flat, lateral motion. *The air circulates.*

Enama crouched.

Her hands settled on the ground, midway between the two passages. Palms open. Fingers spread. Eyes closed. The ritual. The same ritual as before every fork since chapter II — Enama who listens to the ground, Sihle who reads his curves, and the world between them that won't let itself be summarized.

The silence lasted. Six people motionless in the red glow of filaments. The rumbling of water on the left. The dry breath of air on the right. And in the middle, Enama, her hands placed on the decision.

She opened her eyes.

Grabbed Inyoni's wrist. Firm grip — not Sihle's technical grip, Enama's full grip, the one that seized the way you seize a certainty. She pointed to the left passage. The water.

*That way.*

Sihle didn't move. His two fingers stayed on Thabo's forearm. He looked at Enama. The seismograph in his harness vibrated with the background magnitude, 5.5, the little sawtooth patterns that said: the dry passage is safe. The data would not change because a woman had placed her hands on the ground and closed her eyes.

Nobody spoke. Nobody ever spoke — the voice was a waste, a transgression, a confession. But the silence here had a shape Nandi recognized. The same shape as in chapter II, before the Y-fork, when Sihle had pointed right and Enama left and Senzo had had to choose.

Senzo.

Nandi raised her hand. Palm open. Descending arc. *We don't go back up.* Senzo's gesture in Nandi's hands. But the gesture didn't say *left* or *right*. It didn't say *water* or *rock*. It said: *whatever the direction, we continue.* The chief's gesture had become a gesture of encouragement. Not of decision. The difference between the two, Nandi felt it in her own fingers — the thinner fingers, the slower movement, the wider arc. The same word, spoken by a mouth that didn't have the authority of the original mouth.

The gesture didn't settle it. The gesture could no longer settle it. The chief was dead and the chief's gesture, in someone else's hands, no longer carried the weight of decision.

Jabu moved.

He rose from where he'd been crouching — always set back, always at the edge, always in the margin of the group as he had been in the margin of the cart. He advanced toward the left passage. Crouched at the water's edge.

The underground river was black. Not black from dirt — black from depth. The water was clear but the bottom didn't exist, or so far below the surface that the red filament light couldn't reach it. The current was visible: regular eddies, a lateral push from right to left, a speed Nandi estimated at one or two meters per second. Strong. But not violent.

Jabu plunged his hand in.

His fingers entered the water as into a language. The current pressed against his palm — a constant, measurable pressure that his body knew. Jabu was an ocean specialist. Water was his domain. Not the dormant water of gourds — living water, water that moves, water that has a direction and a will. His fingers spread in the current read the force, the temperature, the turbulence.

He withdrew his hand. Shook it. Drops fell on the hot rock and evaporated with a hiss.

Then he made a gesture no one had ever seen him make.

Hand open. Palm up. Lateral motion. Not Thabo's sign for air — a sign that resembled Thabo's but came from water. An invented sign. A sign that Jabu's Contact had never needed to produce because Jabu almost never spoke.

*The current is manageable.*

Six faces looked at him. Five, actually — Nandi was looking with her feet as much as with her eyes, because the vibrations of the water in the rock confirmed what Jabu's hand had just said. The current was strong but regular. No treacherous turbulence. No falls downstream. No rising tide — or not yet.

Jabu stood back up. His hands hung at his sides. Empty. The gesture was made. The word had been said. Jabu returned to his margin.

But Enama had seen.

She approached him. Slowly. Not Enama's step when she ran toward a fork, not the step of intuition — the step of someone who recognizes something. She placed her hand on Jabu's shoulder.

A pressure. Long.

*I trust you.*

The richest Contact Jabu had received since the start of the expedition. Perhaps the richest he had ever received. Jabu's shoulder, usually empty, usually in the off-frame of Contact the way Jabu was in the off-frame of the group, received Enama's pressure and didn't know what to do with it. He didn't respond. He had no word for that. His idiolect contained only *no need* and *the water is there* and the silence between the two.

Sihle watched. The seismograph vibrated in his harness. The data said right. Enama's body said left. Jabu said left. Nandi said nothing but her bare feet, placed on the rock between the two passages, her feet said what they always said — the truth of the frequency, indecipherable to anyone who didn't know how to read.

The conflict.

Enama turned toward Sihle. Opened her mouth.

"The water."

One word. Her voice. For the second time in the novel — the second time this mouth produced an articulated sound instead of Contact, the second time the air between two people carried something other than the rumbling of the world and the sound of footsteps. The first time was before the Y-fork, in chapter II. Senzo was there. Senzo had chosen.

Senzo was no longer there.

Sihle didn't respond with his voice. He responded with Contact — and the Contact he chose said everything: two fingers on Enama's forearm, brief pressure, withdrawal. The coldest Contact in his repertoire. Data against body. Instrument against hand. Two fingers withdrawing like a verdict.

*No.*

Enama didn't flinch. Her hand stayed on Jabu's shoulder. Her gaze on Sihle. Water rumbled in the left passage. Air blew in the right passage. And between the two, six people who didn't know how to decide because the decision had died in chapter IV, under tons of rock, with a man who was looking at a point and walking toward it.

## Scene 4 — The separation

The rope was the last word.

Inyoni cut it. Not with a sudden gesture — with a surgeon's gesture. She measured. One hundred forty meters since the rift, minus the sections cut after Senzo's death, minus the anchor knots. She counted. Her lips moved. The numbers scrolled like a prayer in reverse — not *holds, holds, holds* but *eighty-two, eighty-three, eighty-four*. She reached the end of her count. Placed the knife on the fiber. Looked at Sihle.

Sihle nodded. The gesture that was not Contact.

The fiber gave. Two sections. Inyoni handed the shorter one to Sihle — fifty-eight meters. Kept the longer — eighty-four. The imbalance was no accident. Four people needed more rope than two.

The group split. Not in violence. Not in shouts. In silence — that specific silence of Contact that can't find its words, that absence of touch which is the worst of touches because it says: I don't know how to touch you for what's coming.

Sihle took the right passage. He stowed his seismograph, adjusted his harness, knotted the rope section around his waist. His movements were methodical. Precise. The movements of someone who knows exactly what he's doing and why. The data said right. Sihle went right. The world was simple when you measured it.

Thabo followed him.

Not out of loyalty — out of necessity. The dry passage needed an Airmaster. The air there was hotter, drier, potentially toxic. Thabo went where the air needed him. He wasn't choosing the route — he was choosing the danger he knew how to read.

Before leaving, Thabo turned toward Inyoni. Their shoulders touched. The Contact of their pair — the passive Contact, shoulder against shoulder, the one that says nothing other than *I am here, you are here, we are side by side*. Not a farewell. Contact didn't do farewells. Contact said what was, not what was going to be.

Inyoni didn't hold him back. Her hands were busy — they were counting the remaining meters of rope, the knots to redo, the Charge straps to check. Thirty-two straps on the bags, twenty-four on the Charge. Her hands counted while her shoulder registered the pressure of Thabo's shoulder. The "test-and-holds" — Thabo tests the air, Inyoni tightens the strap. The dialogue of their pair. The last dialogue.

Inyoni's fingers tightened on the rope. *Holds.*

Enama, Nandi, Inyoni, Jabu took the left passage. The water.

Four against two. The body group against the data group. Nandi in the middle, bare feet in the water vapor, the tremens pounding in her temples. Inyoni behind, the Charge on her back, twenty-four straps, thirty-five kilos, the weight of the mission. Jabu in front, his hands hanging, his eyes reading the water the way Thabo read the air — without instrument, without measure, with what the body had always known. And Enama, beside Nandi, her hand still warm from Jabu's shoulder.

Nandi looked at the right passage. Sihle and Thabo were sinking into it. Thabo's cloth — a square of blackened fibers, the breath of the village, the air the dead had breathed — disappeared into the tunnel's darkness. Sihle's seismograph jolted in its harness, its curves recording the last vibrations before distance erased them.

Then the tunnel turned and swallowed them.

Six people became four and two. Contact split. Words divided like bodies.

Before the separation, a moment. A moment nobody noticed except Nandi, because Nandi always watched the hands.

Jabu had crossed Sihle's path at the moment of division. Their trajectories had grazed — Jabu going toward the water, Sihle toward the rock. And Jabu had made his gesture. The small gesture. The almost involuntary shoulder pressure, barely Contact, the most minimal movement in his repertoire — the gesture that meant *no need*.

Sihle hadn't responded. Sihle hadn't felt it. His hands were on the seismograph, his eyes on the curves, his body already in the right passage.

The last Contact between Jabu and Sihle was a non-Contact. A pressure addressed to someone who didn't receive it. The most discreet word of Contact sent into the void.

Nandi saw it. Kept it. Didn't yet know it was a farewell.

## Scene 5 — Death of Jabu

The water passage descended.

Not in a gentle slope — in steps. Ledges of volcanic rock, irregular, slippery, separated by drops of a meter or two that the water crossed in cascades. The underground river had nothing of a great river — it was an adolescent torrent, nervous, changeable, filling all available space and leaving nothing dry.

The water reached their ankles.

Jabu led.

For the first time in the expedition, Jabu led. His body had found its posture — shoulders straight, chin raised, hands no longer hanging but open, fingers in the current. He read the water the way Nandi read the ground. Flow rate by pressure on the palms. Temperature by the burn on the skin — the water here was warm, heated by the rock, thirty-five degrees perhaps. Eddies by vibrations in the wrists. The invisible bottom by the movement of the feet.

He stopped at each ledge. Plunged his hand. Measured. Then the gesture — the gesture he had invented, his only gesture, his only sentence in Contact: hand open, palm up, lateral motion. *The current is manageable.*

The group advanced in the black water.

Nandi walked barefoot in the current. Her toes found holds beneath the water — the volcanic rock had asperities, bumps, hollows that gave purchase if you knew how to search. The water was warm on her burning feet and for the first time in hours, the heat was not pain — it was relief. Warm water on desert calluses. A bath. Almost.

But beneath the water, beneath the warmth and the relief, her feet felt something else. The background tremor. The magnitude 5.5 passing through rock, through water, through her bones. And in the background tremor, something new. An arrhythmia. Like a heartbeat skipping one beat out of three. The frequency was changing — not rising, not falling. In *irregularity*.

Nandi said nothing. Not yet. The arrhythmia could be normal — the caverns were a complex system, frequencies bounced, overlapped, canceled out. Maybe the water was blurring the signal. Maybe her feet were lying.

Nandi's feet never lied.

The water rose to the knees.

Jabu stopped. Measured. The current had accelerated — one or two meters per second more. Not alarming. But the direction had changed. The current no longer pushed right to left — it pushed top to bottom. The water was descending faster than before.

Jabu made the gesture. Hand open, palm up, lateral motion. *The current is manageable.* But this time, the lateral movement had a jolt at the end — a hitch, a tremor. Not a tremor of the body. A tremor of the word. As if the gesture itself was no longer sure of what it said.

Enama placed her hand on Jabu's shoulder. The same pressure as at the fork. Long. *I trust you.*

Jabu advanced.

The water rose to the thighs.

Nandi felt the arrhythmia become a pattern. The skipped beat was no longer one in three — it was one in two. The frequency beneath her feet pounded: boom, silence, boom, silence, boom. The rock was accumulating something. Energy. Tension. Like an elastic being stretched — the rock was tensing and the water was rising as a consequence, because water goes where the rock tells it to go.

She seized Enama's wrist. The calibration grip. Hard. Urgent. Not the usual sharing — a warning.

Enama felt it. Her eyes sought Nandi. Nandi's hands were saying something Contact had no words to express. Not *danger* — the grip on the forearm says *danger*. Not *stop* — three pressures say *stop*. Something between the two. Something that resembled *it's going to change*.

The water rose to the waist.

Jabu stopped. The current was pushing him now — not hard, but constantly, a lateral pressure that demanded effort to stay standing. His hands in the water could no longer find the bottom — the river had deepened, the floor had given way to a liquid abyss, and Jabu's feet touched only the current.

He turned toward the group. His face, in the red light of the filaments, had an expression no one had ever seen on him. Not fear — Jabu didn't show fear. Concentration. The expression of the ocean specialist reading a current he doesn't understand. The water had changed. The water wasn't doing what it was supposed to do. The water was doing something else.

The quake was short.

Five seconds. Magnitude 6, maybe 6.5. Not a quake — a jolt, a spasm of rock, the kind of thing that didn't even deserve a name on the surface. But here, in a water passage, in a tunnel where the river filled the space between the walls, the spasm changed everything.

The rock contracted. The tunnel narrowed by a centimeter — invisible, imperceptible, but sufficient. The water no longer had room. The descending water found itself compressed in a reduced space and did what water always does when compressed: it accelerated.

The current doubled in an instant.

Jabu was hit first. Standing at the front, feet without purchase, body exposed, he took the mass of water like a horizontal punch. His hands reached for the wall — found it, gripped it, held. The water specialist knew water. He knew how to swim. He knew how to fight. His fingers in the rock, his feet searching for support, his body leaning into the current like a tree against the wind.

He held.

The second wave arrived twenty seconds later — the quake had opened a fissure upstream, somewhere in the depths of the rock where water had been accumulating for centuries, and this reserve was now pouring into the tunnel with the force of everything held back too long. The current didn't double. It quadrupled.

Jabu held the wall. His hands held. His feet had found an asperity — not a hold, a suggestion of a hold, a centimeter of rock protruding from the wall that supported the weight of a man and of the river pushing him.

Behind him, Enama had grabbed Nandi. Inyoni had grabbed the wall with one hand and the Charge with the other — the twenty-four straps taut, the case pulling, water infiltrating the joints. *Holds.* Her lips moved. *Holds.*

Jabu looked at the team over his shoulder. The current passed around his body like around a boulder — eddies, whirlpools, the black water rising and rising. He saw Enama, clinging to Nandi. He saw Inyoni, clinging to the Charge. He saw the passage ahead — the tunnel descending, the water plunging into it, the darkness swallowing everything.

And he knew water.

He knew this current. The flow rate, the pressure, the trajectory. His hands in the water had read the direction before the wave arrived — a lateral current, a secondary passage, a path the water took to the side of the main tunnel. A navigable path. A path the water specialist could take to reach the next ledge and extend the rope to the others.

Jabu let go of the wall.

He didn't let go by accident. He let go because he knew how to swim and he knew how to read the current and he knew — he *knew* — that the lateral passage was there, three meters, four maybe, and his body could reach it.

The water took him.

Not the way you take an object — the way you take a person. The water lifted him from the wall, spun him, carried him into the current with the same indifference as the ground carried everything in this world. The geological irony: the water specialist, carried away by water. The man who knew the current, swallowed by the current.

His hands swam. Nandi saw them — in the dying light of the red filaments, Jabu's hands cut through the water with the competence of someone for whom water was a language. No panic. No flailing. Precise, calculated movements, each stroke aimed at the lateral passage.

He reached it. One arm hooked the edge of the lateral fissure. His fingers found the hold. His body hoisted itself — halfway, waist out of the water, legs still in the current.

The third wave.

Bigger than the first two. The quake hadn't only opened a fissure upstream — it had destabilized an entire wall, and tons of water accumulated in a geological pocket were now pouring into the system. The rumbling filled the tunnel. The black water rose all at once — from belly to chest, from chest to neck.

Nandi felt in her feet the vibration change when Jabu's body gave way.

Not a sound. Not a cry — Jabu didn't cry out, Jabu didn't complain, Jabu never made a sound. His hands released the edge. The water carried him into the darkness. The red light of the filaments illuminated for an instant his fingers — spread, open, the same fingers that had measured the current ten minutes earlier — then the current turned and the filaments showed nothing but water.

Nandi closed her eyes.

Beneath her feet, the frequency had changed. A vibration was missing. A body struggling in water had a signal — a movement, a resistance, a weight fighting against the current that the rock transmitted. That signal had just disappeared. Jabu's body was no longer fighting. The water no longer met an obstacle.

The silence of water after the tumult.

Nandi reopened her eyes. The water was receding. Slowly, like a sigh. The current was slowing. The third wave had passed and the system was rebalancing — the upstream pocket was empty now, the water finding new paths, the pressure dropping.

Four people. Three. The number had changed and the water hadn't noticed.

Inyoni counted the straps of the Charge. Twenty-four. *Holds.* Her eyes were dry.

Enama released Nandi's wrist. The calibration grip undone. Her hands were trembling — not from tremens, from the flood. The water had soaked everyone, drenched the clothes, weighed down the bags. Enama looked at her wet hands. The hands that had touched Jabu's shoulder. *I trust you.*

Trust hadn't been enough.

Jabu had no ritual. No shoulder circle. No new word invented to name the absence. Jabu had always been in the margin of Contact — the silent character, the lone cart, the *no need*. His death was in his image: an erasure. The water had come and carried away the silence. Nobody had seen him go. Nobody had held his hand. Jabu's final Contact didn't exist — and that was perhaps the Contact most faithful to what he had always been.

Nandi set her bare feet on the rock above the water. Rock at fifty degrees. Hot. Painful. Her calluses cracked — the first blister. Under the left heel, the skin lifted, a dome of flesh filled with fluid that pulsed to the rhythm of the background magnitude.

She didn't stop. You didn't stop. You walked and the world trembled and Contact had one word fewer.

Four people toward the rally point. Where Sihle and Thabo were waiting.

Or not.

---

## Scene 6 — The off-screen death

Nandi felt Sihle die.

Not die exactly. Disappear. The background vibration lost a note — not a magnitude, not a frequency, something more subtle. Like an orchestra whose one instrument falls silent. Nandi's body, tuned to the rock's vibrations since the desert surface, her body that read the earth through the soles of her feet and the palms of her hands, her body felt the absence before understanding it.

She stopped. Placed her hands on the ground. Eyes closed.

The ground vibrated. The background magnitude, 5.5, the micro-quakes that never ceased. But in this fabric of vibrations, a thread was missing. A body that had been walking in a neighboring tunnel, a body weighing seventy kilos that transmitted its steps to the rock, that body had stopped walking. Stopped weighing. Stopped being.

Nandi opened her eyes. Looked at Enama. Looked at Inyoni. Said nothing. Touched no one. She didn't yet know what she had felt. She didn't know which of the two.

---

The rally point was a wide cavern, a space where the two passages rejoined — the water tunnel and the dry tunnel converging toward the same rocky basin before plunging back into the darkness. The bioluminescence was stronger there — the red filaments lined the ceiling in thick clusters, pulsing like organs.

Thabo arrived first.

Alone.

Nandi saw him before understanding. Thabo, standing at the entrance to the dry passage, the cloth hanging between his fingers, his hands black with soot or gas — something had burned, something had exploded in the dry tunnel, a gas pocket perhaps, an emanation that even the Airmaster hadn't been able to avoid. His cloth was intact — stowed under his shirt at the moment of the explosion. His hands, no.

But it wasn't his hands Nandi was looking at. It was the emptiness behind him.

Thabo was alone.

He advanced into the cavern. His steps were slow — not methodical as usual, not Thabo's step that measures and notes. Slow from fatigue. Slow from what comes after.

He sought Enama's gaze. Found her. Approached.

Enama touched him. Two hands on the shoulders. The Contact of the one who asks. The pressure that questions. *Where is—*

Thabo didn't respond with a codified gesture.

He extended his right hand. Palm open. Facing down. Then he closed it. Slowly. Fingers folding one by one, like a fist clenching nothing, like a hand reaching and not finding.

*Fallen.*

A silence.

The word didn't exist in the team's Contact. Contact had a word for *danger*, for *stop*, for *advance*, for *I am here*. Contact had a gesture for mourning — the shoulder circle, invented after Senzo's death. But Contact had no word for *he fell into the void*. Thabo had just invented it. A hand that descends and closes on nothing.

Enama released Thabo's shoulders.

Her hands made a gesture Nandi didn't recognize.

The right hand closed — hard, fingers clenched, knuckles white. Then it opened. Then it closed. Then it opened. Like a heart. Like a lung. Like a gesture learned in a dream of falling.

Hand that seizes. Hand that releases. Hand that seizes. Hand that releases.

The gesture of guilt. The gesture of a hand repeating what it failed to do — seize and hold. Enama hadn't been there when Sihle fell. Enama was in the water passage, four hundred meters away, in another tunnel, another world. But her hands made the gesture anyway. Her hands seized Sihle's hand five minutes too late, in a different cavern, before people who hadn't seen the fall.

Nandi took Enama's wrist. Squeezed. No message — just the pressure. The calibration grip diverted from its function. No longer a frequency sharing — an anchor. A weight in Enama's hand so the hand would have something to seize that wouldn't let go.

Enama raised her eyes. Her fingers continued their movement. Seize. Release. The rhythm of the gesture was the rhythm of a heart — regular, inevitable, as if Enama's hands couldn't stop.

Nandi understood.

Not because Enama had told her. Not because Thabo had mimed the fall. Not because the ground had vibrated differently when Sihle's body struck the bottom of the chasm. Nandi understood because Contact had no word for this gesture — and when Contact had no word, it meant the word had never needed to exist before.

Nobody had ever needed to say *I held a hand and I let it go*. Contact didn't let go. Contact held. Contact was made to hold — the grips, the pressures, the straps, the ropes, the entire vocabulary of Contact was a vocabulary of holding. You seize. You press. You don't let go.

Enama's hands had invented a new word.

And the word said: *too late*.

---

What Thabo recounted — through Contact, in fragments, through gestures his hands found while searching — assembled the puzzle.

The dry passage had held. Sihle's data had been right — the rock was stable, the frequencies low, the stresses distributed. The miner in Sihle had read the structure correctly. The dry passage would not have collapsed.

Except that the dry passage ran above a chasm.

An invisible chasm — covered by a layer of cooled basalt that formed a natural bridge, thick, solid in appearance. Sihle's seismograph hadn't detected it because the seismograph measured vibrations, not voids. The rock vibrated the same way above the chasm and above solid rock. The data said *stable*. The data was right — the basalt crust was stable.

Until the quake.

The same quake that had sent the flood into the water passage — magnitude 6, five seconds — had cracked the basalt bridge. Not everywhere. At one point. A single point of weakness the miner in Sihle hadn't seen because the point wasn't visible — it was underneath, in the chasm, where the rock had been melting for centuries and where the crust was nothing more than a shell.

Sihle was walking on the shell when it gave way.

Thabo was ahead. Thabo had felt it — not through the cloth, not through the air, through his feet. The ground had vibrated differently under Sihle than it vibrated under Thabo. A second of difference. Thabo had turned. Had seen the ground beneath Sihle crack in a star pattern. Had seen Sihle look at his feet with the expression of someone checking his data and finding that the data was lying.

Had seen Sihle fall.

Thabo didn't recount what followed. His hands made a gesture then stopped. The gesture said: *I reached out my hand.* The stop said: *I didn't reach.*

Sihle had fallen into the chasm. The seismograph with him — the instrument that had measured the world since departure, that had drawn its curves on the roll, that had noted the magnitudes and the frequencies and the numbers Sihle lined up in the margins. The instrument and the man, together, in the void. Data and body, falling at the same speed.

In the margin of the last roll, the number 20. Twenty seconds' lead of body over instrument. The last number Sihle had noted. The number he hadn't wanted to understand.

---

## Scene 7 — Aftermath

Four people.

The number pounded in Nandi's head like a magnitude. Four. Seven at the start. Six after Senzo. Four now. The world was losing numbers. Contact was losing words. Nandi's hands were full of vocabulary and half that vocabulary no longer had a recipient.

Enama no longer touched anything.

It was the most visible sign. Enama, whose hands had been everywhere since departure — on the ground, on the walls, on the rock, on the shoulders, on the wrists, on the gourds, on the roots —, Enama no longer touched. Her hands hung at her sides the way Jabu's hands had hung. An involuntary inheritance. The hands of the biologist, the cook, the survivalist, the hands that knew how to cut roots and prepare the porridge and feel the earth speak beneath the palms — those hands were closed. Clenched. The seize-release gesture had stopped but the fingers stayed curled, as if protecting something inside. Or as if they no longer wanted to touch for fear that everything they touched would fall.

Thabo and Inyoni walked shoulder against shoulder. The "test-and-holds" reinforced — without the cloth this time, without the strap, without the usual dialogue. Just shoulder pressure. Shoulder against shoulder. The passive Contact of their pair, the one that says nothing other than *I am here* and that sufficed when the rest no longer sufficed.

Contact among the survivors had changed. Softer. More hesitant. Not by choice — by fear. Every gesture carried the risk of touching the void. Hands seeking a shoulder risked finding no one. Contact, in a shrinking group, becomes an exercise in memory — the hand reaches toward where Sihle used to stand, toward where Jabu used to crouch, and the hand finds only air.

Nandi sat down. The ground at fifty-five degrees burned through her soaked clothes — the salt of the underground water crackled on the fabric, steam rose from the wet pants as from a cloth placed on an iron. Her feet. The blister under the left heel had burst in the flood. The skin around it was red, blistered. The sole of the right foot still held but the heat pounded it with every contact with the rock. Fifty-five degrees. The pain threshold.

She closed her eyes. Beneath her feet, the background tremor. Magnitude 5.5. Higher than at the entrance to the caverns — the magnitude climbed with depth, like the temperature, like the danger, like everything rising toward them while they descended.

And in the background tremor, the absences. The missing notes. Three bodies no longer walking, no longer weighing, no longer vibrating in the rock. Senzo beneath the rubble of the rift. Jabu in the underground current. Sihle at the bottom of a chasm, seismograph against his chest, curves still wet.

The group's Contact was amputated by half. Of seven idiolects, three were dead. Senzo's three slow pressures. Sihle's two technical fingers. Jabu's useful silence. These gestures would never be made again — not the way Senzo made them, not the way Sihle made them, not the way Jabu didn't make them. The same movements, in other hands, wouldn't say the same thing. Words pass from hand to hand — but they change with each passage, like frequencies change with each layer of rock.

---

Inyoni approached Nandi.

Not a word. Not a shoulder gesture. Inyoni sat beside her and took out the Charge from her bag.

The shock-absorbing case was wet — the floodwater had infiltrated the straps, soaked the joints. Inyoni examined it. Her hands on the casing, her fingers on the straps. Twenty-four. She counted them. Counted the wet straps. Twenty-four. The dry straps. Zero. The case had taken water but the mechanism inside — Sihle's triple suspension — seemed intact. The rubber shock absorbers responded when Inyoni pressed. The ceramic structure showed no cracks.

She turned the Charge over. The calibration dial — a metal disc engraved on the upper face — was intact. The water hadn't reached it. Inyoni turned it slightly. The mechanism clicked — a minuscule sound, a click of metal against metal, that Nandi felt in her hands resting on the rock more than she heard it in the air.

Inyoni placed the Charge between them. Looked at Nandi.

What followed had no words.

Inyoni took Nandi's hands. Placed them on the Charge. The metal was cold — strangely cold in the heat of the caverns, as if the Charge absorbed temperature instead of emitting it. Nandi's fingers touched the surface. The cylinder was the size of her forearm. Smooth. Dense. Twenty-two centimeters in diameter — her hands couldn't reach around it.

Inyoni guided Nandi's hands to the locks. Three. One by one.

The first lock was on the left side — a rotary cylinder embedded in the Charge's body. Inyoni placed Nandi's fingers on it. Turned — clockwise. The lock resisted, then gave, a quarter turn. Click.

*One.*

The second lock, right side. Same mechanism. Clockwise. A quarter turn. Click.

*Two.*

The third lock, underneath. Smaller. More delicate. Counter-clockwise — the trap, the one that would fool someone who didn't know. Inyoni turned Nandi's fingers the other way. Counter-clockwise. A quarter turn. Click.

*Three.*

Nandi counted. Inyoni's hands on hers. The metal under her fingers. The clicks in the rock.

*Holds. Holds. Holds.*

Not Nandi's words. Inyoni's words, passed into Nandi's hands the way Senzo's gesture had passed into Nandi's hands in chapter IV. An inheritance. A loan. Contact transmitting not a message but a knowledge — hands teaching hands.

Inyoni repeated the operation. Faster this time. Her fingers guided Nandi's: clockwise, clockwise, counter-clockwise. Three clicks. Nandi's hands memorized. The position of the locks. The resistance of the metal. The direction of rotation. The number.

Then Inyoni showed the dial.

The calibration disc, engraved with fine lines, with numbers Nandi couldn't read — frequencies, hertz, measurements Sihle would have understood and that the seismograph at the bottom of the chasm could no longer confirm. Inyoni turned the dial slowly. Nandi felt the vibration change under her fingers — not a mechanical vibration, something deeper. The dial modified the Charge's internal frequency. At each notch, at each graduation, the metal emitted a sound the ears couldn't hear but the fingers could feel.

Inyoni stopped at a graduation. Showed it. Pressure on Nandi's finger — *here*. The base frequency. The frequency Inyoni had preset for the backup. The starting point.

Then Inyoni did something the activation plan hadn't foreseen. She took Nandi's hands and placed them flat on the Charge, palms against the metal. And she waited.

Nandi felt.

The metal vibrated. Not from the ground's vibration — its own vibration. The Charge had an internal frequency, a hum in the metal, like a sleeping heart. Nandi's palms on the metal. The palms that had been reading the ground since the desert surface. The same palms.

The dial had to be turned until the Charge's frequency matched the ground's frequency. That's what Sihle's seismograph would have measured. That's what instruments would have done. But the instruments were at the bottom of the chasm.

Nandi's hands didn't need an instrument.

Inyoni knew it. Inyoni, the compulsive counter, the woman of straps and numbers, the climber who never let go — Inyoni had understood what Sihle had never wanted to admit. Nandi's hands felt frequencies. Nandi's tremens was a seismograph. If anyone could calibrate the Charge without an instrument, it was the woman who read the world through the soles of her feet.

Nandi withdrew her hands from the Charge. Looked at Inyoni. A look — not Contact, not touch. A look that said: *I understand what you're asking of me.*

Inyoni reset the locks to their initial position. Stowed the Charge in the bag. Checked the straps. Twenty-four. *Holds.*

The transfer was done. The hands knew. If Inyoni fell — when Inyoni fell — Nandi's hands would know what to do.

---

The team resumed the march.

Four people. The cavern was heating up. The red filaments were turning orange in places — not the blue light of the village, not the red light of the intermediate caverns. Orange. The color of lava seen from afar. The heat was rising.

Nandi placed a bare foot on the ground. Fifty-eight degrees. The blister under the left heel sent a jolt of pain into the calf. The sole of the right foot — still intact, but reddened, the skin tight and shiny like wet leather.

Thabo approached her. Not to touch her — to show her. His finger pointing at the rock. Two types of rock on the ground: one light, almost grey, the other dark, almost black. The light rock was cooler — cooled by the little air still circulating. The dark rock was hotter — heated by the magma below, by the lava rising, by the earth cooking.

Thabo placed his hand on the light rock. Then on the dark rock. The difference — ten degrees perhaps, fifteen. The light rock was bearable. The dark rock was not.

He looked at Nandi's feet. Then the rocks. Then Nandi's feet.

The Contact of pedagogy — without words, without codified gesture. The oldest gesture in the world: showing. *Look. The light rock is cool. The dark rock is hot. Walk on the light.*

Nandi looked at the ground. The checkerboard of light and dark rocks spread before her like a map. The light rocks formed a path — not continuous, not simple, but a path. Hold to hold. Light rock to light rock.

Her feet understood. The pain would be there. The pain wouldn't leave. But the pain could be sorted — separated from the signal, separated from the tremens, isolated in a corner of the body while the rest continued to read. Thabo had just given her a tool. The geologist's tool — color as indicator. Eyes in service of feet.

Nandi placed her foot on the first light rock. Forty-five degrees. Bearable. She advanced to the next. Forty-three. Then the next.

The path of light rocks.

Enama walked ahead. Hands closed. Contact retracted. She no longer touched the walls. No longer touched the ground. Enama's world-Contact — that capacity for dialogue with the earth, that open hand on living rock that had dazzled the reader in chapter II — Enama's world-Contact was shut. Not because the earth had gone silent. Because Enama no longer wanted to listen.

The earth had told her *water*. The water had killed Jabu.

The earth had told her *not rock*. The rock had killed Sihle.

Intuition had been right and reason had died anyway. Neither one had saved. Only Contact between the two could have — but Contact between Sihle and Enama had never existed. They had had a conflict, not a dialogue. Two fingers against the whole palm. Data against body. And the body of the conflict lay at the bottom of a chasm with its numbers.

Enama walked and her hands were closed and Nandi, behind her, saw the clenched fingers, the white knuckles, the seize-release gesture that kept repeating endlessly inside the fists.

The air was warming. The ground vibrated. Magnitude 6. Higher than before. The earth rumbled louder, as if impatient, as if eager for the bodies to descend faster toward it.

Inyoni tightened the straps of the Charge. Twenty-four. *Holds.*

Thabo tested the air with his cloth. The fibers barely moved. The air was still dying — more slowly since the water tunnel had created a lateral current, but dying all the same. Thabo put the cloth away. Frowned.

Nandi walked barefoot on the light rocks. The checkerboard. The path Thabo had shown her. The pain under the left heel. The signal under the right heel. The tremens in the bones. The magnitude in the feet. The absence in the hands.

Four people. Toward the bottom. Where Contact would continue to die.

And Contact had three fewer words.
