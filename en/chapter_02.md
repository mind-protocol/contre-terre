# II — Intermediate Zones

## Scene 1 — The ground changes

The sand stopped first under Nandi's feet.

Not all at once. Not like a border you cross. More like a sentence that changes meaning midway — the same ground, the same vibration, but the grain was no longer the same. Under her toes, the fine desert dust gave way to something more compact, more dense. Crushed rock. Volcanic gravel that cracked under the body's weight and didn't return signals the same way.

She slowed down. Her feet no longer understood.

That was tremens. Not the pain — the confusion. The body had spent three weeks in the southern desert, calibrating itself to its long, deep waves, its bass vibrations that rolled under the sand like an organ played in slow motion. And now the ground was rising in frequency. The waves became dry. Short. Sharp cracks that climbed up through the ankles and hammered the knees.

Saliva rose in her mouth. She swallowed it.

Ahead, Senzo walked without slowing. His feet were shod — soft boots with thin soles, a compromise between protection and reading. He felt the quakes but didn't *read* them the way Nandi did. His body didn't translate. He moved forward, and direction was enough.

Enama was the second to feel the change. She stopped dead, both hands on the hips of a cart, and turned her head toward Nandi. Their gazes met. Enama didn't need Contact to say it — her eyes said: *You too?*

Nandi nodded. Imperceptible. Not a Contact gesture — a gesture of exhaustion.

The column continued. The terrain climbed. The sand had disappeared. Under the cart wheels, the volcanic gravel protested with each turn, a high-pitched grinding that the volcano's wind carried toward them like a warning.

Thabo, mid-column, crouching on the moving cart, had taken out his cloth. He held it by the tips of his fingers, arm raised above his head, and he watched the fibers dance. The wind came from the volcano. Dry. Hot. Sulfurous. Not enough to be dangerous — but enough for Thabo to frown and tuck the cloth into his shirt pocket with a slow gesture that, in the vocabulary of those who knew him, meant: *Noted.*

The volcano's foothills spread before them like an open maw. The flat desert had lied for days — straight horizon, nothing to see, walk and walk. Now the landscape heaved. Basalt ridges, ravines carved by centuries of tremors, faults in the rock that ran like black veins on the world's grey skin. And everywhere, everywhere, the vibration.

Higher here. More nervous.

Nandi vomited.

She stopped, bent over, and her body emptied what it had in its stomach — Enama's porridge, water, bile. She didn't fight it. You didn't fight tremens. You let it pass and walked after.

Senzo heard — or felt, through the rope linking his cart to the middle one, the vibration of a halt. He turned around. Saw Nandi bent over, hands on her knees. He came back toward her. Not fast. Not slow. The exact speed of someone who refuses to worry but worries.

He placed his hand on the top of her back. Two spaced pressures. Standard Contact: *You okay?*

She wiped her mouth with the back of her hand. Grabbed Senzo's wrist. Three quick pressures, a pause, one long pressure. This wasn't standard vocabulary either — it was their dialect, Senzo-and-Nandi Contact, forged over months of expedition. It meant something like: *My body is translating. Let it.*

Senzo withdrew his hand. Resumed the march.

Behind them, Sihle had opened his eyes. His seismograph was drawing tighter curves — the peaks were closing in, the amplitudes rising. He said nothing. He took a pencil and noted a number in the margin of the paper roll. 6.2.

## Scene 2 — Magnitude 7

It started with the carts.

The first cart — Thabo and Inyoni's — lifted two centimeters. Not much. Enough for the wheels to leave the ground for a fraction of a second and fall back with a dull thud that wasn't the sound of wheels on gravel. It was the sound of the world changing its mind.

Then the ground undulated.

Nandi felt it before everyone — not the tremor, what came *before* the tremor. The micro-silence. The infinitesimal suspension of the background vibration, the fraction of a second where the ground holds its breath. Like a heart skipping a beat.

She grabbed Senzo's arm. Not a pressure — a grip. Total. All five fingers dug into his forearm, nails in flesh. In any Contact, it meant the same thing: *Now.*

Senzo raised his fist. Closed hand, arm outstretched. The signal. Everyone stopped. Even Sihle, who didn't need the signal because his seismograph was already screaming — the needle was tearing the paper, the curves were no longer curves but saw teeth.

The earth struck.

Not like in the desert, where the tremors rolled under the sand, long and muffled, almost lazy. Here the rock absorbed nothing. The wave climbed straight up through the basalt, rigid, brutal, and struck the feet like a hammer blow under the sole. Nandi fell to her knees. Jabu grabbed the cart. Thabo pressed his hands to the ground — not from panic, from a reading reflex. Inyoni, standing on the second cart, bent her knees and held. Held the way she held straps, the way she held ropes, the way she held everything — with the strength of hands that never let go of anything.

The main wave lasted eight seconds. Eight seconds during which the world became vertical, horizontal, diagonal, and none of that anymore. Eight seconds where Contact became something else.

The gestures changed.

Gone the measured pressures, the calibrated touches, the learned codes. Under magnitude 7, Contact turned animal. Enama grabbed Nandi by the collar. Not a word — a reflex. Senzo pressed his palm against the rock and his other hand on the shoulder of Thabo who had fallen beside him, and that gesture had nothing to say except *I'm here*. The arm-locks became cramps. The codified grips became survival clutches.

Contact had an emergency register the desert had never triggered.

Stones fell from the ridge above them. Not big — basalt shards, fist-to-skull-sized, bouncing down the slope. Jabu took one on the shoulder. He grunted — voice, useless, automatic, drowned in the rumbling — and pressed himself against the cart.

Then it stopped.

Not silence — silence didn't exist. But the return to background magnitude. 4. Maybe 5. The world's normal pulse, after the crisis.

Senzo stood up. Blood on his palm — the rock had cut. He looked at the team. Counted. Seven. Standing or getting up. Seven.

He raised his hand. Open fist. Five fingers. *We continue.*

Nobody moved right away. The body needed to recover. The post-seismic tremens — a residual vertigo, like the roll of a ship after the storm. Nandi was on all fours, fingers dug into the gravel. She was breathing through her mouth. Each exhale was a small sound — not a word, not a moan, something between the two. Her body was recalculating. The new frequencies entered through the soles of her feet and climbed up the bones like an electric current seeking ground.

Sihle approached her. It was the first time he had deliberately touched her since departure. He placed two fingers on her wrist. Technical Contact — the wrist is information, not emotion. Two pressures: *Data?*

Nandi raised her head. Looked at him. Her eyes were bloodshot — tremens burst the capillaries. She grabbed Sihle's two fingers and placed them on the ground. Pressed. Hard.

*Listen for yourself.*

Sihle withdrew his hand. His fingers couldn't read the ground. His fingers could read the curves on paper. He stood up and returned to his seismograph without another gesture.

Enama, who had seen the exchange, said nothing. But her hands, picking up a gourd fallen from the cart, trembled with an anger that didn't yet have words.

## Scene 3 — The belly of the world

The entrance to the cavern was not an entrance.

It was a fault — a tear in the flank of the foothills, three meters wide, barely one high, descending at an angle into the dark. The kind of opening the ground had opened by accident and nobody had closed because nobody closed anything here. The world opened. The world didn't close.

Thabo went first. That was his role — where there was a hole, Thabo tested the air before anyone set foot inside. He crouched before the fault, took out his cloth, and held it at the opening's height. The fibers trembled, then leaned inward. Inbound airflow. The volcano was inhaling.

He turned back to Senzo. A single gesture: hand flat, palm toward the ground, lateral motion. Clear Contact. *Air is circulating. We can go.*

Senzo looked at the fault. Looked at the map — a roll of thick cloth on which he had traced the descent lines from the surveys of those who had never come back but whose notes had risen through a fault, carried by the wind, found two years later in the desert. The lines said: here, a passage. The lines didn't say whether the passage still held.

He entered.

One by one, crouching then on their knees then nearly lying flat, they passed through the fault. The carts didn't enter — Inyoni detached the loads, distributed the weight into packs, strapped what could be strapped onto backs. Thirty-two straps on a cart. Forty on four backs. Her hands counted without being asked.

The darkness swallowed them.

Not all at once. The daylight from the foothills slid into the fault like a liquid and thinned with each meter. Five meters: grey. Ten meters: dark grey. Twenty meters: nothing.

Jabu lit the first torch. Not fire — a chemical stick, a resin coated on a copper tube that you cracked like a bone. The greenish light flooded the walls and everyone saw at the same time.

The walls were alive.

Luminous filaments, faint, bluish, ran along the rock like a network of veins. Not moss, not lichen — something between the two, an organic matter that pulsed faintly to the rhythm of the background vibration. Magnitude 4. The wall pulsed at magnitude 4. Like a heart beating to the rhythm of the world.

Enama dropped her pack. It wasn't a voluntary gesture — her hands opened by themselves, the pack fell into the dust, and she moved toward the wall the way you approach an animal you don't want to startle. Her fingers landed on the luminous filaments.

The light reacted to touch. Where Enama pressed, the filaments intensified — bluer, almost white — then calmed when she withdrew her hand. Contact. The wall responded to Contact. Not with words, not with coded pressures, but with light. Another language. A language without skin.

She turned to the group. Her eyes shone — not with tears, with reflected light. Her hand trembled against the wall. She searched for Senzo with her gaze and when she found him, she made a gesture no one had ever seen her make. Hand open, placed flat on the living rock, fingers spread. Not human Contact. World-Contact.

Sihle, behind, was writing on his roll. He wasn't looking at the walls. He was looking at his seismograph. The curves had changed — denser, more regular, as if the rock here absorbed the waves instead of transmitting them. He noted: *biological dampening?* with a question mark that said a lot about what he thought of Enama's biology and her hands placed on the walls like a prayer.

Nandi didn't need to touch the wall to know. Her bare feet on the cavern floor told her everything. The frequency had changed again. Not upward this time — downward. Infra-bass, vibrations so deep they passed below the threshold of consciousness and entered the bones directly. Tremens answered. Her stomach contracted. Her jaw clenched. Her vision blurred.

She leaned against the wall. The luminous filaments lit up around her shoulders like a crown.

Senzo saw her. Came back to her. Placed his hand on the back of her neck. The long pressure. Warm. The one that had no official translation in Contact. The one that said nothing but: *I know you're suffering and I'm here.*

Nandi closed her eyes and let the wall's light pulse against her back.

## Scene 4 — The map and the body

The disagreement broke out at a fork.

The cavern had been descending in a gentle slope for three hours — or what felt like three hours; underground, time had no markers. No sun. No shadow. Just the blue light of the filaments and the green light of the chemical sticks and the constant, unceasing vibration of the world rumbling above, below, everywhere.

The fork was a perfect Y. Two tunnels, same width, same slope, same darkness. The rock gave no visual clue. A binary choice in a world that didn't like choices.

Senzo unrolled his map. The lines said: right. The survey — done by a previous expedition, the one no one had returned from but whose notes had risen through a fault, carried by the wind, found two years later in the desert — indicated the right tunnel as the path to the deep caverns.

Sihle confirmed. His seismograph showed stronger activity to the left — more tremors, more amplitudes. Logic said: avoid the activity. Go right. Where it's calmer. Where the rock isn't fighting itself.

Enama crouched. Placed both hands flat on the ground, at the center of the Y. Closed her eyes.

Nobody spoke. Nobody ever spoke. But the silence here had a different texture — a courtroom silence, where everyone waits for the verdict.

Enama opened her eyes. Grabbed Senzo's wrist. Four pressures. Quick. Then she pointed to the left tunnel.

Sihle stepped forward. Not toward Enama — toward Senzo. He placed his hand on the leader's forearm. Two pressures, a pause, two pressures. Technical Contact: *The data says no.* Then he pointed to the right tunnel.

Senzo looked at one. Looked at the other. Looked at the map. Looked at the ground.

Enama didn't let go of his wrist. Her grip said what her eyes said, what her hands on the ground had said: *The earth speaks, and it says left.*

Sihle brandished the seismograph roll. The curves were definitive. The left tunnel was more active. More dangerous. More unstable.

— The earth speaks, said Enama.

Her voice — her real voice, the one that came from the throat, not the one from her hands — tore through the silence like a stone into water. Everyone flinched. You didn't speak. You *never* spoke on expedition. Voice wasted energy. Voice got lost in the rumbling. Voice was an admission of weakness — the acknowledgment that Contact wasn't enough.

Enama knew it. She did it anyway.

— The earth speaks, she repeated, lower this time, almost to herself. And it says left.

Sihle didn't answer with voice. He answered with Contact — a firm grip on Senzo's forearm, firmer than Enama's, more technical, colder. His fingers said: *I'm giving you numbers. She's giving you beliefs.*

Senzo withdrew both arms. Gently. Without abruptness. He freed them from both grips — Enama's, Sihle's — and stood alone at the center of the Y, arms at his sides, the map rolled in his right hand.

He looked at Nandi.

She was leaning against the wall, eyes half-closed, bare feet on the rock. The blue filaments pulsed around her. Her face was pale. Tremens was eating her from inside. But her feet — her feet were reading.

Senzo approached her. Placed his hand on her shoulder. One pressure. Long. The silent question: *You, what do you feel?*

Nandi didn't answer right away. She listened. Not with her ears — with bones, tendons, the soles of her feet pressed into the basalt dust. The right tunnel vibrated little. Calm. Stable. The left tunnel vibrated hard. Nervous. Alive.

But beneath the nervous, beneath the surface agitation, there was something else. A deep, regular wave that descended. The left tunnel didn't lead toward chaos — it led toward the source. The seismic activity wasn't a danger. It was a path.

She grabbed Senzo's wrist. One pressure. Long. Then she pointed left.

Senzo put away his map. Walked toward the left tunnel.

Sihle stood still for three seconds. His clenched jaw made a sound the seismograph didn't register. Then he followed.

The team plunged into the left tunnel. The vibration rose. The blue filaments intensified. The world descended.

## Scene 5 — The threshold

They walked — or crawled, depending on the passages — for what could have been hours. The slope steepened. The air warmed. Sulfur stung the nostrils. Thabo kept his cloth deployed permanently now, arm outstretched before him like a dowser, stopping from time to time to rub the fibers between his fingers and feel the humidity, the heat, the particles.

The tunnel opened onto an overhang.

Before them, below, a cavern so vast the chemical sticks' light didn't reach the walls. Black. Deep. And at its center, far below, an orange glow that wasn't bioluminescence. That wasn't chemistry.

Lava. Not a river — a thread. A thin incandescent ribbon snaking along the cavern floor like a brushstroke on a black canvas. The first visible proof that the volcano was not a landscape. That the volcano was a machine. That something, beneath, was burning.

The heat rose in waves. Dry waves, without wind, that cracked the lips and split the skin of the hands. Contact was about to become painful. Touching another's skin when your own is burning — the language had a physical cost here.

Senzo stopped at the edge of the overhang. He didn't look down. He turned around and looked up — toward the tunnel entrance, toward the slope they had just descended, toward the surface they would never see again.

Then he looked at his team. Six faces lit from below, by the orange of the distant lava. Six pairs of eyes that knew.

He raised his hand. Open palm. Not the departure signal — something else. A slow gesture, the hand rising, turning over, and descending in an arc. A gesture from ancient Contact, the kind no longer used on the surface because the surface had no need for this word.

*We don't go back up.*

Nandi felt the word in her belly before reading it on Senzo's hand. Tremens had already told her. Her feet had known since the fault. Every meter of descent had been one meter less between them and magnitude 11.

Inyoni counted. A reflex — she always counted. Seven people. Four chemical sticks. Rations for fifty-two days. Three hundred meters of rope. One detonator. One seismograph. One cloth.

And Contact.

She tightened the nearest strap. *Holds.*

The team descended into the cavern. The orange light swallowed them from below, and the blue light of the filaments followed them along the walls, and between the two lights there were the seven bodies carrying the only language the world had not yet destroyed.

But the world hadn't finished trying.
