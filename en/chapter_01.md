# I — Desert Surface

## Scene 1 — Opening

Senzo placed his hand on Nandi's shoulder. Three pressures. Slow. Side to side. In their Contact, it meant: *Follow me.* But also: *The world is still trembling.*

She followed him.

The ground vibrated beneath their feet the way it always vibrated, the way it had vibrated the day before and the day before that and every day since days existed. Magnitude 4, maybe 5. The kind of tremor that didn't even deserve a name. Background noise. The world's pulse.

Nandi walked barefoot. Not out of vanity — out of necessity. Soles lied. Leather, rubber, anything separating flesh from ground smothered the signals. She needed bare stone under her toes to read. The desert spoke to her through the soles of her feet and she had no right not to listen.

Ahead of her, Senzo moved at a steady pace, shoulders low, gaze fixed somewhere beyond the horizon line where sand met volcano. The entire expedition lived in that gaze. Not in Sihle's calculations, not in Enama's provisions, not in Inyoni's ropes. In the way Senzo looked at a point and walked toward it as if the whole world were just a detail between him and his destination.

Behind them, the camp was being taken down. Jabu rolled the hammocks, detached the elastic cords from stakes driven two meters into the sand. Everything that wasn't fixed in this world ended up on the ground. Everything that was fixed also ended up on the ground — just more slowly. The architecture of survival came down to that difference: how long things took to fall.

Thabo was testing the air. Crouched facing the wind, a piece of blackened cloth stretched between his fingers, he watched the fiber's movements. Air didn't lie. The ground could give way, walls could collapse, but the air current announced everything before anyone else. Thabo trusted the cloth more than the instruments. Instruments had batteries. The cloth had the wind.

Inyoni, on all fours atop the equipment cart, tightened the straps one by one. Her hands worked without her looking at them. Thirty-two straps. She counted them as she tightened, one gesture per strap, and each gesture was also a word in her head — a word of Contact she repeated on loop like a mechanical prayer. *Holds. Holds. Holds.* Thirty-two times.

Sihle wasn't helping with the teardown. Sihle was sitting on a crate, eyes closed, a portable seismograph on his knees. The device drew curves on a roll of paper — lines that trembled the way everything trembled here, an automatic writing of the world. Sihle read those lines the way others read faces. He saw magnitudes, directions, depths. This morning, the curves were regular. Low. Calm, for this world. He opened his eyes and looked for Enama.

She was at the other end of camp, her back turned. She wasn't looking at Sihle's seismograph. She had never looked at his seismograph. Enama trusted her feet, her hands, the shiver that climbed her spine when the earth changed register. The body knows before the instrument, she said. Sihle answered that the body lies. Their war was ancient. It would last until the bottom of the volcano — or not.

Enama was cutting roots. Fat, fibrous tubers she had dug up at dawn in the sand east of camp, where an underground spring kept the soil damp despite the quakes. She sliced them into strips, crushed them in a stone mortar, added water, crushed again. The thick paste that resulted would be the midday meal. No plates. Gourds. You drank your food here — everything that could spill did spill, and everything always spilled.

Nandi felt the ground change.

Not a quake. Not yet. Something before the quake. A shift in frequency, a slight discord between the vibration she expected and the one arriving. Like a wrong note in an orchestra that no one else could hear. Her stomach clenched. Saliva rose to her lips.

The tremens.

Her body was recalibrating. Every geographical zone had its base frequency, its own seismic register, and the human body adapted to it from birth the way the ear adapts to ambient noise. Nandi was born in the northeastern archipelagos, where the quakes were high and dry, brief snaps like whip-cracks in stone. Here, in the southern desert, the vibrations were low, long, deep — waves that rolled beneath the ground like a sleeping animal breathing.

Her body didn't like these frequencies. It told her so through nausea.

She stopped. Placed a hand on the ground. Closed her eyes.

Under her fingers, the earth murmured. Not words — intentions. The same intention for months, repeated each day a little louder: something was rising. Something deep, slow, massive, climbing toward the surface like a bubble in thick lava. Magnitude 11. The one no one had ever measured. The one the instruments didn't yet know how to draw.

Nandi felt it.

Senzo turned around. He saw her hand on the ground, her eyes closed, and he knew. He came back to her. Placed his palm on her forearm. Two short pressures, then a long one. In their Contact: *What do you feel?*

She opened her eyes. Searched for words. Not voice-words — touch-words. She grabbed Senzo's wrist. Squeezed three times. Fast, fast, slow. Then she placed her index finger on the ground and pressed.

*It's rising.*

Senzo didn't answer right away. He looked at the volcano. The black mass at the edge of the desert, where sand became rock and rock became heat. The mouth of the world. The hole through which all seven of them would descend, to go place a charge at the bottom and trigger what could no longer be prevented — only preempted.

He placed his hand on the back of Nandi's neck. A single pressure. Long. Warm.

*I know.*

Then he turned and walked toward the camp. The teardown continued. The sun was climbing. The ground trembled. No one noticed.

---

When Senzo gave the signal to depart — a raised hand, fist closed, then open, five fingers spread: *We go, now* — the team was already ready. Not because they were efficient. Because there was almost nothing to carry.

Seven people. Four carts. Survival gear, explosives — packed in shock-absorbing cases Sihle had designed himself, triple-suspension boxes that absorbed tremors up to magnitude 8 —, ropes, gourds, liquid rations for sixty days. Sixty days. That was Senzo's estimate. Sihle had calculated forty-five. Enama had said: *We'll see.*

They started walking. Senzo in front. Nandi right behind him — her position in the line was no accident. She was his eyes beneath the ground. His predictor. The only one on the team whose body translated the earth's signals into something resembling language. The others felt quakes the way you feel rain — when it falls. Nandi felt them the way you feel a storm — before.

Behind Nandi, Enama. Then Thabo and Inyoni, side by side, pulling the first cart. Jabu alone on the second. Sihle last, one hand on his portable seismograph bouncing in its chest harness, the other hand free, ready to touch — ready for Contact.

No one spoke.

It wasn't silence. Silence didn't exist here. The world rumbled, sand scraped under the cart wheels, wind whistled through the rock fissures on the horizon. But no one *spoke*, because voice was a useless luxury in a world that drowned out everything. Speaking meant shouting. And shouting meant wasting the energy the body saved for Contact.

They communicated by touch. A hand on a shoulder, the speed of the pressure, the exact location of contact, the duration — everything was significant. Contact had its grammar, its nuances, its registers. A firm grip on the forearm didn't carry the same value as a firm grip on the wrist. The shoulder said urgency. The arm said information. The hand said emotion. The nape of the neck said intimacy.

Senzo had touched the back of Nandi's neck. He didn't touch anyone else's.

The column moved toward the volcano. The desert heated. The ground vibrated. Magnitude 4, constant, regular, boring. The background noise of a world that had never known stillness.

Nandi walked barefoot on the hot sand and she counted the seconds between each micro-tremor. Three seconds. Three seconds. Three seconds. Regular. Normal. Nothing to report.

But beneath the rhythm, below, far below — like a second heart under the first — she felt the other beat. The one with no rhythm. The one that was rising.

She said nothing. Not yet. Senzo knew. And when the others needed to know, he would tell them. That was his role. To decide the route. To decide what gets said. To decide when it gets said.

For now, they walked. Seven people in a desert that trembled. Heading toward a volcano that was going to kill them all.

And the world didn't care.
