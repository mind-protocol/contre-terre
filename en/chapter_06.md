# VI — The Volcanic Zones

## Scene 1 — Heat as a weapon

The heat did not arrive. It was already there.

Nandi understood the difference on the third step into the new tunnel. The deep caverns had been hot — fifty, sixty degrees, the rock cooking underfoot, sweat drying before it could run. But the heat of the caverns was ambient heat. A bath. A lukewarm oven. You could stand it.

The volcanic zones were something else.

The heat here had a direction. It came from below and it rose — not the way steam rises, in swirls, in approximations. It rose like a wall. A front. A continuous thermal wave pressing against the skin and never retreating. The air was no longer air — it was a fluid, thick, charged with sulfur and mineral vapor, and each breath burned the lungs like a first coffee swallowed too fast.

Nandi set her foot on the ground. Sixty-five degrees. The checkerboard of light rocks that Thabo had taught her still existed — but the light rocks were less light. Gray pulling toward brown. Less cooled. The air that had been ventilating them was dying, and without air, the light rocks were losing their thermal lead. Ten degrees of difference in the caverns. Five here. In three hours, maybe two. Every rock would be the same temperature.

The ground vibrated. Magnitude 6.5. Higher than in the deep caverns, more constant, more insistent. The micro-quakes no longer stopped — they overlapped, superimposed, a permanent trembling that made the orange filaments dance on the walls. Orange. No longer red — orange. The color had crossed a threshold that the bioluminescence registered better than instruments.

Thabo walked at the front.

His cloth never left his hand. At every fork, at every widening of the tunnel, he extended his arm and the fibers responded — but the fibers responded less and less. The air still circulated, but weakly, like a heart at the end of its run. The lateral currents of the caverns, fed by water networks and natural ventilation shafts, had dried up. The water had evaporated. The shafts had been blocked by quakes. All that remained was an ascending breath — hot air rising from the magma, loaded with gas, and the little breathable air that descended by gravity, heavier, cooler, hugging the ground.

Thabo crouched. Held the cloth flush with the ground. The fibers moved — a quiver, faint but real. The breathable air was down below. Where Nandi's feet walked. Where the heat was at its maximum.

He turned to the team. Hand flat, downward motion. Not the usual gesture — *air is flowing, we can go*. A modified gesture. *The air is below. Stay low.*

Inyoni adjusted the straps of the Charge on her back. The fibers had dried since the flood — dried and hardened, the salt from the underground water crystallized into the weave, the straps turned stiff as wood. She counted them anyway. Twenty-four. But the count had changed in timbre. In the caverns, *holds* was a statement. Here, *holds* was a question.

Enama walked with her hands closed.

Since Sihle's death, her hands had not touched rock. Had not touched ground. Had not touched walls. Enama's Contact-world — that open hand on the earth, that instinctive listening that had guided the group since chapter II — Enama's Contact-world was dead. Not from the heat. From guilt.

The earth had told her *water*. The water had taken Jabu. The earth had told her *not rock*. The rock had taken Sihle. The intuition had been right on the facts and wrong on the consequences. Being right had saved no one.

Enama walked and her closed fists swung along her body and Nandi, two steps behind her, saw the white knuckles, the protruding tendons, the nails digging into the palms. The grip-release gesture had frozen in the *grip* position. Enama's hands were clutching something no one could see — Sihle's hand, perhaps. The hand she had not held.

Nandi extended hers. The calibration grip — hand on Enama's wrist. The pressure that shared the frequency. That absorbed a part of the signal.

Enama withdrew her wrist.

Not abruptly. Not violently. A slow withdrawal. Deliberate. Sihle's withdrawal — the exact same movement, the two fingers pulling back from the forearm when Contact is refused. Enama was making Sihle's gesture without knowing it. The dead man's gesture had migrated into the living woman's hands.

Contact burned.

Not metaphorically. The heat made touch painful. Seventy degrees at ground level, fifty in the air, the skin damp with sweat and vapor, and each press of a hand on skin lasted a few seconds before the pain rose — the heat trapped between the two skins, amplified by contact, transformed into a burn. Contact, in the volcanic zones, cost. Every tactile word had a price in pain.

Nandi withdrew her hand. Looked at it. Reddened. The skin of her wrist bore the trace of the brief contact with Enama — a mark, almost an imprint, as if the heat had photographed the gesture.

Contact photographed gestures now. The heat printed words into flesh.

## Scene 2 — Enama's guilt

The camp — if you could call camp a one-hour stop in a hollow of rock — was silent.

Not the silence of the caverns, which was full of noise — the background rumble, the crackling of filaments, the seeping of water. A Contact silence. A silence of hands that do not move. Of gestures that are not made. The worst of silences in a world where the silence of the voice is normal but the silence of touch is an amputation.

Enama sat apart.

Not far — the cavern did not allow far. But apart. Her back against the wall, knees drawn up, arms around her knees. Hands hidden. Her hands that had been the most active in the group since departure — the hands that cut roots, that prepared the porridge, that touched the ground and the walls and the shoulders — her hands were buried between her knees and her chest. Outside Contact. Outside the world.

Nandi observed her.

Not out of curiosity. Through the tremens. Nandi's body registered the vibrations of every body around her — the steps, the weights, the heart rates transmitted through the rock. Enama's body had been vibrating differently since the dry passage. Faster. More irregular. A heart that accelerated for no apparent reason, hands that trembled — not from the tremens, from something else. Enama's body carried Sihle's death like an interior earthquake.

Thabo approached Enama.

He crouched beside her. His cloth hanging between his fingers — the passive gesture, Thabo's resting gesture. He did not touch her. He placed his hands on the ground beside hers. Not a direct Contact — a parallel Contact. Two pairs of hands on the same rock. Thabo's fingers were open, Enama's fingers were closed.

He waited.

This was Thabo's Contact — not a Contact of words, not a Contact of coded pressures. The Contact of presence. Shoulder against shoulder, hands side by side. The "test-and-holds" applied not to Inyoni but to Enama. Thabo was testing. He was testing whether Enama could still.

Enama opened her eyes. Looked at Thabo's hands on the ground. Looked at hers, closed. The contrast. The open fingers of the Airmaster, the closed fingers of the biologist. Two postures of the body. Two responses to heat and to the world.

She did not open her hands.

But she shifted her knee. One centimeter. Her elbow touched Thabo's forearm. Not a voluntary Contact — a gravitational Contact. The body's weight yielding one centimeter toward the other. The most minimal Contact that exists — the one that is not even a gesture. Just weight.

Thabo did not move. Forearm against elbow. Weight accepted.

---

Inyoni made Nandi rehearse.

In the dark — Nandi's eyes blindfolded with a piece of cloth Thabo had cut from his sleeve. Hands alone. The Charge between them.

Nandi searched for the locks. The first — on the left. Her fingers found the rotary cylinder. Turned. Clockwise. A quarter turn. Click.

*One.*

The second — on the right. Clockwise. A quarter turn. Click.

*Two.*

The third — underneath. Counter-clockwise. The trap. Nandi turned the wrong way. The lock resisted.

Inyoni's fingers settled over Nandi's. Correction. Counter-clockwise. No pressure of reproach — no pressure at all. Just guidance. Inyoni's fingers over Nandi's fingers, the rotation reversed, the click.

*Three.*

Inyoni had her undo. Then redo. Eyes blindfolded. In the dark. Clockwise, clockwise, counter-clockwise. Click, click, click.

*Holds. Holds. Holds.*

Inyoni's words in Nandi's hands. Words that were not Contact words but counting words — one, two, three — translated into the rhythm of the one who counted everything. The straps. The fibers. The locks. The days remaining.

Nandi removed the blindfold. Looked at her hands on the Charge. The same hands that read the ground, that felt the frequencies, that carried the tremens. The hands that would one day — soon — activate a seismic resonator and trigger magnitude 11.

Inyoni stowed the Charge. The straps. Twenty-four. *Holds.*

But the last strap — the twenty-fourth — snapped under her fingers. The salt from the flood had eaten through the fiber. Inyoni looked at the broken strap. A strand of braided fiber, bleached, crumbling.

She said nothing. Knotted the two ends. Counted the straps. Twenty-four. One of which had a knot.

*Holds?*

## Scene 3 — The critical Airmaster

The gas was waiting for them around the bend.

No warning sign. No color, no smell — CO2 has neither. The first sign was Nandi stumbling. Her legs gave out. No pain, no vertigo — just a surrender. As if the muscles had decided to stop obeying. She fell to her knees. The ground at seventy-two degrees burned through the fabric of her pants. She did not feel the burn. She felt nothing anymore.

Thabo was the first to understand.

The cloth. The cloth was not moving. Not a breath, not a quiver — the fibers hung, dead, inert. The air had stopped. No — the air no longer existed. The air had been replaced.

Thabo grabbed Nandi's arm. Pulled her down — against the ground, flat on her stomach, face against the burning rock. The burn woke Nandi. The pain — seventy-two degrees on her cheek — pierced through the fog of oxygen deprivation and Nandi inhaled. The air at ground level. The little breathable air that remained, heavy, dense, pinned to the ground by elementary physics.

She breathed.

Thabo crawled. The cloth stretched before him, one centimeter from the ground, sliding over the rock. The fibers quivered — barely. A breath. A trickle of oxygen at ground level on the cold lava. Thabo crawled and read the air at one centimeter of height and his free hand dragged Nandi by the wrist and his mouth was against the rock, lips in the dust, breathing what the earth deigned to give him.

Inyoni crawled behind. The Charge on her back — thirty-five kilos crushing her against the ground. The case scraped against the ceiling of the tunnel that had narrowed. The straps groaned. Inyoni counted — not the straps this time. The meters. One. Two. Three. Each meter crawled flat on her stomach in the gas.

Enama crawled last.

Her hands were open.

The gas had opened Enama's hands. Not will — physics. When muscles give out, fists come undone. Enama's fingers had opened despite herself, flat on the rock, palms against the burning ground. Contact-world forced by the gas. Enama's hands touched the earth not because she had chosen to but because the body on the verge of fainting returned to its natural posture — open, receptive, flat.

And Enama felt.

Under her palms, the rock vibrated. The magnitude 6.5 that no longer stopped, the superimposed micro-quakes, the constant rumble. But beneath the rumble — the way Nandi felt the second heartbeat beneath the first — a signal. A path. Air was circulating somewhere beneath the rock. The air followed a fracture, a network of fissures that the gas had not reached because the air moved through them too fast.

Enama seized Thabo's wrist. Hard. Enama's grip — not the technical grip, the full grip, the one that had not touched anyone since Sihle's death.

Thabo felt the direction. Enama was pushing his wrist to the left. Toward a wall that looked solid. That was solid — to the eyes. Not to Enama's hands. Not to the Contact-world that had just reopened because the gas had forced open the biologist's fists.

Thabo changed direction. Crawled to the left. Held his cloth against the wall. The fibers quivered. Stronger than at ground level. Air. A fissure in the rock, invisible, one centimeter wide perhaps, but enough to draw breathable air from a deeper network.

They breathed.

The four faces against the fissure, mouths against the rock, air hissing between lips and stones. Nandi felt life returning — first in the feet, then in the legs, then in the hands. The tremens returned too. The background noise of her body, the signal beneath the signal.

Thabo looked at Enama. She was looking at her hands. Open. Flat on the rock on either side of the fissure. The hands that had found the air. The hands that had refused to touch since Sihle had fallen and that had found the earth again because the gas had forced them.

Enama's Contact-world was not dead. It had been closed. And the earth had reopened it.

## Scene 4 — Nandi's premonition

The quake lasted seventeen seconds.

Nandi felt it forty seconds before it struck. Forty seconds. Twice the quake in the caverns. Triple what surface seismographs could have predicted.

Her body twisted.

Not a trembling — a convulsion. The tremens at its absolute maximum. Every muscle in her body vibrated at a frequency her bones could not contain. Her jaws clenched so hard that a chip of molar broke free and she tasted blood and calcium. Her hands clenched — fingers curled like claws, tendons protruding, nails in the palms. Her feet — her feet that were her instrument, her feet that read the world — her feet convulsed and the pain of the burn fused with the pain of the tremens and there was no longer any difference between reading and suffering.

Enama seized her.

The calibration grip. For the first time since Sihle's death — the grip Nandi had offered and Enama had refused. Nandi's wrist in Enama's hand. The pressure that shared the frequency. That absorbed a part of the signal.

Nandi felt the sharing. The frequency passing from her body into Enama's through the joined hands. The trembling distributing itself between two bodies instead of one. Not half — bodies do not divide like numbers. But enough. Enough for Nandi to open her eyes. To see.

The cavern was shaking.

The orange filaments were detaching from the ceiling in clusters. The rock was seeping — not water, lava. Drops. Beads of molten rock appearing in the ceiling's fissures and falling to the ground with a sharp hiss, like raindrops in the desert inverted. The first visible lava. The first proof that the magma was no longer far below — it was there, in the walls, in the fissures, in the flesh of the mountain.

The quake struck.

Magnitude 7.5. The ground heaved like a back arching. Inyoni pinned the Charge against the wall — her arms around the cylinder, her legs around the case, her body forming a human cradle for the device. *Holds.* The straps groaned. The crystallized salt exploded from three of them — three strands giving way at once. Twenty-one straps. Inyoni counted in the quake. Twenty-one.

Thabo, flat on his stomach, hands on the ground, the cloth crushed under his chest. He was breathing the rock. His body registered the vibrations — not with Nandi's precision, not with the tremens. With the geologist's knowledge. The frequencies in the ground told him what the rock was doing. And the rock was doing something new.

The rock was melting.

Not here — not yet. But below. The basalt layer separating them from the magma chamber was thinning. Each quake weakened it. Each additional degree of temperature softened it. The frequencies Thabo felt in the ground were no longer the frequencies of hard rock — they were the frequencies of plastic rock. Sound deformed as it passed through a layer that was no longer quite solid.

The quake stopped. Seventeen seconds. The rock stabilized. Background magnitude: 7. No longer 6.5 — 7. The floor had risen by half a point.

Nandi was panting. Enama's grip on her wrist still held. The heat between the two skins — unbearable now, the sweat and the heat and the pressure printing the Contact into the flesh. Nandi felt Enama hesitate. The hand wanting to let go — not the grip-release gesture of guilt. Physical pain. Contact burned.

Enama held.

Her eyes on Nandi. The pressure maintained despite the burn. Despite the cost. Contact had a price and Enama was paying it.

Nandi placed her free hand on the ground. Closed her eyes. The background tremor — 7 — and beneath, always beneath, the second heartbeat. Magnitude 11. Stronger. Closer. Not days. Not weeks. Hours.

She opened her eyes. Seized Enama's forearm — not the wrist, the forearm. Higher. The register of information, of the collective. The grip did not say *I'm in pain*. The grip said: *we need to descend faster*.

Nandi pressed. Hard. Constant. The grip that would not let go. Not Senzo's gesture — *follow me*. Nandi's gesture — the gesture of someone whose body felt the earth rising and who knew the earth would not wait.

Enama understood. Her eyes changed — not in expression, in depth. The gaze of the biologist who sees the signal in the other's body. The signal did not say *danger*. The signal said: *now*.

## Scene 5 — Enama's sacrifice

The passage narrowed.

Ahead of them, the gallery Thabo had followed since the CO2 pocket divided. No — it did not divide. It was collapsing. The ceiling descended. The walls closed in. The tunnel became a crawlway, then a hole, then — if the eyes did not lie in the orange light of the filaments — nothing. A wall.

Thabo held out the cloth. The fibers danced — hard. Harder than at any point since the start of the volcanic zones. The air was coming from the other side of the wall. The air was rushing through invisible fissures, capillaries in the rock, and the cloth caught it like an antenna.

But the wall held. Solid rock. No passage.

Inyoni stepped forward. Her hands on the wall — the climber's gesture, fingers searching for fissures, ridges, lines of weakness. She found one. A vertical fissure, twenty centimeters wide, rising into the ceiling. Not wide enough for a body. Not wide enough for the Charge.

She shook her head.

Nandi placed her bare feet on the ground at the foot of the wall. Seventy-eight degrees. The pain rose through her ankles like hot water — not the flood water, the scalding bath water, the water that makes the foot recoil by reflex. Nandi did not recoil. The soles of her feet — the right one still covered with skin, thickened, hardened by days of walking on burning rock; the left one raw, the burst blister covered with a brown crust — her feet read the ground.

There was a void behind the wall. A space. The ground vibrated differently on each side — the frequencies traveled faster through the void than through solid rock, and Nandi felt the acceleration under her toes. Behind the wall, a cavern. Behind the cavern, the descent continued.

But the wall held.

Thabo examined the rock. Placed his hands — blackened, burned, the skin of his palms cracked from the gas pocket — on the wall's surface. The geologist. He felt what his eyes could not see. The rock here was not homogeneous. The basalt formed a hard layer at the surface — one centimeter, perhaps two — but behind it, the rock was porous. Weakened by the heat. Saturated with trapped gas.

Thabo made the gesture. Hand flat, palm toward the wall, then a forward motion. *It can give way.* But his fingers opened at the end of the gesture — *if someone pushes*.

Inyoni looked at the Charge. Thirty-five kilos of seismic resonator. The sole reason for their descent, the object they had carried since the surface, protected by twenty-one straps and a shock-absorbing case eaten by salt. The Charge could not fit through the twenty-centimeter fissure.

The wall had to fall.

Nandi looked at Enama. Enama was looking at the wall. Her hands were open — open since the CO2 pocket, open since the gas had forced the Contact-world, open now no longer from the gas but by choice. Her open hands on the rock. Her palms flat against the wall.

The Contact-world.

Enama felt what Thabo had touched and what Nandi had read. The wall was thin. Fragile. Ready to give. But not here — farther. Lower in the tunnel, where the lava drops seeped from the ceiling, where the rock was hotter still and where the basalt layer thinned to nothing more than a membrane.

Enama removed her hands from the wall. Looked at the tunnel behind them. The path they had taken. Then the tunnel ahead — the tunnel that continued, that descended, that passed beneath the wall through a detour of two hundred meters perhaps through the zone of lava seepage.

Two hundred meters. In the hottest zone. Where lava fell from the ceiling.

Enama turned to Nandi.

She took her hands. Both hands. Not a wrist grip — the hands, palm against palm. The most direct Contact. The most painful Contact in the heat of the volcanic zones. Enama's skin against Nandi's skin, the sweat between them, the burn rising with each second of contact.

Enama did not let go.

Her right hand pressed Nandi's palm. Long pressure. Not a known Contact word. Not a codified grip. Not a calibration grip. Something else. A gesture Enama's hands were inventing at that instant, a gesture that had never needed to exist because no one had ever needed to say what Enama was saying.

The pressure was firm. Constant. Not a farewell — not yet. A transfer. Enama's hands were transmitting something to Nandi's hands. Not knowledge. Not a word. A weight. The weight of the earth. The weight of responsibility. Enama was placing the earth into Nandi's hands the way Inyoni had placed the Charge.

*I entrust you the ground.*

Nandi understood. She did not understand with words — Contact had no words for this. She understood with her hands. Enama's hands that had touched the earth since chapter I, that had felt the routes and the forks and the directions the earth indicated — those hands were transmitting their experience to Nandi's hands. Not the technique — you cannot transmit intuition through touch. But the permission. The permission to trust her feet.

Enama let go.

Her hands left Nandi's. The skin separated with the sound of wet cloth being peeled away — the sweat and the heat had nearly fused the two skins. Nandi felt the absence. The cold of the hot air on palms that no longer touched anything.

Enama turned toward the tunnel.

She said nothing. Contact said nothing. Her hands hung along her body — open this time. No longer closed. No longer clenched. Open and calm.

She walked.

Toward the tunnel that descended beneath the wall. Toward the lava seepage. Toward the two hundred meters of lethal zone that no one had asked to explore.

Thabo extended his arm. The Airmaster's gesture — the cloth before Enama's body, fibers dancing, the test. The air circulated. The air in the tunnel was breathable. Not the question. The question was the lava.

Inyoni moved. One step toward Enama. The hand reaching for the shoulder — the climber's reflex, the gesture of the one who catches, who holds back, who never lets go. *Holds.*

Enama placed her hand on Inyoni's fingers. Pushed them away. Gently. The Contact of refusal — but not Sihle's refusal. A different refusal. The refusal of the one who says: *this part is mine*.

She entered the tunnel.

The ground rose in temperature with each step. Nandi felt it in her feet — not Enama's steps, the vibrations. The rock was heating. Eighty. Eighty-five. The lava seeped from the ceiling in irregular drops, each drop a hiss, each hiss a potential burn.

Enama walked.

Her open hands touched the walls on each side. The Contact-world reopened, fully, definitively. She was speaking to the rock. The rock answered — not with words, with paths. The fissures where lava did not fall. The zones where the basalt was thicker. The places where a foot could land without meeting magma.

Nandi could no longer see her. The tunnel had turned. Enama had disappeared behind the bend of rock and all that remained were the vibrations — Enama's steps in the ground, Enama's weight on the rock, Enama's heart rate transmitted through Nandi's feet. Fifty-seven kilos of biologist, of cook, of survivalist, of woman who carried the guilt of a man fallen into an abyss.

The vibrations continued. Enama was advancing. Step by step. The ground at ninety degrees under her shod feet.

Then a sound. A sound the rock transmitted to Nandi's feet before the air transmitted it to her ears. A crack. The sound of rock giving way — not the sound of massive collapse, not the sound of the abyss. The sound of a wall fissuring. The sound of two hundred meters of weakened basalt yielding under the blows of Enama's open hands.

The wall fell.

The air rushed in — a violent current, burning, charged with gas and lava dust, but air all the same, air coming from the other side, from the cavern Nandi had felt beneath her feet. Thabo's cloth exploded in the current. The fibers danced as they had not danced since the caverns.

Thabo looked at the passage. The wall was no longer a wall — a hole. A passage. Two meters wide, one meter fifty high. Enough for a body. Enough for the Charge.

Nandi listened to the vibrations.

Enama's steps had ceased.

Not ceased — changed. Not the vibration of a body walking. The vibration of a body stopping. The vibration of a body motionless in a zone at ninety-five degrees, in a tunnel where lava seeped from the ceiling, in a place where the basalt she had just broken was releasing the gas trapped behind it.

Nandi closed her eyes. Her feet on the rock. The frequencies. Enama's body — still standing. Still alive. Heart rate slower than before. No panic. No running.

Enama was not coming back.

Enama's hands — Nandi felt it without seeing, understood it without understanding — Enama's hands were flat on the ground. The Contact-world. The last Contact-world. The biologist's palms on the molten rock and the rock answering and the dialogue between the woman and the earth that had lasted since chapter II and that was ending here, in a lava tunnel, in the heat that does not forgive.

Enama had chosen.

Not the lava. Not suicide. The path. The path the earth showed her did not go back. The path went deeper — downward, toward the magma, toward the truth of the earth her hands had always sought. Enama's hands had listened to the ground all her life and the ground was saying now: *come*. And Enama was going.

Guilt was not the motive. Not only. Guilt had closed her hands after Sihle's death. The earth had reopened them. And Enama's open hands, touching the ground one last time, had heard what the earth had been telling her from the beginning: *you were right. Water was the path. Rock was the trap. You were right and he died anyway. But you were right.*

The reason for being right when everyone dies.

That is what Enama's last gesture had said to Nandi — the long pressure, the new pressure, the pressure invented at the instant of departure. *I entrust you the ground.* Not *I'm sorry*. Not *forgive me*. *The ground is yours now. Your feet know.*

Nandi placed her feet on the rock. Seventy-five degrees. The pain. The signal. Magnitude 7 vibrating beneath magnitude 11 rising.

Under her feet, the vibrations of Enama's body were slowing. The heart rate was descending. The weight was diminishing — not from falling. From stillness. From a body that stops fighting the heat and lets itself be taken by the earth.

The group's Contact lost one more word.

The calibration grip. The open hand on the rock. The Contact-world that had dazzled everyone in chapter II. The hands that cut roots and prepared porridge and felt the earth speak. The hands closed then reopened. The hands that said guilt and earth and transfer.

Dead.

Enama's grip on Nandi's wrist would never be made again. The two women of the earth — Enama through the hands, Nandi through the feet — were no longer two. Nandi was alone with the ground. Alone with the earth. Alone with her burned feet and her tremens and the weight of the world that Enama's hands had placed in her palms.

## Scene 6 — After Enama

Three people.

The number was obscene. Seven at the start. Three now. More than half the group in the rock. More than half the Contact in the earth. More than half the words in silence.

Inyoni carried the Charge. The shock-absorbing case was a ruin — straps stiffened by salt, three broken, joints infiltrated. Inyoni had re-strapped it with pieces of rope. Twenty-one original straps plus three makeshift. She counted all twenty-four as if nothing had changed. *Holds.*

Thabo tested the air. The tunnel on the other side of the wall — the passage Enama had opened — descended steeply toward a larger cavern. The air circulated better — the current Enama had felt beneath the rock turned out to be a powerful thermal flux, the hot air from the magma chamber rising and creating a draft below. The breathable air entered from the bottom.

Nandi walked on the light rocks. The rare light rocks. The checkerboard was simplifying — less gray, more brown, the temperature difference between the rocks diminishing. Sixty-five on the light ones. Eighty on the dark ones. Her feet sorted. The pain sorted. The body had learned what Thabo had taught it and the eyes guided the feet and the feet guided the body and the body descended.

The Contact among the three survivors was almost bare.

Thabo spoke only through the cloth. His burned hands could no longer bear direct Contact — the pressure of a palm on an arm provoked a jolt of pain that even the Airmaster's stoicism could not mask. Thabo communicated through the cloth. Cloth held taut, cloth lowered, cloth still. Three states. Three words. *Air is flowing. Air is not flowing. Air is dangerous.*

Inyoni spoke only through the straps. The sound of the fasteners she checked — the click of metal, the friction of fiber — had become her vocabulary. When the straps held, Inyoni was calm. When a strap groaned, Inyoni stopped. The Charge dictated the pace of the march the way Nandi's tremens dictated the rhythm of the quakes.

Nandi no longer spoke.

Not by choice. By economy. Contact cost pain and energy and calories that no one could afford anymore. The water bottles were half empty — no more Enama to prepare rations, no more Jabu to find water. The water they drank was the water they had. No more source. No more underground river. No more flood — the flood had killed Jabu and the water had vanished with him, absorbed by the heat, evaporated, returned to the earth like everything else.

The Contact among three people held in three gestures.

Thabo's cloth. Inyoni's straps. Nandi's feet.

And magnitude 7 rising toward what Nandi felt rising beneath — the 11 that was approaching. The 11 that was no longer a number in the margin of a seismograph roll that no one carried anymore. The 11 that was a pain in Nandi's bones, a pressure in her temples, a second heartbeat stronger than the first.

The air was forty-five degrees. The ground seventy-five. The lava seeped from the ceiling's fissures in regular drops. Three people toward the depths.

Where the Crawlway waited.
